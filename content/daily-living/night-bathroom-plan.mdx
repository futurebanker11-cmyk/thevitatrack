    <h2>Why night trips happen</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="lead"><span class="kicker">Common causes:</span> evening fluids, caffeine or alcohol, salty dinners, ankle swelling that shifts to urine when lying down, prostate enlargement (BPH), overactive bladder, sleep apnea, diabetes, and some medicines (e.g., diuretics).</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Make a steady evening rhythm so your body expects <strong>fewer night trips</strong>.</li>
          <li>Handle <strong>leg swelling earlier</strong> in the day to reduce nighttime urine.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Small changes across the day work better than one big change at bedtime. Start with one or two steps and build.
      </div>
    </div>

  <!-- Plan -->
  <section id="plan" class="section">
    <h2>Your 5-part night bathroom plan</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">1) Daytime hydration</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Spread water through the day and <strong>front-load</strong> by mid-afternoon.</li>
          <li>Within 2–3 hours of bed: only small sips if thirsty.</li>
          <li>On fluid restriction (heart/kidney): follow your daily limit.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">2) Salt & caffeine timing</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Keep dinner <strong>lower in salt</strong>; avoid pickle, papad, instant mixes.</li>
          <li>Stop caffeine ~6 pm (tea/coffee/cola/chocolate).</li>
          <li>Limit alcohol at night; it increases trips and fragments sleep.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">3) Leg elevation for swelling</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Late afternoon/early evening: elevate legs above heart for 30–45 minutes.</li>
          <li>Light compression if approved; ask how and when to use.</li>
          <li>Moves fluid earlier → less urine overnight.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">4) Double-void routine</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Urinate before bed. After 5–10 minutes, <strong>try again</strong> to empty more fully.</li>
          <li>Relax, don’t strain; slow breaths help the pelvic muscles release.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">5) Night tools & backups</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Motion night lights; non-slip slippers; clear path to bathroom.</li>
          <li>Consider a bedside commode or urinal on high-urgency nights.</li>
          <li>Breathable absorbent products if recommended; correct size matters.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

    <table class="table" role="table">

        <tr><th scope="col">Topic</th><th scope="col">What to do</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>

        <tr>
          <td><strong>Diuretics (“water tablets”)</strong></td>
          <td>Ask if dose can be taken earlier (morning/early afternoon).</td>
          <td>Never change timing without clinician advice.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Evening medicines</strong></td>
          <td>Take as prescribed; set a reminder; check if any irritate the bladder.</td>
          <td>Bring a med list to visits; discuss timing for best sleep.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Fluids</strong></td>
          <td>Front-load intake; smaller sips close to bedtime.</td>
          <td>Limit late soups and high-water fruit at night.</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">If thirst wakes you often, review daytime hydration and salt intake with your clinician.</p>

  <!-- Prostate / OAB -->
  <section id="bph-oab" class="section">
    <h2>Prostate & overactive bladder tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If you have BPH</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Double-void at bedtime; sitting can relax pelvic muscles.</li>
          <li>Limit evening fluids; reduce alcohol/caffeine.</li>
          <li>Ask about medicines that relax or shrink the prostate.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If you have overactive bladder</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Bladder training: extend time between trips gradually (see below).</li>
          <li>Identify triggers (caffeine, artificial sweeteners, citrus for some).</li>
          <li>Discuss bladder-calming medicines if symptoms persist.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        New severe urgency, burning, fever, or blood in urine needs prompt care.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Pelvic floor -->
  <section id="pelvic" class="section">
    <h2>Pelvic floor & bladder training</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Pelvic floor basics (Kegels)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Tighten the muscles used to stop urine/gas. Hold 3–5 seconds; relax 5–10 seconds.</li>
          <li>Do 10 reps, 2–3 times daily. Don’t hold breath or tighten belly/buttocks.</li>
          <li>Consistency for 6–8 weeks improves control.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Bladder training</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Keep a diary; plan trips (e.g., every 2 hours), extend by 15 minutes weekly as tolerated.</li>
          <li>When urge hits: pause, do 5 quick pelvic squeezes, slow breath, then walk calmly to the bathroom.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        A pelvic floor therapist can tailor exercises, especially after prostate surgery or childbirth history.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Safety -->
  <section id="safety" class="section">
    <h2>Night-safety setup</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Motion night lights bed → bathroom; keep a torch on the bedside table.</li>
          <li>Clear rugs/cords; non-slip slippers; consider grab bars and a raised seat.</li>
          <li>Rise slowly: sit, pause 3 breaths, stand, steady, then walk.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        Frequent dizziness on standing? Ask about blood pressure checks and medicine timing.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="redflags" class="section">
    <h2>When to seek care</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Burning pain, fever, or foul-smelling urine.</li>
        <li>Blood in urine; new severe lower back/flank pain.</li>
        <li>Sudden inability to urinate or severe lower abdominal pain.</li>
        <li>Night trips with weight gain/swelling or breathlessness.</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can signal infection, blockage, kidney/heart issues, or other urgent problems.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Does drinking less solve it?</p>
        <p>Not by itself. <strong>Better timing</strong> plus lower-salt dinners and leg elevation work better. Follow any clinician-set fluid limits.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Why do my legs swell by day?</p>
        <p>Fluid collects in lower legs; when you lie down, it moves into the bloodstream and becomes urine. Elevating legs earlier helps.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is a bedside commode okay?</p>
        <p>Yes—especially if you rush or feel unsteady at night. Keep the path lit and clear even if you use a commode.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When do I check for sleep apnea?</p>
        <p>Snoring, pauses in breathing, morning headaches, or daytime sleepiness with frequent night urination—ask about a sleep study.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
