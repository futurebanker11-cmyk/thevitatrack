  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What confusion can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> <strong>Sudden confusion (delirium)</strong> is a rapid change in attention, thinking, or awareness. It’s common in older adults and often triggered by illness, pain, dehydration, or medicines. <strong>Dementia</strong> develops slowly over months to years. Any <em>new or sudden</em> confusion should be treated as <strong>urgent</strong> until serious causes are ruled out.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Typical signs:</strong> not knowing where you are, misplacing time/day, waxing/waning alertness, agitation or sleepiness, hallucinations, reversed sleep-wake cycle.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>FAST stroke signs:</strong> face droop, arm weakness, speech trouble, sudden severe headache</li>
        <li>Fever with shaking chills, very high/low heart rate or blood pressure, fast breathing, or oxygen saturation low</li>
        <li>Head injury or fall, especially if on <strong>blood thinners</strong></li>
        <li>Very <strong>low or high blood sugar</strong>, or known diabetes with confusion</li>
        <li>Severe dehydration: minimal urine, dizziness, new confusion</li>
        <li>New severe headache, neck stiffness, seizure, or repeated vomiting</li>
        <li>Overdose or suspected medication mix-up</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These may signal stroke/TIA, sepsis, low oxygen, brain bleed, significant metabolic problem, or drug toxicity.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- What to do now -->
  <section id="now" class="section">
    <h2>What to do <em>now</em></h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Immediate steps (if safe and while seeking help)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Check <strong>blood sugar</strong> if available; treat low sugar per plan (glucose tablets/juice) and recheck</li>
          <li>Measure <strong>temperature</strong> and <strong>oxygen</strong> if a home oximeter is available</li>
          <li>Offer <strong>fluids</strong> unless choking risk; avoid alcohol/sedatives</li>
          <li>Gather a <strong>medication list</strong> (include OTCs/herbals) and recent dose changes</li>
          <li>Stay with the person, keep the environment <strong>calm and well lit</strong>, remove hazards</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call a clinician promptly</strong> for any new confusion, even if mild. If red flags are present or confusion worsens rapidly, seek emergency care.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Causes of sudden confusion">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Category</th><th>Examples</th><th>Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td>Infections</td>
          <td>UTI, pneumonia, skin infections, COVID/flu</td>
          <td>Fever/chills, cough, burning urination, new weakness</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Metabolic/organ</td>
          <td>Low/high blood sugar, low sodium, kidney/liver failure, low oxygen</td>
          <td>Thirst, swelling, jaundice, shortness of breath</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Medications/toxins</td>
          <td>Sedatives, sleep aids, opioids, anticholinergics, antihistamines, steroids, alcohol; <em>polypharmacy</em></td>
          <td>Recent new drug or dose change; daytime sleepiness or agitation</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Neurologic</td>
          <td>Stroke/TIA, seizure/post-ictal, head injury, subdural bleed</td>
          <td>Focal weakness, slurred speech, headache after fall, on blood thinners</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Pain & retention</td>
          <td>Uncontrolled pain, urinary retention, severe constipation/fecal impaction</td>
          <td>Restlessness, abdominal discomfort, no bowel movement for days</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Sleep & environment</td>
          <td>Sleep deprivation, sensory overload, poor lighting/hearing/vision</td>
          <td>Worse at night (“sundowning”), improved with familiar cues</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Mood & withdrawal</td>
          <td>Severe depression, alcohol/benzodiazepine withdrawal</td>
          <td>Anxiety, tremors, sweating, recent stopping of sedatives</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Safe steps at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If no emergency signs</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Hydrate (water, oral rehydration, soups) and offer small, frequent foods</li>
          <li>Ensure glasses/hearing aids are on and working</li>
          <li>Regular bathroom schedule; watch for constipation or urinary retention</li>
          <li>Keep a day/night routine: blinds open by day, quiet and dim at night</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Communication:</strong> Speak slowly, one step at a time, offer choices (“tea or water?”), and reassure—avoid arguing about facts.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medication cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Do not</strong> stop prescription medicines without guidance—ask for a rapid <em>medication review</em></li>
          <li>Avoid or minimize <strong>anticholinergics</strong> (e.g., diphenhydramine), <strong>sedatives</strong> (benzodiazepines, “Z-drugs”), and daytime opioids where possible</li>
          <li>Check for duplicates (e.g., multiple sleep aids) and interactions (OTCs/herbals)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Alcohol & dehydration</strong> commonly worsen confusion—limit alcohol and encourage fluids unless on fluid restriction.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Environment -->
  <section id="environment" class="section">
    <h2>Calm environment tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Good lighting with minimal glare; night-lights for bathroom path</li>
          <li>Large-face clock and calendar in view; family photos; simple signs on doors</li>
          <li>Reduce noise (TV/radio off if not watched), limit visitors to one or two at a time</li>
          <li>Keep pathways clear; remove throw rugs; lock up hazardous items</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Gentle music, a familiar blanket, or a brief walk can reduce agitation.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical workup for confusion">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Confirm delirium, find triggers</td>
          <td>Onset/time course, meds, vitals, hydration, pain, CAM screen</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Point-of-care</strong></td>
          <td>Immediate threats</td>
          <td>Glucose check, pulse oximetry, ECG</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs</strong></td>
          <td>Identify metabolic/infectious causes</td>
          <td>CBC, CMP/electrolytes, kidney/liver, TSH, B12, urinalysis/urine culture; +/- drug levels</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Structural causes</td>
          <td>Chest X-ray (pneumonia), CT/MRI head for stroke/bleed (esp. after fall or neuro deficits)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Other</strong></td>
          <td>Targeted tests</td>
          <td>Blood gases, infection testing (e.g., COVID/flu), bladder scan for retention</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Treatment targets the cause—fluids, antibiotics for infection, oxygen, medication adjustments, pain/constipation relief, or stroke care pathways.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Start time and pattern (sudden vs gradual, better/worse at night)</li>
          <li>Fever, blood sugar readings, oxygen numbers if available</li>
          <li>Fluid intake, urine output, bowel movements</li>
          <li>Medication changes (including OTCs/herbals) in past 2 weeks</li>
          <li>Recent illnesses, pain, falls, or travel/hospital stays</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring this log to appointments—it speeds diagnosis and safer treatment.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with dignity</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Stay calm, introduce yourself each time, offer simple choices</li>
          <li>Prevent wandering: door alarms, supervised walks, remove car keys</li>
          <li>Ensure nutrition: small snacks/fluids every 1–2 hours if safe</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> any new confusion, fever, oxygen <90–92% at rest, low blood sugar not improving, head injury on blood thinners, or rapidly worsening symptoms.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Delirium vs dementia?</p>
        <p><strong>Delirium</strong> is sudden and fluctuating, often from illness or meds; <strong>dementia</strong> is gradual and progressive. New confusion needs urgent evaluation.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is UTI a common cause?</p>
        <p>Yes—especially in frail adults. But not all confusion is UTI; clinicians will check other causes and avoid unnecessary antibiotics.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do sleeping pills help?</p>
        <p>Most make confusion worse. Prioritize sleep routine, light/daytime activity, and address pain/constipation before medications.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When is hospital care needed?</p>
        <p>Any red flags, rapidly worsening confusion, inability to hydrate safely, very abnormal vitals, or suspected stroke/serious infection.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
