  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What sudden vision changes can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> A <strong>sudden change in vision</strong> is always important. Causes range from benign (temporary migraine aura) to emergencies such as <strong>stroke/TIA</strong>, <strong>retinal detachment</strong>, <strong>central retinal artery occlusion (CRAO)</strong>, <strong>acute angle-closure glaucoma</strong>, or <strong>giant cell arteritis</strong>. <em>Time matters</em>—rapid assessment can save sight.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Describe:</strong> one eye vs both (cover each eye to test), pain or painless, flashes/floaters, “curtain” over vision, double vision, headache, jaw pain with chewing, nausea, recent injury, diabetes or vascular risks, and medicines taken.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Sudden, painless vision loss</strong> in one eye (CRAO/CRVO, retinal detachment, optic nerve stroke)</li>
        <li><strong>Flashes with a shower of new floaters</strong> or a <strong>dark curtain/veil</strong> (retinal tear/detachment)</li>
        <li>Severe <strong>eye pain</strong>, headache, <strong>halos</strong> around lights, nausea/vomiting (acute angle-closure glaucoma)</li>
        <li><strong>Double vision</strong>, face/arm weakness, trouble speaking, imbalance (possible <strong>stroke</strong>)</li>
        <li>New vision loss with <strong>scalp tenderness, jaw pain when chewing</strong>, or headache in adults &gt;50 (possible <strong>giant cell arteritis</strong>)</li>
        <li>Chemical splash/trauma to the eye, or sudden vision change after head injury</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can threaten sight or life. Seek emergency care now.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Sudden vision changes causes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Cause</th><th>Typical features</th><th>Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Retinal detachment / tear</strong></td>
          <td>Flashes, many floaters, shadow/curtain</td>
          <td>Prior cataract surgery, high myopia, trauma</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Posterior vitreous detachment (PVD)</strong></td>
          <td>Sudden floater(s) ± brief flashes, vision usually OK</td>
          <td>Needs exam to rule out retinal tear</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>CRAO / amaurosis fugax</strong></td>
          <td>Painless monocular vision loss, sudden</td>
          <td>Stroke equivalent—evaluate carotids/heart urgently</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>CRVO / vitreous hemorrhage</strong></td>
          <td>Blurred vision, dark haze/shadows</td>
          <td>Risk: hypertension, diabetes, glaucoma, blood thinners (report)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Ischemic optic neuropathy / GCA</strong></td>
          <td>Sudden painless loss; color desaturation</td>
          <td>Age &gt;50 with headache, scalp tenderness, jaw claudication—treat fast</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Acute angle-closure glaucoma</strong></td>
          <td>Severe eye pain, red eye, halos, headache, nausea</td>
          <td>Often in dim light after dilation; mid-dilated pupil</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stroke / TIA (occipital or brainstem)</strong></td>
          <td>Visual field loss or double vision</td>
          <td>Neurologic deficits (speech, weakness, imbalance)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Optic neuritis</strong></td>
          <td>Pain with eye movement; subacute loss</td>
          <td>Color washout; afferent pupillary defect</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Migraine aura</strong></td>
          <td>Shimmering zig-zags/scintillating scotoma, 5–60 min</td>
          <td>Often both eyes; may be followed by headache or occur alone</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Corneal issues / dry eye</strong></td>
          <td>Sudden blur that clears with blinking/tears</td>
          <td>Contact lens wear, gritty sensation, light sensitivity if abrasion</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Metabolic/medication</strong></td>
          <td>Transient blur or color tinge</td>
          <td>Very high/low blood sugar; digoxin (yellow), PDE-5 inhibitors (blue/blur)</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Do now -->
  <section id="do-now" class="section">
    <h2>What to do right now</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Do not drive.</strong> Arrange a ride to an emergency/urgent eye clinic.</li>
          <li><strong>Cover-test:</strong> Cover each eye in turn—note if the problem is in one eye or both; share this at triage.</li>
          <li><strong>New curtain/veil, many floaters, or painless vision loss:</strong> go <em>now</em> (retina/artery emergencies).</li>
          <li><strong>Eye pain with halos, headache, nausea:</strong> go now (possible acute glaucoma).</li>
          <li><strong>Chemical splash:</strong> irrigate with clean water/saline for <strong>15 minutes</strong> immediately, then seek care.</li>
          <li>If diabetic and unwell, check glucose; treat low sugars and bring readings.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Bring:</strong> medication list (especially blood thinners), recent glasses/contacts, and any photos of what you’re seeing (field loss patterns can help).
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medication notes</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Do not start steroid eye drops</strong> on your own—can worsen infections or angle closure.</li>
          <li><strong>PDE-5 inhibitors</strong> (sildenafil, tadalafil): sudden vision loss/defect—<strong>stop and seek urgent care</strong>.</li>
          <li><strong>Blood thinners</strong>: report use if vision changes follow strain/trauma (vitreous hemorrhage risk).</li>
          <li>Long-term drugs with eye risks (e.g., <em>hydroxychloroquine</em>) rarely cause sudden loss but tell your clinician.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Contact lenses:</strong> remove if eye is painful/red; do not reuse until cleared. Avoid sleeping in lenses.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Comfort -->
  <section id="comfort" class="section">
    <h2>Comfort & safety</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Limit bright light; use sunglasses if light-sensitive</li>
          <li>Avoid eye rubbing; consider a clean <strong>eye shield</strong> if trauma suspected</li>
          <li>Prevent falls: clear paths, use handrails, ask for assistance walking</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>One-eye vs both-eyes tip:</strong> If the change remains when either eye is covered, it’s likely from the brain (e.g., stroke/occipital) rather than the eye itself—seek emergency care.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical evaluation for sudden vision changes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Urgent assessment</strong></td>
          <td>Rule out stroke/ocular emergencies</td>
          <td>Visual acuity/fields, pupils (RAPD), eye pressure, slit-lamp exam</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Dilated fundus exam</strong></td>
          <td>Retina/optic nerve view</td>
          <td>Look for tears, detachment, artery/vein occlusion, hemorrhage</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Confirm/inform treatment</td>
          <td>OCT, ocular ultrasound (B-scan), CT/MRI for stroke/optic neuritis</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs</strong></td>
          <td>Identify inflammatory/vascular causes</td>
          <td>ESR/CRP and platelets for suspected GCA; glucose/electrolytes</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stroke/embolus workup</strong></td>
          <td>Prevent recurrence</td>
          <td>Carotid ultrasound/CTA, echocardiogram, heart rhythm monitoring</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Protect vision</td>
          <td>Retinal laser/cryotherapy or surgery; IOP-lowering meds for glaucoma; urgent steroids for GCA; stroke pathway as indicated</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Timing affects outcomes—seek care promptly even if symptoms improve.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Exact <strong>onset time</strong>, progression (minutes/hours), and any triggers</li>
          <li>One eye vs both (cover test), pain level, light sensitivity</li>
          <li>Flashes/floaters, curtain/field defects, double vision</li>
          <li>Headache, jaw pain, scalp tenderness, weakness/numbness, speech or balance changes</li>
          <li>Blood sugar readings if diabetic; recent BP values</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring notes, medication list, and eyewear/contacts case to appointments—it speeds diagnosis and treatment.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with urgency & calm</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Arrange immediate transport to emergency/eye care; do not delay</li>
          <li>Note onset time, observed deficits, and medicines (especially blood thinners)</li>
          <li>Assist with navigation and prevent falls; reduce visual/lighting strain</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> any sudden vision change, new curtain/floaters, eye pain with nausea/halos, or vision loss with headache/jaw pain in adults over 50.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is sudden blurred vision an emergency?</p>
        <p>Yes—assume it is until proven otherwise. Rapid evaluation can prevent permanent vision loss or detect a stroke.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Are new floaters always serious?</p>
        <p>Not always. A common cause is posterior vitreous detachment, but any <strong>new shower of floaters or flashes</strong> needs a same-day retinal exam to rule out a tear.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What does a “curtain” over vision mean?</p>
        <p>Often a retinal detachment—go to emergency eye care immediately.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How do I tell if it’s one eye or both?</p>
        <p>Cover one eye, then the other. If the problem remains with either eye covered, it likely involves the brain/visual pathways—seek emergency care.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
