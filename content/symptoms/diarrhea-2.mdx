  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What diarrhea means</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Diarrhea is loose/watery stools (often Bristol types 6–7), sometimes with cramps, urgency, or nausea. In seniors, the main risk is <strong>dehydration and electrolyte loss</strong>. Most acute cases improve in 1–3 days, but red-flags need urgent care.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Acute (days)</strong> is usually infection or food-related. <strong>Persistent (2–4 weeks)</strong> suggests medicines, intolerance, or post-infectious IBS. <strong>Chronic (&gt;4 weeks)</strong> can be inflammatory bowel disease, bile acid diarrhea, pancreatic issues, or malabsorption—see clinician.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Signs of <strong>severe dehydration</strong>: confusion, fainting, minimal urine, very dry mouth, fast heartbeat</li>
        <li><strong>Blood or black tarry stools</strong>, or intense abdominal pain</li>
        <li>High fever (&ge;38.9°C / 102°F), shaking chills, or repeated vomiting unable to keep fluids down</li>
        <li>Recent <strong>antibiotic use</strong> with severe diarrhea (possible C. difficile)</li>
        <li>Recent travel with fever, or known immune compromise/kidney or heart failure</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These may need IV fluids, stool tests, or antibiotics under medical care.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Dehydration -->
  <section id="dehydration" class="section">
    <h2>Dehydration signs</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Thirst, dry mouth/tongue, dizziness when standing</li>
          <li>Dark or very little urine (aim for pale yellow)</li>
          <li>Fatigue, headache, cramping</li>
          <li>In frail seniors: confusion, sleepiness, low blood pressure</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Goal:</strong> steady small sips of fluids or oral rehydration solution (ORS) until urine is pale and symptoms improve.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

          <td><strong>Food intolerances</strong></td>

          <td>Bloating, gas; triggered by milk/wheat/fruit juices</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Inflammatory/malabsorption</strong></td>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Rehydration & self-care</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">First 24–48 hours (no red-flags)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>ORS:</strong> frequent small sips of oral rehydration solution; if unavailable, alternate water + salty crackers/broth + a little juice</li>
          <li><strong>Pause irritants:</strong> alcohol, very fatty/spicy foods, excess caffeine, large dairy servings</li>
          <li><strong>Rest gut gently:</strong> start with bananas, rice, applesauce, toast, plain yogurt; advance as tolerated</li>
          <li><strong>Zinc</strong> (short course) may reduce duration in some adults—ask clinician if appropriate</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <p class="kicker">Call your clinician soon if</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Diarrhea lasts &gt;48–72 hours or recurs frequently</li>
          <li>You take diuretics, ACE inhibitors, or have heart/kidney disease</li>
          <li>You recently used antibiotics or were hospitalized (possible C. difficile)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small">This page is educational and not a diagnosis. Seek urgent care for the warning signs above.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds-caution" class="section">
    <h2>Medicines & cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Loperamide</strong> can reduce urgency—<em>avoid</em> if fever, blood/mucus in stool, or suspected food poisoning/dysentery.</li>
          <li><strong>Bismuth subsalicylate</strong> may help mild traveler’s diarrhea; can darken stool/tongue.</li>
          <li><strong>Antibiotics</strong> are rarely needed for routine viral diarrhea; may be used for specific bacterial causes—doctor’s advice only.</li>
          <li><strong>Probiotics</strong> may help some cases but are optional; prioritize ORS and diet.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        Review medicines that can <em>cause</em> diarrhea (metformin, magnesium, sugar alcohols). Do not stop prescriptions without guidance—ask about adjustments.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Diet -->
  <section id="diet" class="section">
    <h2>Food & hydration tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Helpful choices</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>ORS, water, diluted juice, broths; aim for pale yellow urine</li>
          <li>Low-fat, low-fiber foods initially; add soluble fiber later (oats, bananas)</li>
          <li><strong>Lactose-light</strong> choices for a few days if sensitive</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Limit/avoid for now</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Alcohol, greasy/fried foods, very spicy dishes</li>
          <li>Excess caffeine and large amounts of artificial sweeteners (sorbitol, mannitol)</li>
          <li>Large raw roughage until stools begin to form</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

          <td>Identify severity, risks, and likely cause</td>
          <td>Travel/food exposures, recent antibiotics/hospital stay, medications, dehydration check</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Basic labs</strong></td>
          <td>Assess dehydration & electrolytes</td>
          <td>CBC, BMP; kidney function if low intake or diuretics</td>

        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stool tests</strong></td>
          <td>Confirm infection/inflammation</td>

          <td>For persistent or severe cases</td>
          <td>Colonoscopy for chronic/inflammatory signs; imaging if complications suspected</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Correct fluids & target cause</td>
          <td>ORS/IV fluids, targeted antibiotics if bacterial, antidiarrheals when appropriate</td>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What should I drink first?</p>
        <p>Oral rehydration solution (ORS) or alternating water, broth, and diluted juice in small frequent sips. Aim for pale yellow urine.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is loperamide safe?</p>
        <p>Yes for mild, non-bloody diarrhea without fever. Avoid if blood/mucus in stool, high fever, or suspected food poisoning—seek care instead.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When are antibiotics needed?</p>
        <p>Only for specific bacterial infections, severe traveler’s diarrhea, or C. difficile. Most viral cases do not need antibiotics.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How long can I watch at home?</p>
        <p>If no red-flags, try self-care for up to 48–72 hours. Persistent, worsening, or recurrent symptoms need clinical evaluation.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
