  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What swelling can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Swelling of the feet/ankles (<strong>peripheral edema</strong>) happens when extra fluid collects in tissues. It’s often related to <strong>vein valve weakness</strong> (venous insufficiency) or <strong>salt/fluid retention</strong>. It can also signal <strong>heart</strong>, <strong>kidney</strong>, or <strong>liver</strong> problems, <strong>lymph</strong> blockage, <strong>medicines</strong>, or a <strong>blood clot</strong>.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p>Clues: one vs both legs, how high the swelling goes, skin color/temperature, pain vs heaviness, and changes with elevation or overnight.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Sudden one-leg swelling</strong> with pain/redness (possible DVT/blood clot)</li>
        <li>Swelling with <strong>shortness of breath, chest pain,</strong> or coughing blood (possible clot in lungs/heart strain)</li>
        <li>Very <strong>red, hot, tender</strong> skin with fever or feeling unwell (possible cellulitis)</li>
        <li>Fast weight gain, severe breathlessness at rest or when lying flat (possible heart failure flare)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Rapid care can prevent complications.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Cause</th><th scope="col">Typical features</th><th scope="col">Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Chronic venous insufficiency</strong></td>
          <td>Heaviness, pitting edema; worse by evening, better with elevation</td>
          <td>Varicose veins, skin darkening/itching near shins/ankles</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Heart failure</strong></td>
          <td>Leg swelling + breathlessness, fatigue, orthopnea</td>
          <td>Rapid weight gain; BNP elevated; responds to diuretics & salt restriction</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Kidney disease</strong></td>
          <td>Generalized swelling (legs, around eyes)</td>
          <td>Foamy urine (protein), high creatinine; blood pressure issues</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Liver disease / low albumin</strong></td>
          <td>Leg swelling ± abdominal fluid</td>
          <td>Jaundice, easy bruising; low albumin on labs</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Lymphedema</strong></td>
          <td>Non-pitting, firm swelling; skin thickening</td>
          <td>Often one-sided; after surgery, radiation, or chronic infection</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Deep vein thrombosis (DVT)</strong></td>
          <td>Acute one-leg swelling, pain, warmth</td>
          <td>Recent immobility, surgery, cancer, long travel</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Cellulitis</strong></td>
          <td>Red, hot, tender area ± fever</td>
          <td>Often one-sided; requires antibiotics</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Medications</strong></td>
          <td>Pitting swelling; often both legs</td>
          <td>Calcium-channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine), NSAIDs, steroids, hormones, thiazolidinediones</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Hypothyroidism</strong></td>
          <td>Puffy features, fatigue, dry skin</td>
          <td>Elevated TSH; improves with thyroid treatment</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Long standing/sitting, heat, high salt</strong></td>
          <td>Evening swelling, mild discomfort</td>
          <td>Improves with elevation, walking, salt reduction</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>

  <!-- First steps -->
  <section id="first-steps" class="section">
    <h2>First steps at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Elevation & movement</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Feet up above heart level 15–20 minutes, 2–3×/day.</li>
          <li>Ankle pumps and short walks each hour while awake.</li>
          <li>Avoid long sitting/standing; change positions often.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Salt & fluids (as advised)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Moderate salt intake; favor fresh foods over packaged.</li>
          <li>If you have heart/kidney disease, follow your clinician’s fluid and salt targets closely.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <p class="kicker">Footwear & comfort</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Supportive, roomy shoes; avoid tight socks at the calf.</li>
          <li>Skin moisturiser to prevent cracking; inspect between toes daily.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Speak with your clinician</strong> for new swelling, one-sided swelling, rapid weight gain, or if you take medicines that can worsen edema.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Compression & skin care -->
  <section id="care" class="section">
    <h2>Compression & skin care</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Compression socks</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Consider <strong>15–20 mmHg</strong> knee-highs for daily use if DVT and arterial disease have been ruled out.</li>
          <li>Put on in the morning when swelling is lowest; remove at night.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Gentle daily moisturiser and prompt care of skin breaks reduce infection risk in swollen legs.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">

          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>

          <td><strong>Basic labs & urine</strong></td>
          <td>Assess organs & protein</td>
          <td>Creatinine/eGFR, electrolytes, liver enzymes/albumin, TSH, urinalysis for protein</td>

        <tr>
          <td><strong>Heart tests</strong></td>
          <td>Heart failure signs</td>
          <td>BNP/NT-proBNP, chest X-ray, echocardiogram if indicated</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Other</strong></td>
          <td>Selected cases</td>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment options</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Lifestyle & compression</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Elevation, walking program, calf strengthening, and appropriate compression stockings.</li>
          <li>Weight management and salt moderation support long-term control.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Medicines</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Diuretics</strong> (water tablets) only when indicated (e.g., heart failure), with monitoring of kidneys and potassium.</li>
          <li>Medicine review/substitutions if a drug is causing edema (e.g., adjust amlodipine dose).</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Condition-specific care</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Treat infections (cellulitis), manage heart/kidney/liver disease, lymphedema therapy (manual drainage, specialised wraps).</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        Plans are individualized—follow your clinician’s guidance, especially if you have heart, kidney, or liver conditions.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Morning vs evening leg/ankle size; how high swelling reaches.</li>
          <li><strong>Daily weight</strong> (same time): report fast gains (e.g., ≥1–2 kg in a few days).</li>
          <li>Breathlessness, urine output, and salt intake notes.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        A simple log plus photos of ankles (same angle/light) helps monitor progress and guide care.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I need diuretics?</p>
        <p>Only if swelling is from conditions like heart failure and your clinician recommends them. They aren’t helpful for all causes and can affect kidneys and electrolytes.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Which compression level?</p>
        <p>Many start with <strong>15–20 mmHg</strong>. Higher levels may be used under guidance. Ensure arterial circulation is adequate before use.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can my blood pressure pill cause swelling?</p>
        <p>Yes—calcium-channel blockers such as amlodipine commonly do. Your clinician may adjust the dose or switch medicines.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">One leg only—is it serious?</p>
        <p>One-sided, painful, warm swelling may be a clot or infection—seek urgent evaluation.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
