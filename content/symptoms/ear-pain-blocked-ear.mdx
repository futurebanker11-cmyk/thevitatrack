  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What ear pain or blockage can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Ear pain (<strong>otalgia</strong>) and a <strong>blocked/plugged</strong> feeling are common. In older adults, frequent causes include <strong>earwax (cerumen) buildup</strong>, <strong>swimmer’s ear</strong> (outer-ear infection), <strong>middle-ear fluid/pressure</strong> from colds/allergies, and <strong>referred pain</strong> from jaw/teeth/throat. Sudden hearing loss, severe pain with fever, or swelling behind the ear are urgent.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Describe:</strong> pain vs. pressure, constant vs. with chewing/swallowing, recent cold/allergy flare, swimming, hearing change, ringing, drainage, or rash.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Sudden hearing loss</strong> in one ear (over hours–3 days)—treat as urgent</li>
        <li>Severe ear pain with <strong>fever</strong> and diabetes/immunosuppression (risk of severe outer-ear infection)</li>
        <li>Swelling, redness, or tenderness <strong>behind the ear</strong>, or ear pushed outward</li>
        <li>Head injury with ear bleeding/clear fluid, or new severe headache/neurologic signs</li>
        <li>Worsening vertigo, facial weakness, or a <strong>painful ear rash</strong> (possible shingles/Ramsay Hunt)</li>
        <li>Foreign object in ear, or sharp pain after inserting a cotton swab (possible eardrum tear)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can signal sudden sensorineural hearing loss, malignant otitis externa, mastoiditis, skull fracture, shingles affecting the ear, or eardrum perforation.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Ear pain and blockage causes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Condition</th><th>Typical features</th><th>Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Cerumen (earwax) impaction</strong></td>
          <td>Blocked feeling, decreased hearing, itchiness, whistling hearing aid</td>
          <td>Worse after shower/swim; improves after wax removal</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Otitis externa</strong> (“swimmer’s ear”)</td>
          <td>Outer-ear pain, tender ear canal, worse when pulling ear</td>
          <td>Recent water exposure; canal swollen/red; may have discharge</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Otitis media / effusion</strong></td>
          <td>Deep ear pain/pressure, muffled hearing, recent URI</td>
          <td>No tragus pain; may feel popping/crackling; fever sometimes</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Eustachian tube dysfunction</strong></td>
          <td>Fullness, popping, worse with altitude/flight</td>
          <td>Often after colds/allergies; may benefit from nasal sprays</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>TM perforation/barotrauma</strong></td>
          <td>Sudden pain then relief, hearing drop, possible drainage</td>
          <td>After pressure change, blast, or Q-tip; keep ear dry and seek care</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>TMJ/dental/referred pain</strong></td>
          <td>Jaw or tooth pain radiating to ear</td>
          <td>Worse with chewing, jaw clicking, dental problems</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Shingles of ear</strong> (Ramsay Hunt)</td>
          <td>Severe ear pain, vesicle rash, hearing loss, facial weakness</td>
          <td>Urgent antiviral treatment window—seek care promptly</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Chronic skin issues</strong></td>
          <td>Itchy flaky canal (eczema/psoriasis)</td>
          <td>May need medicated drops; avoid scratching</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Self-care that’s usually safe</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If no red flags</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Do not insert</strong> cotton swabs, hairpins, or ear candles</li>
          <li>For wax: try a few days of <strong>wax-softening drops</strong> (carbamide peroxide/mineral oil) unless you suspect a perforation or have ear tubes</li>
          <li>Keep ear <strong>dry</strong> during outer-ear irritation; a <em>warm compress</em> can soothe pain</li>
          <li>Chew gum/yawn and sip water to equalize pressure during colds/flight</li>
          <li>Pain control: consider <strong>acetaminophen</strong>; avoid NSAIDs if bleeding risk or kidney issues</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>See a clinician</strong> within a few days if pain/pressure or hearing issues persist, or sooner if you use hearing aids and have blockage or drainage.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medication & drops: cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Nasal steroid sprays</strong> (e.g., fluticasone) help allergies/eustachian tube problems—benefit after few days</li>
          <li><strong>Short-term</strong> nasal decongestant sprays may help for flights (use ≤3 days to avoid rebound)</li>
          <li><strong>Oral decongestants</strong> can raise BP/heart rate and worsen sleep—use cautiously and avoid with many heart conditions</li>
          <li>If the <strong>eardrum is perforated</strong>, some antibiotic drops are preferred; <em>avoid ototoxic drops</em> unless prescribed</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Hearing aids:</strong> Clean and dry domes/tubing; if feedback or blockage occurs, stop inserting until wax is cleared by a clinician.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Prevention -->
  <section id="prevention" class="section">
    <h2>Prevention & ear-care tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>After swimming/shower: tilt head to drain; gently dry the outer ear with a towel</li>
          <li>Avoid scratching the ear canal—use <strong>moisturizing drops</strong> (mineral/olive oil) if itchy and <em>no perforation</em></li>
          <li>For frequent swimmer’s ear: ask about preventive <strong>acidifying drops</strong></li>
          <li>Manage allergies to reduce eustachian tube congestion</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Travel/flight:</strong> Use filtered earplugs or nasal sprays before takeoff/landing; swallow, yawn, or do gentle Valsalva (pinch nose & blow lightly) if safe.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical evaluation">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Identify source of pain/block</td>
          <td>Otoscopy for wax/infection/perforation; jaw/teeth/throat exam</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Wax removal</strong></td>
          <td>Clear obstruction</td>
          <td>Irrigation, suction, or curette (avoid if perforation suspected)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Hearing tests</strong></td>
          <td>Assess loss type</td>
          <td>Weber/Rinne tuning fork; audiogram; tympanometry for middle-ear fluid</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs/imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Complications/atypical cases</td>
          <td>CT/MRI for severe infection, mastoid tenderness, facial weakness, or persistent unilateral symptoms</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Target cause</td>
          <td>Antibiotic/antifungal ear drops, pain control, nasal therapy, antivirals for shingles, ENT referral when needed</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Report <strong>sudden hearing loss</strong> immediately—there’s a short treatment window.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Start date/time, pain score, and triggers (chewing, pressure changes, water)</li>
          <li>Hearing changes, ringing, drainage color/amount, fever</li>
          <li>Recent colds/allergy flares, swimming, flights</li>
          <li>All medications/drops used and response</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring a short log and any hearing-aid notes to appointments—it speeds diagnosis and helps tailor treatment.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with dignity</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Help schedule timely care for <strong>sudden hearing changes</strong> or persistent pain</li>
          <li>Keep ears dry if drops are prescribed; use reminders for dosing</li>
          <li>Assist with safe hearing-aid cleaning and battery/charger checks</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> fever and severe ear pain, spreading redness/swelling, drainage with significant pain, facial weakness, or new vertigo.
      </div>
    </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is a blocked ear usually wax?</p>
        <p>Often, yes—especially with hearing aids. Safe removal by a clinician gives quick relief. Avoid cotton swabs and ear candles.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When is ear pain an emergency?</p>
        <p>Sudden hearing loss, severe pain with fever in diabetes/immunosuppression, swelling behind the ear, facial weakness, or head injury with ear bleeding.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do decongestants help ear pressure?</p>
        <p>Nasal steroids help allergies over days; brief nasal decongestant sprays may help for flights. Oral decongestants can raise blood pressure—use cautiously.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can jaw or teeth cause ear pain?</p>
        <p>Yes—TMJ or dental issues often refer pain to the ear, especially if worse with chewing or jaw movement.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
