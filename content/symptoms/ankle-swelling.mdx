  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What ankle swelling means</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> <strong>Ankle swelling (edema)</strong> happens when extra fluid collects in the tissues. It may be harmless after a long day standing—but in older adults it can also signal vein problems, heart/kidney/liver issues, <em>blood clots</em>, medication effects, or infection.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Clues to notice:</strong> one vs. both ankles, pain/redness/warmth, pitting (thumb leaves a dent), shortness of breath, weight gain, skin color changes, or new medications.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Sudden, painful <strong>one-sided</strong> swelling with redness/warmth (possible DVT)</li>
        <li>Swelling with <strong>shortness of breath</strong>, chest pain, or coughing blood</li>
        <li>Rapid weight gain (≥1–2 kg/2–5 lb in a few days) with breathlessness or waking at night gasping</li>
        <li>Skin that’s hot with spreading redness, fever, or open weeping sores</li>
        <li>New severe swelling after leg injury or recent surgery/long travel</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can signal blood clots, heart failure flare, infection (cellulitis), or bleeding.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Patterns -->
  <section id="patterns" class="section">
    <h2>Patterns & likely causes</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Patterns & causes of edema">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Pattern</th><th>More likely causes</th><th>Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Both ankles, worse by evening</strong></td>
          <td>Chronic venous insufficiency, medications (e.g., calcium-channel blockers), salt/fluid retention</td>
          <td>Improves with elevation/compression; look for brownish skin, varicose veins</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>One ankle only</strong></td>
          <td>DVT, cellulitis, sprain/fracture, gout/pseudogout, lymphatic blockage</td>
          <td>Pain/redness/warmth → urgent assessment</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Pitting edema</strong> (thumb leaves dent)</td>
          <td>Venous disease, heart/kidney/liver conditions, some meds</td>
          <td>Track daily weights and salt intake</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Non-pitting, firm skin</strong></td>
          <td>Lymphedema, longstanding venous disease, hypothyroidism</td>
          <td>Skin care and specialty compression/therapy help</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Swelling + shortness of breath</strong></td>
          <td>Heart failure, lung clots</td>
          <td>Urgent if breathing worsens, new at night, or fast weight gain</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Self-care that’s usually safe</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">At home (if no red flags)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Elevate</strong> feet above heart 20–30 minutes, 2–3× daily</li>
          <li><strong>Move the calves:</strong> ankle pumps, heel-toe raises, short walks each hour you’re awake</li>
          <li><strong>Protect skin:</strong> moisturize daily, treat cracks early; choose breathable socks</li>
          <li><strong>Shoes:</strong> supportive, wide toe box; avoid tight straps around the ankle</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call your clinician soon if:</strong> swelling is new, persists >1–2 weeks, is only on one side, or you have heart/kidney/liver disease and swelling is increasing.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Compression -->
  <section id="compression" class="section">
    <h2>Compression basics</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How to use</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Start with <strong>15–20 mmHg</strong> knee-highs in the morning; remove at bedtime</li>
          <li>Smooth wrinkles; replace pairs every 3–6 months</li>
          <li>If swelling extends above calves, ask about thigh-highs or wraps</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Important:</strong> Don’t start compression if you have <em>severe leg pain at rest, cold/pale feet, or known severe PAD</em> without clinician advice. Many people need an ankle-brachial index (ABI) check first.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Diet & Meds -->
  <section id="diet-meds" class="section">
    <h2>Salt & medication tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Daily habits</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Limit added salt and high-sodium foods (soups, processed meats, pickles); cook with herbs/spices</li>
          <li>Follow fluid guidance from your clinician, especially for heart/kidney conditions</li>
          <li>Weigh yourself daily after waking; note changes of ≥1 kg (2 lb)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <p class="kicker">Medication check (talk to your clinician/pharmacist)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>May <strong>worsen swelling:</strong> calcium-channel blockers (e.g., amlodipine), NSAIDs, steroids, thiazolidinediones for diabetes, certain hormones</li>
          <li>Diuretics (“water pills”) should be used only as prescribed; report dizziness, dehydration, or big weight swings</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical evaluation steps">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Identify pattern, risks, skin/vein/lymph signs</td>
          <td>Onset, one vs both, pain/redness, meds, travel/surgery, heart/kidney/liver history</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Leg ultrasound</strong></td>
          <td>Detect blood clots or venous disease</td>
          <td>Compression duplex for DVT; reflux study for varicose/valve issues</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Blood/urine tests</strong></td>
          <td>Assess organ causes</td>
          <td>Kidney/liver function, electrolytes, albumin, thyroid (selected), urine protein</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Heart evaluation</strong></td>
          <td>Look for heart failure</td>
          <td>BNP, chest X-ray, echocardiogram as indicated</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>ABI (ankle-brachial index)</strong></td>
          <td>Check artery flow before compression</td>
          <td>Guides safe compression strength</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Treatment depends on cause—vein care, diuretic adjustment, antibiotics for cellulitis, anticoagulation for DVT, or lymphedema therapy.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Daily weight</strong> (morning after bathroom)</li>
          <li><strong>Ankle circumference</strong> at the same spot each day</li>
          <li>One vs both sides; time of day; pitting vs non-pitting</li>
          <li>Breathlessness, cough at night, or waking to breathe</li>
          <li>New meds or salty meals before swelling worsened</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring notes/photos to visits—helps find triggers and adjust treatment quickly.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with dignity</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Help with morning compression socks; check skin daily (heels/ankles/shins)</li>
          <li>Encourage short walks/ankle pumps during TV or reading breaks</li>
          <li>Use low-salt shopping/cooking; track weights on a simple chart</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> fast weight gain, new shortness of breath, one-sided painful swelling, fever/redness, or open sores that don’t heal.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is evening ankle swelling normal?</p>
        <p>Mild, painless swelling after sitting/standing can be common. If new, worsening, one-sided, or with breathlessness, get checked.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Should I just take a water pill?</p>
        <p>Not without guidance. Diuretics can help some causes but can also cause dehydration, dizziness, or kidney issues if used incorrectly.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do compression socks really work?</p>
        <p>Yes for many people with venous swelling—if properly fitted and safe to use. Ask about an ABI test if you have artery disease.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can salt really make ankles swell?</p>
        <p>Yes. High sodium increases fluid retention. Cooking with less salt and avoiding packaged high-sodium foods often helps within days.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
