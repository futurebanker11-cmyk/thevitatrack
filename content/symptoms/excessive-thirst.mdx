  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What excessive thirst can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Feeling unusually thirsty (often with frequent urination) can signal <strong>high blood sugar</strong>, <strong>dehydration</strong>, medicine side effects, or less commonly problems with the kidneys or the hormone that balances water (ADH).</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Watch for companions:</strong> peeing more than usual, dry mouth, fatigue, blurry vision, weight loss, leg cramps, new nighttime urination. New or worsening thirst deserves attention—especially in people with diabetes or on diuretics.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Very high blood sugar (e.g., <strong>&gt;300 mg/dL</strong> or <strong>&gt;16.7 mmol/L</strong>) with nausea, vomiting, or drowsiness</li>
        <li>Fruity breath, deep/rapid breathing, belly pain, confusion (possible diabetic ketoacidosis)</li>
        <li>Severe dehydration: fainting, minimal urine, very dry mouth, fast heartbeat, confusion</li>
        <li>Thirst with <strong>chest pain</strong>, stroke symptoms, or fever with altered mental status</li>
        <li>Excessive water intake with headache, confusion, or seizures (possible low sodium)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These may require urgent fluids, electrolyte checks, and treatment for diabetic emergencies or other causes.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes by category</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Common causes of excessive thirst">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Category</th><th scope="col">Examples</th><th scope="col">Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Diabetes & high blood sugar</strong></td>
          <td>Type 2 diabetes, steroid-induced hyperglycemia; rarely new-onset type 1 in adults</td>
          <td>Frequent urination, blurry vision, fatigue, weight loss; home glucose often high</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Dehydration</strong></td>
          <td>Heat, illness (fever/diarrhea), low intake, vomiting</td>
          <td>Dark urine, dizziness on standing, dry skin/mouth</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Medicines</strong></td>
          <td>Diuretics, SGLT2 inhibitors, lithium, anticholinergics, antipsychotics, high-dose vitamin D/calcium</td>
          <td>Onset after starting/changing dose; may have dry mouth or increased urination</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Electrolyte/hormone issues</strong></td>
          <td>High calcium, low potassium, high sodium</td>
          <td>Constipation, abdominal pain, confusion with severe imbalances</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Diabetes insipidus</strong></td>
          <td>Central (low ADH), nephrogenic (kidneys resist ADH; e.g., from lithium)</td>
          <td>Large volumes of very dilute urine; labs show high sodium/osmolality</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Behavioral/other</strong></td>
          <td>High-salt diet, dry environments, anxiety, primary/psychogenic polydipsia</td>
          <td>Often normal labs; symptoms vary with intake and stress</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Safe steps at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">First 24–48 hours (no red-flags)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Hydrate steadily:</strong> small, frequent sips of water; add an oral rehydration solution (ORS) if you’ve had diarrhea/vomiting or heat exposure</li>
          <li><strong>Check blood sugar</strong> if you have diabetes; follow your sick-day plan and call your clinician if readings are repeatedly high</li>
          <li><strong>Review salt/sugar intake:</strong> cut back on very salty foods and sugary drinks that worsen thirst</li>
          <li><strong>Cool environment:</strong> rest in shade/AC, use a fan, and avoid exertion in heat</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <p class="kicker">Call your clinician soon if</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Thirst persists beyond 48 hours without clear cause</li>
          <li>You’re urinating excessively at night or producing very large volumes of dilute urine</li>
          <li>There’s <strong>unintentional weight loss</strong>, new blurry vision, or recurrent infections</li>
          <li>Thirst begins after a <strong>new medication</strong> (diuretic, lithium, SGLT2 inhibitor, steroids)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small">This page is educational and not a diagnosis. Seek urgent care for the warning signs above.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds-caution" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine notes & cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Diuretics</strong> (water pills) increase urination and can dehydrate—ask about dose/timing adjustments.</li>
          <li><strong>SGLT2 inhibitors</strong> (for diabetes) cause glucose loss in urine → more thirst/urination; keep hydrated and report dizziness or UTIs.</li>
          <li><strong>Lithium</strong> can cause nephrogenic diabetes insipidus; do not stop suddenly—contact your prescriber if thirst/urination surge.</li>
          <li><strong>Steroids</strong> and some antipsychotics raise blood sugar; monitor if you have diabetes or risks.</li>
          <li><strong>Anticholinergics</strong> dry the mouth and may feel like thirst; consider alternatives if bothersome.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        Do <em>not</em> change or stop prescriptions on your own. Bring a complete medication/supplement list to visits.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Diet -->
  <section id="diet" class="section">
    <h2>Hydration & diet tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Helpful choices</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Water as main beverage; add slices of citrus or mint for taste</li>
          <li>ORS or broths during illness/heat; aim for <strong>pale-yellow urine</strong></li>
          <li>Fruits/vegetables with high water content (melon, cucumbers, citrus, tomatoes)</li>
          <li>If diabetic: choose <strong>no-sugar</strong> beverages; spread carbs evenly through the day</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Limit/avoid</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Sugary drinks (sodas/juices) that spike blood sugar and worsen thirst</li>
          <li>Very salty foods (pickles, chips, processed meats) that drive thirst</li>
          <li>Excess alcohol and high-caffeine drinks—can dehydrate</li>
          <li><strong>Over-drinking plain water</strong> rapidly (e.g., &gt;1 L/hour) → risk of low sodium; balance with electrolytes if sweating/ill</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When is thirst an emergency?</p>
        <p>With very high blood sugar, vomiting, confusion, deep/rapid breathing, or signs of severe dehydration. Call emergency services.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can medicines cause excessive thirst?</p>
        <p>Yes—diuretics, SGLT2 inhibitors, lithium, steroids, and anticholinergics are common culprits. Don’t stop them yourself; ask about adjustments.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How much should I drink?</p>
        <p>Sip regularly to keep urine pale yellow. Avoid quickly chugging large volumes of plain water—use ORS if sweating or ill.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What tests will I need?</p>
        <p>Glucose, electrolytes, kidney function, urinalysis, and sometimes osmolality testing to check for diabetes insipidus or other causes.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
