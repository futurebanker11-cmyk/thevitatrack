  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What wounds & skin tears can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Older skin is thinner and tears easily. Even small wounds can bleed more (especially on blood thinners) and get infected faster. Good <strong>bleeding control, gentle cleaning, moist wound care,</strong> and timely help reduce complications and scarring.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Look for:</strong> depth/gaping, contamination (dirt, bite), loss of feeling/motion, color/temperature change of the limb, and signs of infection (spreading redness, warmth, pus, fever).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Bleeding won’t stop</strong> after firm direct pressure for 10–15 minutes, or blood spurts</li>
        <li>Deep, gaping wounds; <strong>exposed fat, tendon, bone</strong>; or you can’t fully move/feel the area</li>
        <li>Severe crush injury, large skin flap loss, or signs of poor circulation (pale/blue, cold, numb)</li>
        <li>Redness rapidly spreading, pus with fever/chills, or streaks toward the body</li>
        <li>Dirty/puncture wounds (nails, farm/soil), <strong>animal/human bites</strong>, or glass/foreign body you can’t remove safely</li>
        <li>Head injury on <strong>blood thinners</strong> (warfarin, DOACs) even if cut seems small</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These may require sutures, imaging, antibiotics, tetanus care, or surgery. Don’t drive yourself if bleeding heavily or dizzy.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

        <tr>
          <td><strong>Laceration</strong></td>
          <td>Cut with edges that may gape</td>
          <td>May need sutures/strips/glue within hours; control bleeding first</td>

          <td><strong>Abrasion</strong></td>
          <td>Scraped skin with embedded dirt</td>
          <td>Rinse well; avoid harsh chemicals inside the wound</td>
        </tr>

          <td>Small surface hole, deeper track</td>

          <td>Skin breakdown over bony areas</td>
          <td>Off-load pressure, keep clean/moist, monitor closely</td>

          <td>Foot/ankle ulcers, slow healing</td>

          <td>Punctures, crush, contamination</td>

  <!-- Do now -->
  <section id="do-now" class="section">
    <h2>First-aid: what to do now</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Bleeding control</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Wash your hands</strong> or use sanitizer. If available, put on clean gloves.</li>
          <li>Apply <strong>direct pressure</strong> with clean cloth/gauze for 10–15 minutes without peeking.</li>
          <li>Elevate the area above the heart if possible. Add more layers if soaking; don’t remove the base layer.</li>
          <li>If trained and still bleeding, consider a <strong>hemostatic gauze</strong> pad; seek care if uncontrolled.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Gentle cleaning & covering</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Rinse under <strong>clean running water</strong> for several minutes. Use mild soap around (not inside) the wound.</li>
          <li><strong>Avoid</strong> pouring hydrogen peroxide, iodine, or alcohol into the wound—they damage healing tissue.</li>
          <li>For <strong>skin tears</strong>, gently place the flap back in position with moistened cotton tip; do not overly stretch.</li>
          <li>Apply a thin layer of <strong>plain petroleum</strong> or antibiotic ointment if advised; cover with a <strong>non-stick</strong> pad and light wrap.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small">This page is educational and not a diagnosis. Seek urgent care for the warning signs above.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>If mild & stable</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Daily care</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Change dressing <strong>daily</strong> or if wet/dirty. Keep the wound <strong>moist (not wet)</strong> and covered.</li>
          <li>Watch for infection: increasing redness, warmth, swelling, pain, pus, fever, or bad odor.</li>
          <li>Protect skin: long sleeves, shin guards for walkers, remove sharp furniture edges, good lighting to prevent bumps.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Diabetes/poor circulation:</strong> check feet daily, wear protective footwear, off-load pressure points, and call early for any breakdown or redness.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds-caution" class="section">
    <h2>Tetanus, antibiotics & medicine cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Tetanus:</strong> If your last shot was <strong>&gt;10 years</strong> ago (clean/minor wound) or <strong>&gt;5 years</strong> ago (dirty/puncture wounds), you may need a booster. Bring vaccine records.</li>
          <li><strong>Antibiotics:</strong> Not needed for most clean cuts. Often used for bites, deep punctures, heavily contaminated wounds, diabetic feet, or spreading infection—per clinician advice.</li>
          <li><strong>Blood thinners/antiplatelets</strong> (warfarin, DOACs, clopidogrel) increase bleeding—apply longer pressure and seek help sooner if bleeding persists.</li>
          <li><strong>Steroids</strong> (pills or topical) can slow healing; review long-term use with your clinician.</li>
          <li><strong>Topicals:</strong> Avoid strong antiseptics inside the wound. Fragrance-free moisturizers help surrounding fragile skin.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        Keep an updated <strong>medicine & vaccine list</strong> and note any drug allergies before starting antibiotics.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Diet -->
  <section id="diet" class="section">
    <h2>Nutrition & pressure relief</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support healing</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Aim for <strong>adequate protein</strong> at each meal (eggs, dairy/curd, dal/beans, fish, chicken, tofu/paneer).</li>
          <li>Include <strong>vitamin C</strong> (citrus, berries, tomatoes) and <strong>zinc</strong> sources (meat, beans, nuts). Supplements only if advised.</li>
          <li>Stay hydrated; limit alcohol and smoking—they slow healing.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Off-loading for ulcers/tears</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Change position at least every <strong>2 hours</strong> in bed; use cushions to protect heels/elbows.</li>
          <li>Keep sheets wrinkle-free; avoid shear when repositioning (lift, don’t drag).</li>
          <li>Use well-fitting shoes; inspect feet daily, especially if you have neuropathy.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How do I stop bleeding?</p>
        <p>Use firm, direct pressure with clean gauze for 10–15 minutes without lifting to look. Elevate the area. Add layers if soaking—don’t remove the base layer.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Should I use peroxide or iodine?</p>
        <p>No inside the wound. Rinse with clean water; use mild soap around the edges. Peroxide/iodine can damage healing tissue.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I need antibiotics?</p>
        <p>Not for most clean cuts. Bites, deep punctures, and contaminated wounds often need them. A clinician will decide based on risk.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When should stitches be placed?</p>
        <p>Gaping/deep cuts or those on the face/joints may need closure within several hours. Seek timely care to reduce infection and scarring.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
