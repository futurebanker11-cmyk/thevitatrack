      <!-- 2 What is rotator cuff -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What is the rotator cuff? Common symptoms <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <p>The <strong>rotator cuff</strong> is a group of four muscles and tendons that keep the shoulder ball centered and help lift and rotate your arm. With age, overuse, or injury, these tendons can become irritated (<em>tendinopathy</em>) or tear. Pain is often felt on the outside of the shoulder and upper arm, worse when lifting, reaching overhead, or lying on that side.</p>
              <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Typical symptoms">
                <table class="responsive">
                  <thead><tr><th>Problem</th><th>What you may notice</th></tr></thead>
                  <tbody>
                    <tr>
                      <td data-col="Problem">Tendinopathy/strain</td>
                      <td data-col="What you may notice">Aching with lifting or reaching; stiffness after rest</td>
                    </tr>
                    <tr>
                      <td data-col="Problem">Bursitis</td>
                      <td data-col="What you may notice">Sharp pain with overhead motion; tenderness at top/outside of shoulder</td>
                    </tr>
                    <tr>
                      <td data-col="Problem">Partial tear</td>
                      <td data-col="What you may notice">Weakness lifting to the side; night pain</td>
                    </tr>
                    <tr>
                      <td data-col="Problem">Full-thickness tear</td>
                      <td data-col="What you may notice">Sudden weakness after injury; difficulty raising arm</td>
                    </tr>
                  </tbody>
                </table>
              </div>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Neck link:</strong> Neck arthritis or pinched nerves can mimic shoulder pain. If pain shoots below the elbow with tingling in fingers, ask your clinician about the neck.
            </div>

      <!-- 3 First aid -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Gentle first aid (first 48–72 hours) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ol>
            <li><strong>Relative rest:</strong> Avoid painful overhead reach and heavy lifting, but keep the arm moving at waist-height to prevent stiffness.</li>
            <li><strong>Cold, then warm:</strong> Cold packs 10–15 minutes up to 3×/day for sharp pain. After day 2–3, consider warm shower or heating pad 10 minutes before gentle exercises.</li>
            <li><strong>Support for sleep:</strong> Lie on the non-painful side with a pillow hugging the arm, or lie on your back with a small pillow under the elbow so the shoulder rests.</li>
            <li><strong>Medicines:</strong> Use only as prescribed (pain relievers, topical gels). If you have kidney, heart, or stomach problems, <em>avoid self-dosing</em> anti-inflammatories—ask your clinician.</li>
          </ol>

      <!-- 4 Triggers -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Common triggers & daily habits to fix <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Overhead repetition:</strong> Hanging laundry high, high shelves, long painting/cleaning sessions</li>
            <li><strong>Sudden load:</strong> Lifting a heavy suitcase or bucket with the arm stretched out</li>
            <li><strong>Posture:</strong> Rounded shoulders, low screen height, long phone scrolling</li>
            <li><strong>Sleep pressure:</strong> Lying on the painful shoulder or with arm above the head</li>
            <li><strong>Deconditioning:</strong> Weak shoulder blades and core increase cuff strain</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 5 Starter movements -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Starter movements (pain-free range) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Pendulum (relax)</h3>
              <p>Lean on a counter with the good arm. Let the sore arm dangle. Gently sway the body so the arm draws small circles. 30–60 seconds, 2–3×/day.</p>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Table slide (flexion)</h3>
              <p>Sit at a table. Place both hands on a towel. Slide hands forward, letting the sore shoulder bend comfortably. Hold 2–3 seconds, repeat 10 times.</p>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Wall crawl (to tolerance)</h3>
              <p>Face a wall. Walk fingers up within comfort. Stop before sharp pain. Hold 3 seconds, walk down. Repeat 8–10 times. No forcing.</p>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 6 Posture & setup -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Posture, daily setup, and sleep tips <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Screen level:</strong> Raise screens to eye height; bring work closer to avoid reaching.</li>
            <li><strong>Elbows near the body:</strong> Keep items between waist and chest height. Use two hands for heavier objects.</li>
            <li><strong>Breaks:</strong> Every 30–45 minutes, roll shoulders back and down 5 times.</li>
            <li><strong>Sleep:</strong> Non-painful side with a pillow between arms, or on the back with a small pillow under the elbow/wrist.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 7 Medicines overview -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Medicines & injections (talk with your clinician) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Pain relief options</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Topical gels/patches; oral pain relievers <em>only</em> if safe for your kidneys, heart, and stomach</li>
                <li>Short course anti-inflammatory medicines when appropriate</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Injections</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Subacromial steroid injections may calm bursitis</li>
                <li>Image-guided injections can improve accuracy</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Medicine plans should be individualized—especially if you take blood thinners or have kidney, heart, or stomach conditions.</p>

            <li>Sudden loss of strength after a fall or lift</li>
            <li>Night pain that does not improve</li>
            <li>Repeated shoulder dislocations or a feeling of slipping out</li>

      <!-- 10 Myths -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Myths to ignore <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>“No pain, no gain.”</strong> Sharp pain is a stop sign. Gentle motion is good; forcing is not.</li>
            <li><strong>“Rest until it’s gone.”</strong> Too much rest stiffens the joint. Move within comfort each day.</li>
            <li><strong>“Surgery is the only fix.”</strong> Many seniors do well with targeted therapy and habit changes.</li>
          </ul>

      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">How long does rotator cuff pain take to heal?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Mild strains may calm in 2–6 weeks with home care and gentle exercises. Larger tears or long-standing pain can take months and may need specialist care.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Should I use a sling?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">A sling is sometimes used briefly after injury, but long use can stiffen the shoulder. Ask your clinician and begin gentle motion as soon as it’s safe.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Which exercises should I avoid?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Avoid painful overhead presses, heavy lateral raises with straight elbows, and sudden jerking moves. Build up with light bands, elbows near your sides.</p>
