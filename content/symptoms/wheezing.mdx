  <!-- About -->
  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What wheezing can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> <strong>Wheezing</strong> is a high-pitched whistling sound when breathing—usually from narrowed airways. In older adults, common causes include <strong>asthma</strong>, <strong>COPD</strong>, <strong>infections</strong> (bronchitis, pneumonia, flu/COVID), <strong>heart failure</strong> (“cardiac asthma”), <strong>allergic reactions</strong>, <strong>aspiration</strong> (food/liquid into lungs), and <strong>medication effects</strong> (beta-blockers, NSAID/aspirin sensitivity).</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Describe:</strong> onset, triggers (cold air, exertion, perfumes), cough/phlegm, fever, chest tightness, leg swelling, exposure to allergens, new medicines, and oxygen/peak-flow readings if you use them.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Severe shortness of breath, <strong>can’t speak full sentences</strong>, ribs/neck pulling in, or lips/fingertips turning blue</li>
        <li>Wheezing with <strong>hives, swelling of face/tongue, or throat tightness</strong> (possible anaphylaxis)</li>
        <li>New confusion, fainting, or oxygen saturation &lt;92% (if you monitor) despite treatment</li>
        <li>Chest pain/pressure, fast or irregular heartbeat</li>
        <li>High fever with shaking chills, brown/rust sputum, or coughing up blood</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can signal life-threatening airway narrowing, severe infection, or heart problems. Seek urgent care now.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Wheezing causes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Cause</th><th>Typical features</th><th>Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Asthma (late-onset)</strong></td>
          <td>Episodic wheeze, chest tightness, cough (often at night)</td>
          <td>Triggers: allergens, exercise, cold air; improves with bronchodilator</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>COPD (emphysema/chronic bronchitis)</strong></td>
          <td>Daily cough/phlegm, exertional breathlessness</td>
          <td>Smoking history; frequent winter flare-ups</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Infections</strong></td>
          <td>Wheeze with fever, cough, sputum</td>
          <td>Viral bronchitis/flu/COVID; pneumonia if fever + chest pain + fast breathing</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Heart failure</strong></td>
          <td>Breathlessness when lying flat, nighttime cough</td>
          <td>Leg swelling, rapid weight gain; crackles ± wheeze</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Allergic reaction/anaphylaxis</strong></td>
          <td>Sudden wheeze, hives, swelling</td>
          <td>Food, drugs, insect stings; needs epinephrine and emergency care</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Aspiration / reflux</strong></td>
          <td>Cough/wheeze after eating or at night</td>
          <td>Swallowing problems, heartburn; risk of pneumonia</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Medication effects</strong></td>
          <td>New or worse wheeze after starting a drug</td>
          <td>Non-selective beta-blockers, NSAIDs/aspirin (AERD), ACE-inhibitor cough</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Airway issues</strong></td>
          <td>Localized wheeze or stridor</td>
          <td>Foreign body, vocal cord dysfunction, tumors—needs evaluation</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Home steps that help</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If no red flags</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Sit upright, loosen tight clothing, <strong>pursed-lip breathe</strong> (inhale 2 sec, exhale 4–6 sec)</li>
          <li>Use your <strong>quick-relief inhaler</strong> (albuterol): 2 puffs with spacer; repeat every 20 minutes up to 3 times if needed</li>
          <li>Start your <strong>action plan</strong> if you have one (add ipratropium/nebulizer as prescribed)</li>
          <li>Warm fluids and a <strong>humid, not hot</strong> shower can loosen mucus (avoid steam burns)</li>
          <li>Limit smoke, perfumes, strong cleaners; keep windows shut on high-pollen or very cold days</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call your clinician</strong> the same day if you need your rescue inhaler more than every 4 hours, if symptoms last &gt;24–48 hours, or if you have new fever, chest pain, or swelling.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medication & inhaler notes</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Rescue bronchodilators</strong> (albuterol, levalbuterol): relief in minutes; side-effects include tremor, jitteriness, or palpitations</li>
          <li><strong>Controllers</strong> (inhaled corticosteroids ± LABA; LAMA): reduce flares—<em>use daily</em> as directed</li>
          <li><strong>Ipratropium/tiotropium</strong> can help COPD and some asthma flares</li>
          <li><strong>Oral steroids</strong> are for moderate–severe flares per plan—short course; discuss diabetes/bone/bleeding risks</li>
          <li><strong>After ICS</strong>: rinse, gargle, and spit to prevent thrush/hoarseness</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Check interactions:</strong> non-selective beta-blockers (e.g., propranolol) and high-dose NSAIDs/aspirin may worsen wheeze in susceptible people. Never stop heart meds without medical advice—ask for safer alternatives (e.g., cardio-selective beta-blockers).
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Technique -->
  <section id="technique" class="section">
    <h2>Spacer & breathing tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Metered-dose inhaler with spacer</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Shake inhaler; attach to spacer; exhale fully</li>
          <li>Press once; take a <strong>slow, deep breath</strong> over 3–5 seconds</li>
          <li>Hold breath 10 seconds; exhale slowly. Wait 30–60 seconds before the next puff</li>
          <li>If hand weakness or poor timing, ask about <strong>breath-actuated</strong> or <strong>nebulizer</strong> options</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Pursed-lip breathing:</strong> Breathe in through your nose for 2 seconds, then out through pursed lips for 4–6 seconds. Practice when calm so it’s easy during flares.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical evaluation for wheezing">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>

        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Assess severity & likely cause</td>
          <td>Breathing rate/effort, pulse oximetry, chest sounds, edema</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Bronchodilator trial</strong></td>
          <td>See response</td>
          <td>Albuterol/ipratropium via inhaler or nebulizer</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Imaging & tests</strong></td>
          <td>Look for infection/heart strain</td>
          <td>Chest X-ray; viral testing; ECG/BNP if heart failure suspected</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Spirometry/peak flow</strong></td>
          <td>Diagnose asthma/COPD</td>
          <td>Pre-/post-bronchodilator measurements</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs</strong></td>
          <td>Guide treatment</td>
          <td>Blood gases in severe cases; eosinophils/IgE if allergic phenotype</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Relieve obstruction & treat cause</td>
          <td>SABA/SAMA, systemic steroids, oxygen if needed, antibiotics for confirmed bacterial infection, diuretics for heart failure, epinephrine for anaphylaxis</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Follow-up ensures inhaler technique, trigger control, and vaccine updates (flu, COVID, pneumonia).</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Symptom scores (breathlessness, cough, sleep disruption)</li>
          <li>Rescue inhaler use (puffs/day) and response time</li>
          <li>Peak flow/oxygen readings if you use them; note personal best</li>
          <li>Fever, sputum color/amount, swelling or rapid weight change</li>
          <li>Triggers (weather, pollen, smoke, infections, meals/reflux)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring your log and inhalers/spacer to visits—technique checks often improve control quickly.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with calm & readiness</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Help position upright, cue pursed-lip breathing, and locate the rescue inhaler/spacer</li>
          <li>Count breaths, watch ability to speak, check pulse-ox if available</li>
          <li>Know the person’s action plan and allergies; avoid smoke/irritants at home</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician or emergency services</strong> for: severe breathlessness, minimal response to rescue inhaler, new blue lips/fingertips, confusion, chest pain, or signs of allergic reaction.
      </div>
    </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is wheezing always asthma?</p>
        <p>No. COPD, infections, heart failure, allergies, reflux/aspiration, and some medicines can all cause wheeze. The pattern and tests point to the cause.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What’s the difference between wheeze and stridor?</p>
        <p><strong>Wheeze</strong> is usually during exhalation from lower airways. <strong>Stridor</strong> is a harsh sound on inhalation from upper airway narrowing—treat as urgent.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do antibiotics help wheezing?</p>
        <p>Only if there’s a confirmed bacterial infection (e.g., pneumonia). Most wheezy flares from asthma/COPD are treated with inhalers ± steroids.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How often can I use my rescue inhaler?</p>
        <p>During a flare: 2 puffs, repeat every 20 minutes up to 3 times. If you still struggle to breathe or need it again within 3–4 hours, seek medical advice.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
