  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What itching can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> <strong>Pruritus</strong> (itching) is very common with age due to <strong>dry skin</strong> and a thinner skin barrier. Other causes include <strong>eczema/contact dermatitis</strong>, <strong>hives</strong>, <strong>scabies/bedbugs</strong>, <strong>medication reactions</strong>, and internal issues like <strong>kidney, liver, thyroid, iron deficiency, or diabetes</strong>. Itching may occur with or without a visible rash.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Describe:</strong> sudden vs gradual, constant vs worse at night, location (scalp, back, folds), new soaps/clothes/meds, travel/exposures, and any weight loss, fever, night sweats, jaundice, or dark urine.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Itching with <strong>swelling of lips/tongue</strong>, trouble breathing, or dizziness/fainting (possible anaphylaxis)</li>
        <li>Rapidly spreading rash with <strong>hives + wheeze</strong>, or <strong>blisters/skin peeling</strong> (severe drug reaction)</li>
        <li>New <strong>jaundice</strong> (yellow skin/eyes), dark urine, or pale stools</li>
        <li>Fever with a painful or rapidly worsening skin infection</li>
        <li>Severe itching after starting a <strong>new medicine</strong> (especially antibiotics, anticonvulsants, allopurinol)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can indicate severe allergy, Stevens–Johnson syndrome/TEN, cholestasis, or serious infection.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Itch causes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Cause</th><th>Typical features</th><th>Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Dry skin (xerosis)</strong></td>
          <td>Fine scaling on shins/arms/back; worse in winter and after hot showers</td>
          <td>Improves with thick moisturizers and shorter lukewarm bathing</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Eczema / contact dermatitis</strong></td>
          <td>Red, itchy patches; may ooze/crust</td>
          <td>New soaps, fragrances, dyes, or jewelry; improves when avoided</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Hives (urticaria)</strong></td>
          <td>Raised, itchy welts that move around; each spot &lt;24h</td>
          <td>Often triggered by infection, foods, heat, or meds</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Scabies / bites</strong></td>
          <td>Severe night itch; small bumps/lines in finger webs, wrists, waist</td>
          <td>Household contacts itchy; needs prescription treatment & cleaning</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Medication reaction</strong></td>
          <td>Generalized itch with/without widespread rash</td>
          <td>Recent new or dose-changed medicine (opioids, statins, antibiotics, diuretics)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Kidney disease (uremic itch)</strong></td>
          <td>Generalized itch, often worse at night; minimal rash</td>
          <td>History of CKD/dialysis; dry skin common</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Liver/bile duct problems</strong></td>
          <td>Generalized itch, palms/soles; may have jaundice or dark urine</td>
          <td>Worsens at night; look for pale stools, RUQ discomfort</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Thyroid, iron deficiency, diabetes</strong></td>
          <td>Itch with dry skin or recurrent infections</td>
          <td>Weight/temperature changes (thyroid), fatigue/pale (iron)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Neuropathic/psychogenic itch</strong></td>
          <td>Itch without rash; localized burning/tingling or linked to stress</td>
          <td>Post-shingles areas, neck/back nerve compression, mood symptoms</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-checks -->
  <section id="self-check" class="section">
    <h2>Simple self-checks</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">At home (if no red flags)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>List all <strong>new products</strong> (soap, detergent, lotions) and <strong>meds/supplements</strong> from the last 4 weeks</li>
          <li>Note timing (day vs night), locations, and scratching-related <strong>skin breaks</strong></li>
          <li>Check for <strong>household itching</strong> or recent travel/visitors (possible scabies/bedbugs)</li>
          <li>Look for jaundice, leg swelling, or weight loss/night sweats</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Bring to visits:</strong> product/medication list, photos of rash, and a 1–2 week symptom diary (what worsens/helps).
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Skin care that soothes</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Lukewarm</strong> 5–10 min baths/showers; avoid hot water</li>
          <li>Apply a <strong>thick, fragrance-free moisturizer</strong> within 3 minutes of bathing (ointment or cream, not lotion)</li>
          <li>Use <strong>gentle cleansers</strong> (fragrance/dye-free); switch to <strong>free-&-clear</strong> laundry detergent</li>
          <li>Wear <strong>soft cotton</strong>; avoid wool and tight clothing</li>
          <li>Short nails; consider <strong>cotton gloves</strong> at night; cool compresses</li>
          <li>Oatmeal or baking-soda baths; menthol/camphor lotion for brief relief</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Sleep & stress:</strong> Keep room cool, use a humidifier in dry seasons, and practice brief relaxation breathing to reduce the “itch–scratch” cycle.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medication notes</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Topical steroids</strong> (low–mid strength) can calm inflamed patches for <em>1–2 weeks</em>; avoid face/groin unless directed</li>
          <li><strong>Non-sedating antihistamines</strong> help hives; sedating types may help sleep but increase <strong>fall/confusion risk</strong> in older adults—use cautiously</li>
          <li><strong>Scabies</strong> needs prescription permethrin or oral therapy for patient and close contacts</li>
          <li>For <strong>cholestatic or uremic itch</strong>, clinicians may use bile-acid binders, gabapentin, mirtazapine, or phototherapy—don’t self-start</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Medication review:</strong> Ask to reassess drugs that can worsen itch (opioids, niacin, hydroxychloroquine, some statins/diuretics). Never stop critical meds without guidance.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Triggers -->
  <section id="triggers" class="section">
    <h2>Trigger checklist</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Hot showers, dry air, wool fabrics</li>
          <li>Fragranced soaps, bubble baths, fabric softeners</li>
          <li>Heat, sweat, stress, alcohol, spicy foods (histamine release)</li>
          <li>New <strong>medications/supplements</strong> (start dates!)</li>
          <li>Pets, travel stays, secondhand furniture (mites/bedbugs)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Remove/replace one potential trigger at a time for 1–2 weeks to spot patterns.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical evaluation for pruritus">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & skin exam</strong></td>
          <td>Identify primary rash vs. scratch changes</td>
          <td>Look at scalp, back, finger webs, body folds; dermatoscopy</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Basic labs</strong></td>
          <td>Screen for internal causes</td>
          <td>CBC, ferritin/iron, CMP (liver/bilirubin), creatinine/eGFR, TSH, fasting glucose/A1C ± hepatitis tests</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Targeted tests</strong></td>
          <td>Confirm suspected conditions</td>
          <td>Skin scraping for scabies, patch testing (contact allergy), bile acids, stool/parasite tests when relevant</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Relieve itch & treat cause</td>
          <td>Topicals, antihistamines for hives, antiparasitics, phototherapy, systemic agents for kidney/liver-related itch</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Seek care sooner if itching is <strong>generalized without a clear rash</strong>, lasts &gt;4 weeks, or comes with systemic symptoms.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Daily itch score (0–10), worst times, and sleep disruption</li>
          <li>Locations and any visible rash (photos help)</li>
          <li>New products/meds, foods, heat/exercise, stress events</li>
          <li>Scratching injuries or signs of infection (pus, increasing redness)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        A simple 1–2 week diary often reveals triggers and shortens time to relief.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with dignity</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Offer fragrance-free moisturizers within 3 minutes after bathing</li>
          <li>Keep nails short; consider mitts at night if scratching causes wounds</li>
          <li>Help identify and remove triggers (detergents, fabrics, pets in bed)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> widespread rash, fever, jaundice, new swelling/breathing trouble, or skin infections from scratching.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is all-over itch without a rash serious?</p>
        <p>Often dry skin or meds—but persistent generalized itch can signal kidney, liver, thyroid, iron deficiency, or diabetes. Get basic labs if it lasts &gt;4 weeks.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What’s the best moisturizer?</p>
        <p>Thick, fragrance-free <strong>creams/ointments</strong> (petrolatum, ceramides, urea) beat lotions. Apply right after bathing.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do antihistamines help?</p>
        <p>They help <strong>hives</strong>. For dry-skin itch, moisturizers & topical steroids (short courses) work better. Sedating antihistamines can increase falls—use cautiously.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Could it be scabies?</p>
        <p>Consider if <strong>night-time itch</strong>, finger-web bumps, and other household members are itchy. Needs prescription treatment—see a clinician.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
