  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What swallowing trouble can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Swallowing trouble (<strong>dysphagia</strong>) ranges from mild “food sticking” to choking risk. In seniors, causes include <strong>throat/neurologic problems</strong> (stroke, Parkinson’s), <strong>esophageal narrowing</strong> (stricture, rings), <strong>reflux injury</strong>, <strong>motility disorders</strong> (achalasia), mouth/dental issues, and <strong>medicines</strong>.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Two main types:</strong> <em>Oropharyngeal</em> (trouble starting a swallow, coughing/choking right away) and <em>esophageal</em> (food feels stuck lower down seconds after swallowing).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Inability to swallow saliva</strong> with drooling, or complete blockage after food (“steakhouse syndrome”)</li>
        <li>Breathing difficulty, <strong>blue lips</strong>, repeated choking with poor oxygen levels</li>
        <li>Sudden swallowing trouble with <strong>one-sided weakness</strong>, facial droop, or speech changes (possible stroke)</li>
        <li>Swallowed a <strong>button battery</strong>, sharp object, or large bone</li>
        <li>Severe chest pain after swallowing, blood in vomit, or persistent vomiting</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These may need urgent endoscopy, airway protection, or stroke treatment—do not delay.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Patterns -->
  <section id="patterns" class="section">
    <h2>Patterns & common causes</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Swallowing patterns and causes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Pattern/Clue</th><th scope="col">More likely</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Cough/choke right away</strong>; nasal regurgitation; weak voice</td>
          <td>Oropharyngeal dysphagia</td>
          <td>Stroke, Parkinson’s, dementia, myasthenia, poor dentition; high aspiration risk—SLP evaluation</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Food “sticks” lower down</strong> a few seconds after swallow</td>
          <td>Esophageal stricture, Schatzki ring, cancer</td>
          <td>Often progressive, worse with solids → needs endoscopic evaluation</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Burning/heartburn; nighttime cough</strong></td>
          <td>GERD with esophagitis</td>
          <td>Acid control and lifestyle measures help; assess for narrowing</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Intermittent solids & liquids</strong>; chest pain; regurgitation of undigested food</td>
          <td>Motility disorder (e.g., achalasia, spasm)</td>
          <td>May need manometry and targeted therapy</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Pain on swallowing</strong> (odynophagia)</td>
          <td>Pill injury, infection, severe reflux</td>
          <td>Bisphosphonates, doxycycline, KCl can injure esophagus—review pill habits</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Allergies/asthma, food impactions</strong></td>
          <td>Eosinophilic esophagitis (EoE)</td>
          <td>Often younger but can occur in adults; biopsy confirms; diet/meds help</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Weight loss, anemia, progressive symptoms</strong></td>
          <td>Malignancy until proven otherwise</td>
          <td>Urgent GI referral for imaging/endoscopy</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <div class="note" style="margin-top:12px">
      <strong>Dental & mouth issues</strong> (dry mouth, ill-fitting dentures, thrush, sores) commonly worsen swallowing—addressing them often helps quickly.
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Do now -->
  <section id="do-now" class="section">
    <h2>What to do right now</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If food feels stuck (but you can breathe)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Stop eating; <strong>sip warm water</strong> or carbonated beverage in small sips</li>
          <li>Walk gently and relax; avoid forceful swallowing or dry bread/meat</li>
          <li>If unable to swallow saliva, chest pain, or breathing trouble → <strong>emergency care</strong></li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <p class="kicker">If mild, new symptoms</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Switch to <strong>soft, moist foods</strong>; small bites; chew well; sip with bites</li>
          <li>Stay <strong>upright</strong> during meals and for 30–60 minutes after</li>
          <li>Try short course of <strong>acid reduction</strong> if heartburn is prominent</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small">This page is educational and not a diagnosis. Seek emergency care for the warning signs above.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>If mild & stable</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Safer swallow strategies</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Eat slowly; <strong>small bites/sips</strong>; avoid talking while chewing</li>
          <li><strong>Chin-tuck</strong> posture when swallowing thin liquids if you cough easily</li>
          <li>Try <strong>thicker liquids</strong> (soups, smoothies) if thin liquids cause coughing—formal thickness guidance from a speech-language pathologist (SLP) is best</li>
          <li><strong>Moisture matters:</strong> sauce, gravy, yogurt, or ghee/oil can help foods slide</li>
          <li>Good mouth care; ensure dentures fit and are worn correctly</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Aspiration prevention:</strong> sit upright at 90°, keep head midline, take one sip/bite at a time, and stop when fatigued. If coughing fits occur repeatedly, pause and retry later.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds-caution" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine notes & cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Pill esophagitis risks:</strong> bisphosphonates, doxycycline, tetracycline, clindamycin, potassium chloride, iron—take with <em>full glass of water</em> and stay upright 30–60 minutes</li>
          <li><strong>Dry mouth culprits:</strong> anticholinergics (oxybutynin), some antidepressants, antihistamines—ask about alternatives</li>
          <li><strong>Sedatives/opioids:</strong> can blunt protective reflexes—use the lowest effective dose</li>
          <li><strong>Acid reducers</strong> (PPIs/H2 blockers) may heal reflux injury; review duration and need</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        Bring a complete <strong>medicine & supplement list</strong> to appointments. <em>Do not</em> crush/alter pills unless a pharmacist confirms it’s safe.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Diet -->
  <section id="diet" class="section">
    <h2>Eating & aspiration tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Helpful choices</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Soft proteins (eggs, yogurt, dal, fish), tender meats with gravy, mashed vegetables, oatmeal, khichdi</li>
          <li>High-calorie <strong>smoothies</strong> with yogurt/milk, banana, nut butter; add milk powder for protein</li>
          <li>Frequent small meals/snacks to maintain weight and energy</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Limit/avoid</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Dry, crumbly foods (crackers, dry bread), mixed textures (thin soups with chunks) if these trigger coughing</li>
          <li>Alcohol before meals (reduces reflexes)</li>
          <li>Eating within 2–3 hours of bedtime if reflux is an issue; elevate head of bed 6–8 inches</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Localize problem & risk</td>
          <td>Onset, solids vs liquids, weight loss, cough/choke, dentition, neuro exam</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Bedside swallow screen</strong></td>
          <td>Check aspiration risk</td>
          <td>Observe water swallow; may trigger SLP referral</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>SLP instrumental studies</strong></td>
          <td>Visualize the swallow</td>
          <td>Videofluoroscopic swallow study (VFSS) or fiberoptic endoscopic evaluation (FEES)</td>

        <tr>
          <td><strong>Endoscopy (EGD)</strong></td>
          <td>Diagnose/ treat esophageal issues</td>
          <td>Dilation for strictures/rings; biopsy for inflammation or malignancy</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Barium esophagram</strong></td>
          <td>Outline anatomy/movement</td>
          <td>Detect narrowing, rings, motility patterns, Zenker diverticulum</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Manometry & pH testing</strong></td>
          <td>Define motility & reflux</td>
          <td>Assess achalasia/spasm; confirm reflux burden</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment plan</strong></td>
          <td>Improve safety & nutrition</td>
          <td>Diet texture plan, posture/technique training, reflux control, dental care, dilation/surgery if indicated</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>

    <div class="grid">

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is trouble with liquids different from solids?</p>
        <p>Yes. Trouble starting swallows or with thin liquids suggests <strong>oropharyngeal</strong> dysphagia; solids getting stuck suggests <strong>esophageal</strong> narrowing.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can reflux cause swallowing problems?</p>
        <p>Yes. Chronic acid can inflame and narrow the esophagus. Acid control and, if needed, dilation can help.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How can I take pills safely?</p>
        <p>Sit upright, take one pill at a time with a full glass of water, and stay upright 30–60 minutes—especially with bisphosphonates, doxycycline, iron, or potassium.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Who helps with swallowing therapy?</p>
        <p>A <strong>speech-language pathologist (SLP)</strong> assesses swallowing and gives individualized posture, exercises, and diet texture guidance.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
