    <h2>What eye redness can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> The cause depends on <strong>pain level</strong>, <strong>discharge/tearing</strong>, <strong>vision changes</strong>, <strong>light sensitivity</strong>, and <strong>contact lens use</strong>. Mild gritty irritation is often dry eye; <strong>severe pain or vision loss is urgent.</strong></p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>One eye vs. both:</strong> single-eye pain/light sensitivity is more concerning than mild redness in both eyes from dryness or allergies.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Sudden vision loss</strong>, double vision, or a dark curtain</li>
        <li><strong>Severe eye pain</strong>, <strong>halos around lights</strong>, hard eye, nausea/vomiting (possible acute glaucoma)</li>
        <li><strong>Eye injury</strong>, chemical splash, or a foreign body you can’t remove</li>
        <li><strong>Light sensitivity</strong> with deep aching pain (possible uveitis/keratitis)</li>
        <li>Contact lens wearer with painful red eye or <strong>pus-like discharge</strong></li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can threaten vision; urgent ophthalmology or emergency care is needed.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes by pattern</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Pattern</th><th scope="col">Clues</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Dry eye (very common)</strong></td>
          <td>Gritty, burning, worse later in day or with screens/wind; watery reflex tears</td>
          <td>Improves with preservative-free artificial tears; warm compress/lid hygiene help</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Allergic conjunctivitis</strong></td>
          <td>Itchy, watery, stringy discharge; both eyes; sneezing/allergies</td>
          <td>Cool compress, antihistamine eye drops; avoid rubbing</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Viral conjunctivitis</strong></td>
          <td>Watery discharge, recent cold; very contagious</td>
          <td>Hand hygiene; avoid sharing towels; usually self-limited</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Bacterial conjunctivitis</strong></td>
          <td>Thick pus-like discharge, lids stuck in morning</td>
          <td>May need antibiotic drops; lens wearers at higher risk</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Blepharitis / Meibomian gland</strong></td>
          <td>Crusty lids, burning, fluctuating vision</td>
          <td>Warm compress + lid scrubs; consider omega-3s if advised</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Corneal abrasion / foreign body</strong></td>
          <td>Severe pain, tearing, light sensitivity, feels like sand</td>
          <td>Needs exam; do not wear contacts; avoid numbing drops at home</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Uveitis (iritis)</strong></td>
          <td>Deep aching pain, light sensitivity, blurred vision</td>
          <td>Urgent ophthalmology; steroid drops only if prescribed</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Acute angle-closure glaucoma</strong></td>
          <td>Severe pain, headache, halos, nausea, hard tender eye</td>
          <td>Emergency; immediate pressure-lowering treatment</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Subconjunctival hemorrhage</strong></td>
          <td>Bright red patch, no pain/vision change</td>
          <td>Usually harmless; check BP/anticoagulant use if frequent</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- First steps -->
  <section id="first-steps" class="section">
    <h2>Safe first steps & home care</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Comfort care</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Use <strong>preservative-free artificial tears</strong> up to 4–6×/day; ointment at bedtime if very dry.</li>
          <li><strong>Warm compress</strong> 5–10 min, then gentle lid massage; follow with lid wipe.</li>
          <li><strong>Cool compress</strong> for allergy itching; avoid rubbing.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <p class="kicker">Hygiene & habits</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Wash hands before touching eyes; change pillowcases/towels during infections.</li>
          <li>Follow the <strong>20-20-20</strong> rule for screens; add a humidifier if air is dry.</li>
          <li>For contact lens users: <strong>stop lenses</strong> until fully cleared; never sleep in lenses unless prescribed.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Call your clinician</strong> if symptoms last &gt;48–72 hours, you have pus-like discharge, light sensitivity, vision change, or you wear contacts.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Medicine cautions -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Use with guidance</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Antibiotic drops</strong> only when bacterial infection suspected or prescribed.</li>
          <li><strong>Antihistamine/mast-cell stabilizer</strong> eye drops help allergies.</li>
          <li><strong>Lubricating gels/ointments</strong> are thicker and last longer (blur briefly).</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Avoid “redness-relief” vasoconstrictor drops</strong> for routine use (rebound redness).</li>
          <li><strong>Never use leftover steroid eye drops</strong> unless an eye specialist prescribes them — can worsen infections and raise eye pressure.</li>
          <li>Oral <strong>antihistamines</strong> can <em>dry eyes more</em>; balance benefits/side-effects with your clinician.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>

        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Identify severity/cause</td>
          <td>Visual acuity, pupils, eyelids/lashes, dye staining for scratches</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Slit-lamp & staining</strong></td>
          <td>Surface damage & infection</td>
          <td>Fluorescein uptake for abrasion/ulcer; lid eversion for foreign body</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Eye pressure (IOP)</strong></td>
          <td>Rule out acute glaucoma</td>
          <td>Tonometer measurement</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Culture / imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Severe or non-healing cases</td>
          <td>Corneal scraping/culture; rarely CT if serious orbital issues</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Cause-directed</td>
          <td>Lubricants, anti-allergy drops, antibiotics, short supervised steroids, glaucoma/uveitis therapy</td>
        </tr>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Which eye(s), onset, exposures (allergens, sick contacts, screens, wind)</li>
          <li>Pain level, light sensitivity, vision change, discharge color/amount</li>
          <li>Contact lens use and solutions; new makeup/creams around eyes</li>
          <li>Response to drops/compresses and timing</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        A 2–3 day diary (with photos if changes are visible) helps clinicians tailor treatment.
      </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is pink eye always contagious?</p>
        <p>No. Viral and bacterial forms are contagious; <em>allergic</em> and many dry-eye cases are not.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">My eye is fire-red but painless — worry?</p>
        <p>If it’s a <strong>sharp red patch</strong> with no pain/vision change, it may be a subconjunctival bleed — often harmless. Check BP and medicines; see a clinician if frequent.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can screens cause red eyes?</p>
        <p>Yes. Less blinking → tear evaporation. Follow 20-20-20, use artificial tears, and add humidity.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When can I wear contacts again?</p>
        <p>When the cause is treated and your clinician okays it. Never wear lenses during infections or corneal problems.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
