  <!-- About -->
  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What poor appetite & weight loss can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Unintentional weight loss in older adults is common and <strong>important</strong> to evaluate. Loss of muscle raises the risk of falls, infections, and slower recovery. Track changes and look for treatable causes.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Concerning trend:</strong> losing <strong>≥5% of body weight in 6–12 months</strong> (e.g., 3 kg from 60 kg) or <strong>clothes/ belts getting looser</strong> without trying.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- ===== In-article AD ===== -->
  

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Severe weakness, <strong>confusion</strong>, dehydration (very little urine, dizziness), or fainting</li>
        <li>Persistent vomiting, blood in vomit, or <strong>black/tarry stools</strong></li>
        <li>Chest pain, shortness of breath, or rapid, unexplained heart rate</li>
        <li>Painful trouble swallowing, food/liquid coming back up, or repeated choking</li>
        <li>Rapid weight loss over weeks with fevers/night sweats or a new hard lump</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can indicate bleeding, infection, heart/lung problems, or blockage requiring urgent evaluation.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes by clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Causes of poor appetite and weight loss">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Clue</th><th scope="col">More likely</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Early fullness, heartburn, belly pain</strong></td>
          <td>Gastritis/ulcer, reflux, delayed stomach emptying</td>
          <td>Check NSAID/alcohol use; consider acid reduction & evaluation</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Change in taste, dry mouth, sore mouth/dentures</strong></td>
          <td>Oral/dental problems, thrush, medicine side effects</td>
          <td>Dental check; mouth care; consider saliva substitutes/rinses</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Trouble or pain with swallowing</strong></td>
          <td>Esophageal stricture, reflux injury, neurologic issues</td>
          <td>Needs swallow/ENT or GI evaluation</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Low mood, loss of interest, poor sleep</strong></td>
          <td>Depression, grief, social isolation</td>
          <td>Screening & supportive therapies help; mirtazapine sometimes used</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Cough, breathlessness, ankle swelling</strong></td>
          <td>Heart failure, COPD</td>
          <td>Fluid/energy imbalance; optimize cardiopulmonary care</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Night sweats, persistent fever, unexplained pain</strong></td>
          <td>Chronic infection, inflammatory disease, malignancy</td>
          <td>Needs timely workup</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Thyroid symptoms (heat intolerance, tremor)</strong></td>
          <td>Overactive thyroid</td>
          <td>Simple blood test (TSH/FT4)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Change in diabetes meds or poor control</strong></td>
          <td>Hyperglycemia or hypoglycemia; SGLT2/GLP-1 effects</td>
          <td>Review regimen; monitor sugars; adjust with clinician</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>New medicines</strong></td>
          <td>Appetite-reducing drugs</td>
          <td>Opioids, digoxin, SSRIs/SNRIs, cholinesterase inhibitors, metformin, GLP-1s, stimulants</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Food access or cooking difficulty</strong></td>
          <td>Practical barriers</td>
          <td>Consider meal delivery, community resources, caregiver help</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Safe steps to try now</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If no red-flags</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Small, frequent meals</strong> every 2–3 hours; don’t wait for hunger</li>
          <li><strong>Protein at each eating</strong> (eggs, yogurt, paneer/tofu, fish, chicken, dal)</li>
          <li><strong>Fortify foods:</strong> add milk powder, nut butters, olive oil, ghee, avocado, cheese</li>
          <li><strong>Drink calories</strong>: smoothies, lassi, milkshakes, oral nutrition shakes between meals</li>
          <li><strong>Light activity</strong> (10–20 min walk) can stimulate appetite; fresh air/sunshine helps</li>
          <li>Set a <strong>meal routine</strong> with pleasant setting; eat with family/friends when possible</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <p class="kicker">Call your clinician soon if</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Weight keeps dropping or appetite is poor for &gt;2–4 weeks</li>
          <li>You have swallowing trouble, persistent nausea/vomiting, or mouth problems</li>
          <li>Depression, grief, or memory changes are affecting eating</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small">This page is educational and not a diagnosis. Seek urgent care for the warning signs above.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds-caution" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine notes & cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Review medicines</strong> that curb appetite or cause nausea (see causes table). Ask about timing changes or alternatives.</li>
          <li><strong>Acid reducers/anti-nausea meds</strong> may help reflux/queasy stomach—short, targeted use.</li>
          <li><strong>Appetite stimulants</strong> (e.g., megestrol, dronabinol) have risks and limited benefit in frail seniors—usually <em>not first-line</em>.</li>
          <li><strong>Mirtazapine</strong> can aid appetite and sleep when depression/insomnia coexist; discuss fit and dosing.</li>
          <li><strong>Do not</strong> start herbal/OTC “weight gain” products without review—interaction and safety concerns.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Blood sugar/diabetes meds:</strong> If using GLP-1 or SGLT2 drugs and losing too much weight, ask about dose/med changes.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Diet -->
  <section id="diet" class="section">
    <h2>Protein & calorie tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Targets (general)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Aim for <strong>1.0–1.2 g protein/kg/day</strong> (e.g., 60–72 g if 60 kg), unless your clinician advises otherwise</li>
          <li>Spread protein across meals; add a <strong>bedtime snack</strong> (yogurt, cheese, nuts)</li>
          <li>Fluids: sip through the day; consider <strong>oral rehydration</strong> during illness/heat</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Make every bite count</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Enrich soups/khichdi with lentils, ghee, cheese, egg, or milk powder</li>
          <li>Easy snacks: peanut/almond butter toast, hummus with crackers, trail mix, boiled eggs</li>
          <li>If taste is bland, use herbs, lemon, and spices; if metallic, try plastic cutlery and colder foods</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

          <td><strong>Imaging/targeted tests</strong></td>
          <td>Assess structural disease</td>

          <td>Identify treatable barriers</td>

        </tr>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Weight</strong> weekly (same scale/time); note % change</li>
          <li>Daily intake: meals/snacks, protein sources, shakes</li>
          <li>Symptoms affecting eating: nausea, pain, taste, mouth sores, constipation/diarrhea</li>
          <li>Mood, sleep, activity, and social meals</li>
          <li>All medicines/supplements and recent changes</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring this log to appointments—it speeds diagnosis and a tailored nutrition plan.
      </div>
    </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How much weight loss is concerning?</p>
        <p>Generally <strong>≥5% of body weight in 6–12 months</strong> or any rapid, unintentional loss—especially with fatigue, pain, or fever.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Are nutrition shakes okay?</p>
        <p>Yes—as <em>add-ons</em> between meals, not meal replacements. Blend with fruit/nut butter or milk powder for extra calories and protein.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Should I try appetite pills?</p>
        <p>Usually not first. Risks can outweigh benefits. Treat causes (mouth pain, reflux, mood, meds) and optimize protein-calorie intake first.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is intermittent fasting good for seniors losing weight?</p>
        <p>No. If you’re unintentionally losing weight or frail, focus on <strong>consistent intake</strong> with protein at each meal and healthy snacks.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
