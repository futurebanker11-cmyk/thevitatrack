  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What leg pain with walking can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Pain, cramping, or heaviness that appears with walking and eases with rest is often called <strong>claudication</strong>. In older adults, the two most common causes are <strong>peripheral artery disease (PAD)</strong>—reduced blood flow to the legs—and <strong>spinal stenosis</strong> causing <strong>nerve compression</strong> (“neurogenic claudication”). Vein problems, arthritis, tendon issues, and sciatica can also contribute.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Describe:</strong> exactly <em>where</em> the pain is (calf, thigh, buttock), <em>how far</em> you can walk before it starts, whether it improves with <strong>standing still vs bending forward/sitting</strong>, and any numbness, color change, or wounds on the feet.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Sudden</strong> severe leg pain, cold/pale foot, numbness/weakness, or loss of pulses (possible acute artery blockage)</li>
        <li>Severe constant foot pain at rest, non-healing wounds, or black/blue toes (critical limb ischemia)</li>
        <li>Leg pain with <strong>chest pain, shortness of breath, or stroke symptoms</strong></li>
        <li>Swollen, red, <strong>tender calf</strong> with warmth (possible blood clot—DVT)</li>
        <li>Fever with rapidly spreading redness down the leg (cellulitis)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can be emergencies: acute limb ischemia, critical PAD, DVT/PE, or severe infection.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Leg pain with walking causes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Cause</th><th>Typical features</th><th>Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Peripheral artery disease (PAD)</strong></td>
          <td>Cramping/heaviness with walking, relieved by <em>standing still</em> within minutes</td>
          <td>Calf most common; cool feet, weak pulses, hair loss on shins, slow-healing sores</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Spinal stenosis (neurogenic claudication)</strong></td>
          <td>Leg pain/tingling with walking/standing, relieved by <em>sitting or bending forward</em></td>
          <td>“Shopping cart sign”: better when leaning on a cart; back pain often present</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Venous disease</strong> (varicose/venous insufficiency)</td>
          <td>Achy/heavy legs worse at day’s end, improved by <em>elevation</em></td>
          <td>Swelling, skin discoloration around ankles; not classically triggered by short walks</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Sciatica / nerve root irritation</strong></td>
          <td>Shooting pain from back/buttock down leg; numbness/tingling</td>
          <td>Worse with certain movements or sitting; may or may not be exertional</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Hip/knee osteoarthritis</strong></td>
          <td>Joint pain/stiffness that eases as you “warm up”; flares after longer activity</td>
          <td>Pain localized to joint line or groin (hip); reduced range of motion</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Muscle/tendon overuse</strong></td>
          <td>Localized soreness with specific activity; tender to touch</td>
          <td>New shoes, hills, or activity increase; improves with rest and stretching</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Medication-related myalgia</strong></td>
          <td>Diffuse muscle aches or cramps</td>
          <td>Recent start/dose change of statins or diuretics (low potassium/magnesium)</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Self-care that’s usually safe</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If no red flags</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Walk regularly</strong> to the edge of discomfort, rest, then repeat (interval walking). Aim most days.</li>
          <li><strong>Elevate legs</strong> for swelling/venous ache; consider gentle calf pumps/ankle circles</li>
          <li><strong>Hydrate</strong> and avoid smoking/vaping; limit alcohol before walks</li>
          <li>Use <strong>warm-up & gentle calf/hip flexor stretches</strong>; start on flat ground</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call your clinician</strong> if walking distance is shrinking, you develop night/rest pain in feet, or any wounds on toes/heels.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Footwear -->
  <section id="footwear" class="section">
    <h2>Footwear & foot-care tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Supportive shoes with <strong>firm heel counter</strong> and cushioned midsole</li>
          <li>Replace worn shoes; consider <strong>shock-absorbing insoles</strong></li>
          <li>Moisturize feet (not between toes), trim nails straight, inspect daily if diabetes/PAD</li>
          <li>Choose <strong>graduated compression socks</strong> (15–20 mmHg) for venous swelling—avoid if significant PAD unless advised</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Walking aids:</strong> A light cane or trekking poles can reduce joint load and improve endurance.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medication notes</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>PAD:</strong> clinicians often prescribe antiplatelet therapy and a <strong>statin</strong>; blood pressure, diabetes, and cholesterol control are key</li>
          <li><strong>Cilostazol</strong> may improve walking distance in PAD—<em>not for heart failure</em></li>
          <li>Check for meds that worsen cramps (diuretics causing low K/Mg) or muscle pain (statins)—don’t stop critical meds without guidance</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Pain relievers:</strong> Acetaminophen is gentler on the stomach. NSAIDs can irritate the stomach/kidneys or interact with blood thinners—ask first.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical evaluation for exertional leg pain">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Differentiate vascular vs neurogenic vs joint</td>
          <td>Pulses, skin/ulcers, neurologic exam, spine/hip/knee assessment</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Ankle–Brachial Index (ABI)</strong></td>
          <td>Screen for PAD</td>
          <td>Blood-pressure cuffs on arm/ankle at rest and sometimes after walking</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Duplex ultrasound</strong></td>
          <td>Locate artery narrowing/vein issues</td>
          <td>Arterial duplex for PAD; venous duplex for reflux or DVT</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Detail anatomy/nerve causes</td>
          <td>CTA/MRA before procedures; lumbar spine MRI for stenosis; joint X-rays for arthritis</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Risk labs</strong></td>
          <td>Cardiometabolic risk</td>
          <td>Lipids, A1C/glucose, kidney function, electrolytes, TSH when indicated</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Improve walking & protect limb/heart</td>
          <td>Supervised exercise therapy (SET), smoking cessation, medications, possible angioplasty/stent or surgery for severe PAD; PT for stenosis/arthritis</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Because PAD is a <strong>heart/brain risk marker</strong>, clinicians may also check for coronary/cerebrovascular risks.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Walking distance</strong> to symptom onset (e.g., blocks or minutes)</li>
          <li>Whether relief comes with <em>standing still</em> or <em>sitting/bending forward</em></li>
          <li>Location/character of pain (calf, thigh, buttock; cramp vs burning vs numb)</li>
          <li>Any <strong>skin color change, temperature change, or wounds</strong> on feet/toes</li>
          <li>Smoking status, blood pressure, glucose logs if applicable</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring this log to visits—helps distinguish PAD from spinal/nerve or joint causes and guides treatment.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with dignity</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Encourage <strong>interval walking</strong> on safe routes; help set small goals</li>
          <li>Support medication adherence and risk-factor control (BP, diabetes, lipids)</li>
          <li>Check feet weekly for sores or color/temperature changes, especially in diabetes/PAD</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> new rest pain, non-healing wounds, sudden cold/pale foot, or rapidly decreasing walking distance.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How do I tell PAD from spinal stenosis?</p>
        <p>Pain from <strong>PAD</strong> eases by <em>standing still</em>. Pain from <strong>stenosis</strong> improves by <em>sitting or bending forward</em> (leaning on a cart). PAD often gives cool feet/weak pulses.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can walking make PAD worse?</p>
        <p>No—<strong>graded walking</strong> is a core treatment and can improve distance over time. Stop only for red-flag symptoms.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What is the ABI test?</p>
        <p>The <strong>ankle–brachial index</strong> compares blood pressure at the ankle vs arm. Low values suggest PAD and guide next steps.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When is a procedure needed?</p>
        <p>Consider angioplasty/stent or surgery for <strong>lifestyle-limiting</strong> symptoms despite therapy, or for <strong>rest pain/non-healing wounds</strong>.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
