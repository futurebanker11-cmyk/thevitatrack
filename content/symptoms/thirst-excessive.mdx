  <!-- About -->
  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What excessive thirst can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Feeling unusually thirsty (<strong>polydipsia</strong>) often signals the body needs fluids or that you’re losing water faster than you drink. Common reasons include <strong>dehydration</strong> (heat, fever, vomiting/diarrhea), <strong>high blood sugar</strong> from diabetes, <strong>dry mouth</strong> from medications, high-salt foods, and less often <strong>diabetes insipidus</strong> (a water-balance disorder), <strong>high calcium</strong>, or <strong>kidney disease</strong>.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Describe:</strong> how long thirst has lasted, urination amount/frequency (especially nighttime), recent illness/heat, new medicines, blood sugar readings, weight change, swelling, and urine color.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Extreme thirst with <strong>frequent urination, nausea/vomiting, abdominal pain, deep/fast breathing, fruity breath, confusion</strong> (possible <strong>DKA/HHS</strong> from very high blood sugar)</li>
        <li>Signs of severe <strong>dehydration</strong>: very low/dark urine, dizziness/fainting, fast heartbeat, low blood pressure, confusion</li>
        <li>Heat exhaustion/heat stroke: hot dry skin or heavy sweating, confusion, headache, cramping</li>
        <li>Thirst with <strong>no urination</strong> in 8–10 hours or painful cramps after diuretics/illness</li>
        <li>Sudden swelling/shortness of breath while drinking large volumes (possible <strong>fluid overload</strong> in heart/kidney disease)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These may indicate a medical emergency and need prompt care.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Excessive thirst causes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Cause</th><th>Typical features</th><th>Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Dehydration</strong> (illness, heat, low intake)</td>
          <td>Dry mouth, dizziness, dark urine</td>
          <td>Recent vomiting/diarrhea/fever; hot weather; limited access to fluids</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>High blood sugar (diabetes)</strong></td>
          <td>Thirst + frequent urination, fatigue, blurry vision</td>
          <td>High home glucose; infections; weight loss if very high sugars</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Medications</strong></td>
          <td>Dry mouth, thirst</td>
          <td>Diuretics, anticholinergics (oxybutynin), some antihistamines, antidepressants, antipsychotics, SGLT2 inhibitors</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>High-salt foods / alcohol / caffeine</strong></td>
          <td>Transient thirst, nighttime urination</td>
          <td>Salty meals, drinks; improves when reduced</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Dry mouth (xerostomia)</strong></td>
          <td>Sticky mouth, trouble swallowing dry foods</td>
          <td>Mouth breathing, Sjögren’s, radiation, dentures, meds above</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Diabetes insipidus (DI)</strong></td>
          <td>Large volumes of very dilute urine + intense thirst</td>
          <td>Brain/pituitary disease (central) or kidney resistance (nephrogenic); abnormal osmolality tests</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Electrolyte/hormone issues</strong></td>
          <td>Thirst, weakness</td>
          <td>High calcium, high sodium; adrenal or thyroid problems</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Psychogenic/polydipsia</strong></td>
          <td>Habit-driven high water intake</td>
          <td>Risk of low sodium (hyponatremia) if excessive water without salts</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Hydration & home steps</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If no red flags and no fluid restriction</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Sip fluids regularly aiming for <strong>pale-yellow urine</strong></li>
          <li>Use <strong>oral rehydration solution (ORS)</strong> during illness/heat; alternate with water</li>
          <li>Limit alcohol and high-caffeine drinks when very thirsty</li>
          <li>Choose foods with water: soups, stews, yogurt, citrus-free herbal teas</li>
          <li>Cool environment, light clothing, rest during hottest hours</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Diabetes:</strong> check glucose more often; if repeatedly &gt;250–300 mg/dL with thirst/urination, contact your clinician. Never ignore nausea, abdominal pain, or rapid breathing.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medication notes</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Review <strong>diuretic timing</strong> (morning/early afternoon) and discuss dose if dizziness or very low urine occurs</li>
          <li>Ask about alternatives if <strong>anticholinergic</strong> medicines cause severe dry mouth</li>
          <li><strong>SGLT2 inhibitors</strong> (e.g., empagliflozin) increase urination; ensure adequate fluids and monitor sugars</li>
          <li>Artificial saliva, sugar-free lozenges/gum can help <strong>xerostomia</strong></li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>If on fluid restriction</strong> for heart/kidney disease: follow your care plan—do <em>not</em> overdrink. Sudden weight gain, swelling, or breathlessness needs prompt advice.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Comfort -->
  <section id="comfort" class="section">
    <h2>Mouth & environment tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Frequent small sips; keep a <strong>water bottle</strong> within reach</li>
          <li><strong>Humidify</strong> bedroom air; avoid mouth-breathing (treat nasal congestion)</li>
          <li>Mouth care: brush/floss; <strong>alcohol-free</strong> mouthwash; denture hygiene</li>
          <li>Limit very salty or sugary snacks that worsen thirst</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Home ORS recipe:</strong> 1 liter clean water + 6 level teaspoons sugar + ½ teaspoon salt. Stir until fully dissolved. (Commercial ORS preferred when available.)
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical evaluation for excessive thirst">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Determine fluid loss vs endocrine/metabolic cause</td>
          <td>Vitals, hydration status, medication review, weight changes</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs</strong></td>
          <td>Check sugar, salts, kidney function</td>
          <td>Glucose/HbA1c, electrolytes, calcium, BUN/creatinine, urinalysis</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Osmolality testing</strong></td>
          <td>Assess DI or water-balance disorders</td>
          <td>Serum/urine osmolality, urine specific gravity; sometimes water-deprivation test</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Targeted studies</strong></td>
          <td>Look for underlying disease</td>
          <td>Thyroid/adrenal tests; pituitary imaging if central DI suspected</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Correct fluids & address cause</td>
          <td>Oral/IV rehydration, diabetes management, adjust meds; desmopressin for central DI; treat calcium disorders</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Plans depend on severity, medical history, and test results.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Daily thirst level (0–10), <strong>urine color</strong>, and frequency/amount of urination</li>
          <li>Fluid intake and weight (watch rapid changes)</li>
          <li>Blood sugars if diabetic; note any sickness, heat exposure, vomiting/diarrhea</li>
          <li>New medicines started and timing vs thirst</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring this log and a complete medication/supplement list—helps target the cause and avoid complications.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with dignity</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Offer fluids at regular intervals; consider flavored water/ORS if plain water is unappealing</li>
          <li>Monitor for confusion, dizziness, very dark urine, or rapid breathing</li>
          <li>Check glucose if the person has diabetes; review meds that cause dry mouth</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> persistent excessive thirst &gt;24–48 hours, very high sugars, signs of dehydration, or swelling/breathlessness in those on fluid limits.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How do I know if I’m dehydrated?</p>
        <p>Thirst, dry mouth, dark urine, dizziness, or fatigue suggest dehydration. Pale-yellow urine usually means you’re well hydrated.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can high blood sugar cause thirst?</p>
        <p>Yes. Excess glucose pulls water into the urine, causing frequent urination and thirst. Check sugars more often and contact your clinician for readings consistently &gt;250–300 mg/dL or if you feel unwell.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What should I drink?</p>
        <p>Water and oral rehydration solution during illness/heat are best. Limit alcohol and high-caffeine drinks until you feel better.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is drinking too much water harmful?</p>
        <p>Yes—rarely, excessive water can lower blood sodium (hyponatremia), causing headache, nausea, or confusion, especially if you’re on fluid limits. Follow your care plan.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
