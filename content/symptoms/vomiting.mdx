  <!-- About -->
  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What vomiting can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Vomiting is the forceful emptying of stomach contents. In older adults, risk of <strong>dehydration</strong> and <strong>electrolyte imbalance</strong> is higher. Causes range from simple infections and medication side effects to bowel obstruction, heart attack, or brain problems (e.g., stroke, high pressure).</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Paired symptoms matter:</strong> fever/diarrhea → infection; severe belly pain/bloating → obstruction; chest pain/sweats → heart; severe headache/neck stiffness → brain/meningitis; spinning sensation → inner ear; black stools → bleeding.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Vomiting with <strong>chest pain/pressure</strong>, sweating, or shortness of breath</li>
        <li><strong>Blood</strong> in vomit or coffee-ground material; <strong>black/tarry stools</strong></li>
        <li>Severe belly pain, <strong>swollen/rigid abdomen</strong>, or no gas/stool passage</li>
        <li><strong>Green (bile) or fecal-smelling</strong> vomit, especially with belly pain/bloating</li>
        <li>Repeated vomiting that prevents fluids for <strong>&gt;8–12 hours</strong> or any signs of dehydration (very little dark urine, dizziness, confusion)</li>
        <li>Severe headache, <strong>neck stiffness</strong>, new weakness/vision/speech changes, or after a <strong>head injury</strong> (especially on blood thinners)</li>
        <li>High fever with shaking chills, or severe vomiting in frail adults with other illnesses</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can indicate bleeding, obstruction, infection, heart/brain emergencies, or dangerous dehydration.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes by clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Vomiting causes and clues">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Clue</th><th scope="col">More likely</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Fever, diarrhea, recent sick contact/food</strong></td>
          <td>Gastroenteritis/food poisoning</td>
          <td>Usually self-limited; watch hydration and urine output</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Severe crampy pain, bloating, no gas/stool</strong></td>
          <td>Bowel obstruction or ileus</td>
          <td>Urgent evaluation; do <em>not</em> take laxatives/antiemetics without advice</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Upper belly pain after fatty meals; fever</strong></td>
          <td>Gallbladder (biliary colic/cholecystitis)</td>
          <td>Right-upper pain, nausea; jaundice = urgent</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Severe persistent upper belly pain to back</strong></td>
          <td>Pancreatitis</td>
          <td>Often with alcohol/gallstones/meds; needs labs and imaging</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Burning pain, black stools or coffee-ground vomit</strong></td>
          <td>Ulcer or GI bleeding</td>
          <td>NSAIDs/blood thinners increase risk; urgent care</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Chest pressure, sweat, breathless</strong></td>
          <td>Heart attack (especially in diabetics/women)</td>
          <td>Treat as cardiac until ruled out</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Spinning sensation (vertigo), ear symptoms</strong></td>
          <td>Inner ear (vestibular neuritis/labyrinthitis)</td>
          <td>Nausea prominent; balance unsafe—support to walk</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Severe headache, morning vomiting, new neuro signs</strong></td>
          <td>Brain pressure, bleed, or meningitis</td>
          <td>Urgent evaluation—especially with neck stiffness, fever, or confusion</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>New medicine or dose change</strong></td>
          <td>Medication side effect</td>
          <td>Opioids, antibiotics, digoxin, GLP-1s, chemo, iron, metformin, theophylline, NSAIDs</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>High sugars, thirst, frequent urination</strong></td>
          <td>DKA/ketosis (diabetes)</td>
          <td>Check glucose/ketones; emergency if high and vomiting</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Alcohol excess or withdrawal</strong></td>
          <td>Irritation/withdrawal</td>
          <td>Hydration; severe withdrawal needs medical care</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Do now -->
  <section id="do-now" class="section">
    <h2>What to do right now</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If no red-flags</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Stop solid food</strong> briefly. Start <strong>small sips</strong> (5–15 ml) every 5–10 minutes of oral rehydration solution (ORS), water, clear broth, or diluted juice. Increase as tolerated.</li>
          <li>Try <strong>ginger</strong> tea/lozenges or peppermint tea; rest in a quiet, cool room.</li>
          <li>If you were prescribed an <strong>anti-nausea medicine</strong> (e.g., ondansetron), take as directed.</li>
          <li>Check <strong>temperature</strong> and note urine output. Aim for light-yellow urine every 3–4 hours.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <p class="kicker">Call your clinician soon if</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Vomiting lasts &gt;24 hours (or &gt;12 hours if frail) or you cannot keep fluids down</li>
          <li>There is weight loss, persistent upper-belly pain, or frequent nighttime symptoms</li>
          <li>You recently started medicines known to cause nausea</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small">This page is educational and not a diagnosis. Seek urgent care for the warning signs above.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>If mild & stable</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Rehydration at home</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>ORS is best.</strong> If unavailable: mix <strong>6 level tsp sugar + ½ tsp salt in 1 liter clean water</strong>. Stir well. (Avoid if you have fluid restrictions—ask first.)</li>
          <li>Take frequent <strong>small sips</strong> or use a spoon. Pause 10–15 minutes if vomiting recurs, then restart slower.</li>
          <li>When better, advance to <strong>simple foods</strong> (banana, rice, applesauce, toast, yogurt, khichdi, oatmeal).</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Do not use</strong> alcohol, very fatty or spicy foods, or large meals during recovery. Avoid <strong>laxatives</strong> and most anti-diarrheal meds unless advised (can worsen some causes).
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds-caution" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Ondansetron</strong>: effective; can cause constipation and rarely affect heart rhythm (QT). Tell your clinician if you have heart disease or are on QT-prolonging drugs.</li>
          <li><strong>Metoclopramide / Prochlorperazine</strong>: may cause drowsiness or movement side effects—use short term and with guidance in seniors.</li>
          <li><strong>NSAIDs</strong> (ibuprofen/naproxen) may worsen gastritis/ulcers—<strong>avoid</strong> if stomach pain/bleeding risk.</li>
          <li><strong>Diabetes meds</strong>: vomiting can lower intake and affect sugars—follow your sick-day plan and monitor glucose more often.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        Keep an updated <strong>medicine list</strong> (include new antibiotics, pain meds, GLP-1 injections) and bring it to visits.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Diet -->
  <section id="diet" class="section">
    <h2>Hydration & food tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Helpful choices</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Clear fluids first (ORS, coconut water, diluted juice, broths), then soft foods as tolerated</li>
          <li>Ginger tea, crackers, toast, plain rice/khichdi, bananas, yogurt/curd</li>
          <li>Small, frequent portions; sit upright; avoid lying flat for 1 hour after eating</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Limit/avoid</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Alcohol, very fatty/fried foods, and strong odors</li>
          <li>Large volumes of plain water at once (can trigger more vomiting)—prefer steady sips</li>
          <li>Milk-heavy foods early in recovery if they worsen nausea</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Step</th><th scope="col">Purpose</th><th scope="col">Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Identify severity & source</td>
          <td>Onset, exposures, belly exam, neuro/heart checks, medicines review</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs</strong></td>
          <td>Check dehydration & causes</td>
          <td>Electrolytes, kidney/liver tests, CBC, glucose/ketones, lipase; troponin/ECG if cardiac symptoms</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Look for obstruction/inflammation</td>
          <td>Abdominal X-ray/CT, ultrasound (gallbladder), head CT if neurologic red flags</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Stabilize & relieve</td>
          <td>IV fluids, antiemetics, acid reduction, treat specific cause (e.g., antibiotics, gallbladder care)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Further GI tests</strong></td>
          <td>Evaluate bleeding or chronic symptoms</td>
          <td>Endoscopy for ulcers/bleeding; H. pylori testing if indicated</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Plans vary by severity, medical history, and exam findings.</p>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Number of vomiting episodes and ability to keep fluids down</li>
          <li>Urine output (frequency/color), weight, and temperature</li>
          <li>Associated symptoms: belly pain pattern, diarrhea, headache, chest pain, vertigo</li>
          <li>All medicines and any recent changes</li>
          <li>Exposure to spoiled food/sick contacts/recent travel</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring this log to appointments—it speeds diagnosis and treatment.
      </div>
    </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When is vomiting an emergency?</p>
        <p>Blood or coffee-ground vomit, black stools, chest pain, severe belly pain/bloating, green/fecal vomit, head/neck symptoms, or inability to keep fluids for &gt;8–12 hours—seek urgent care.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What should I drink first?</p>
        <p>Small, frequent sips of <strong>oral rehydration solution</strong> (ORS) are best. If unavailable, use diluted juice or broth. Avoid large gulps.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can I take anti-nausea pills?</p>
        <p>Yes if previously prescribed (e.g., ondansetron). If new or worsening symptoms, or heart rhythm concerns, check with your clinician first.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What foods are okay when improving?</p>
        <p>Start with bland, low-fat foods: bananas, rice, applesauce, toast, yogurt, oatmeal, khichdi—advance slowly as tolerated.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
