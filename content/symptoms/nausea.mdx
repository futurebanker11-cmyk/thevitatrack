  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What nausea can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> <strong>Nausea</strong> is an unsettled stomach feeling, sometimes with vomiting. In older adults, causes include <strong>infections/foodborne illness</strong>, <strong>stomach irritation</strong> (reflux, gastritis, ulcer), <strong>medications</strong>, <strong>inner-ear/vertigo</strong>, <strong>migraine</strong>, <strong>constipation or blockage</strong>, <strong>UTI or pneumonia</strong>, and even <strong>heart attack</strong>—especially in people with diabetes or women.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Describe:</strong> timing (sudden/gradual), triggers (food, motion, medicines), vomiting color/amount, fever/diarrhea, abdominal or chest pain, dizziness/vertigo, urine color, and recent sick contacts or travel.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Nausea with <strong>chest pain/pressure</strong>, shortness of breath, cold sweat, or fainting</li>
        <li><strong>Vomiting blood</strong> or “coffee-ground” material; <strong>black/tarry</strong> stools</li>
        <li>Severe, <strong>persistent vomiting</strong> (can’t keep fluids for 6–8 hours) or signs of dehydration/confusion</li>
        <li><strong>Green (bile)</strong> or fecal-smelling vomit with belly swelling and no gas/stool (possible blockage)</li>
        <li>Severe headache/neck stiffness, high fever, or new neurological symptoms</li>
        <li>After a <strong>head injury</strong> or with severe abdominal pain/rigid abdomen</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can indicate heart attack, GI bleeding, bowel obstruction, severe infection, or head injury complications.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Nausea causes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Cause</th><th>Typical features</th><th>Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Gastroenteritis / foodborne illness</strong></td>
          <td>Nausea, vomiting, diarrhea, cramps, fever</td>
          <td>Sick contacts or risky foods; lasts 1–3 days</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Gastritis / ulcer / reflux</strong></td>
          <td>Upper belly burning, worse after NSAIDs/alcohol</td>
          <td>Black stools or anemia suggest bleeding—seek care</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Medications</strong></td>
          <td>Starts after a new med/dose</td>
          <td>Common culprits: antibiotics, opioids, metformin, iron, GLP-1s, digoxin, chemo</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Vestibular (inner ear) / migraine</strong></td>
          <td>Spinning dizziness or pounding headache with light/sound sensitivity</td>
          <td>Worse with head movement; relief in dark room</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Gallbladder / pancreas</strong></td>
          <td>RUQ pain after fatty meals or severe upper belly/back pain</td>
          <td>Fever or jaundice (gallbladder); persistent vomiting (pancreas)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Constipation / ileus / obstruction</strong></td>
          <td>Bloating, minimal gas/stool, crampy pain</td>
          <td>Green/feculent vomit or severe distention → urgent</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Infections outside the gut</strong></td>
          <td>UTI or pneumonia can cause nausea in elders</td>
          <td>Burning urination, cough, fever, confusion</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Heart causes</strong></td>
          <td>Nausea, sweat, fatigue ± chest pressure</td>
          <td>Higher suspicion in diabetes/older women—don’t ignore</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Anxiety, pain, heat/dehydration</strong></td>
          <td>Queasy stomach, lightheadedness</td>
          <td>Improves with rest, cool air, and fluids</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Hydration & food that help</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If no red flags</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Small sips</strong> every 5–10 minutes: water, oral rehydration solution, clear broths, or diluted juice</li>
          <li>Advance to <strong>bland foods</strong> as tolerated: crackers, toast, rice, bananas, applesauce, yogurt, soup</li>
          <li><strong>Ginger</strong> tea/chews/capsules and <strong>peppermint</strong> can ease queasiness</li>
          <li>Avoid alcohol, heavy/fatty or spicy foods, and large meals until better</li>
          <li>For diabetes: check glucose more often; seek care if high sugars or ketone symptoms</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>ORS at home:</strong> 1 liter water + 6 level tsp sugar + ½ tsp salt. Stir well. (Commercial ORS is ideal.)
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medication & OTC notes</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Review meds</strong> that often cause nausea (opioids, antibiotics, iron, metformin, GLP-1s). Ask about dose timing with food.</li>
          <li><strong>OTC options:</strong> bismuth subsalicylate (not with blood thinners, kidney disease, or aspirin allergy), meclizine for motion sickness/vertigo (may cause drowsiness), antacids for reflux.</li>
          <li><strong>Prescription antiemetics</strong> (e.g., ondansetron, metoclopramide) may be used when safe for you—ask your clinician.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Avoid NSAIDs</strong> on an empty stomach—they can worsen gastritis/ulcers. Don’t stop critical meds without guidance.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Comfort -->
  <section id="comfort" class="section">
    <h2>Comfort strategies</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Rest with head elevated; breathe cool fresh air</li>
          <li>Try <strong>acupressure P6</strong>: press 3 finger-widths above wrist crease on inner forearm for 2–3 minutes</li>
          <li>Limit strong odors; brush/rinse mouth after vomiting</li>
          <li>If vertigo: keep head still; rise slowly; consider vestibular exercises when better</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call your clinician</strong> if symptoms persist &gt;24–48 hours, you lose weight, have dark urine/low urination, or pain/fever develops.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical evaluation for nausea">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Find cause & severity</td>
          <td>Vitals, hydration, belly and neuro exam; medication review</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs</strong></td>
          <td>Check complications/causes</td>
          <td>Electrolytes, kidney/liver tests, CBC; glucose/ketones; urinalysis</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>ECG / troponin</strong></td>
          <td>Rule out cardiac causes</td>
          <td>Especially if chest symptoms or risk factors</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Look for obstruction/inflammation</td>
          <td>Abdominal X-ray/CT, ultrasound; chest X-ray if pneumonia suspected</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Relieve symptoms & correct fluids</td>
          <td>Oral/IV fluids, antiemetics, acid suppression, antibiotics only when indicated</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Plans depend on severity, age, medical history, and exam findings.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Onset, frequency, and <strong>triggers</strong> (foods, motion, meds)</li>
          <li>Vomiting episodes: time, amount, and appearance (clear, bile, blood)</li>
          <li>Fluids in vs urine out (color), weight changes, fever</li>
          <li>Abdominal pain, diarrhea/constipation, dizziness or chest symptoms</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring this diary and a complete medication/supplement list to visits—it speeds diagnosis and treatment.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with dignity</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Offer frequent small sips; prepare bland, easy-to-digest foods</li>
          <li>Check for new meds and timing with food; help with refills if antiemetics are prescribed</li>
          <li>Watch for dehydration (dark urine, dizziness, confusion) or red-flag symptoms and arrange timely care</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> sustained vomiting, signs of GI bleeding, severe pain/fever, confusion, or chest symptoms with nausea.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What should I drink first?</p>
        <p>Start with small sips of <strong>oral rehydration solution</strong> or clear liquids every 5–10 minutes. Increase as tolerated.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Could nausea be a heart attack?</p>
        <p>Yes—especially with chest pressure, sweat, shortness of breath, or in people with diabetes. Treat as urgent.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Which OTCs help?</p>
        <p>Bismuth can help stomach upset (avoid with blood thinners/kidney disease). <strong>Meclizine</strong> helps motion sickness but may cause drowsiness. Ask what’s safe for you.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Should I stop a medicine that makes me nauseated?</p>
        <p>Don’t stop essential meds on your own. Call your clinician—dose timing, food pairing, or an alternative may help.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
