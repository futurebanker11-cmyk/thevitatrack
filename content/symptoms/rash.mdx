  <!-- About -->
  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What a rash can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> A new or changing rash may be due to <strong>skin irritation or allergy</strong>, <strong>infection</strong> (viral, bacterial, fungal), <strong>immune conditions</strong>, <strong>shingles</strong>, <strong>medicines</strong>, heat, or dryness. Important clues include <strong>itch vs pain</strong>, <strong>symmetry</strong>, <strong>fever</strong>, <strong>blisters</strong>, and whether new drugs or products were started recently.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p>Photos taken daily in the same light help track changes and guide care decisions.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Rash with <strong>trouble breathing</strong>, throat swelling, dizziness or fainting (possible severe allergy)</li>
        <li>Widespread <strong>blistering</strong>, skin peeling, sore mouth/eyes, or high fever</li>
        <li>Rash with <strong>stiff neck</strong>, confusion, or purple spots that don’t blanch</li>
        <li>Painful rash on face/eye or rapidly spreading redness with fever and chills</li>
        <li>New medicine within last 1–3 weeks plus fever and spreading rash</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Rapid evaluation can prevent serious complications.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Type</th><th scope="col">Typical look/feel</th><th scope="col">Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Contact dermatitis</strong> (irritant/allergic)</td>
          <td>Red, itchy patches ± small blisters</td>
          <td>New soap, cream, plants, metals; sharp edges where skin touched item</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Drug rash</strong> (exanthem)</td>
          <td>Widespread pink/red spots</td>
          <td>New medicine in last 1–3 weeks; may have fever/itch; stop only with clinician advice</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Shingles</strong> (herpes zoster)</td>
          <td>Painful band of blisters on one side</td>
          <td>Burning/tingling before rash; urgent eye care if on face/eye region</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Hives</strong> (urticaria)</td>
          <td>Raised, very itchy welts that move</td>
          <td>Come and go within 24 hours at spots; triggers include foods, meds, infection</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Eczema</strong> (atopic)</td>
          <td>Dry, itchy, scaly patches</td>
          <td>Worse with dryness, fragrances; often on flex areas</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Psoriasis</strong></td>
          <td>Thick, red plaques with silvery scale</td>
          <td>Elbows, knees, scalp; may have nail pitting</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Fungal</strong> (ringworm, jock itch, athlete’s foot)</td>
          <td>Ring-shaped/scaly edge, itchy</td>
          <td>Warm, moist areas; improves with antifungal, not with steroid alone</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Scabies</strong></td>
          <td>Intense night itch; small bumps</td>
          <td>Finger webs, wrists, waist; others at home also itchy</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Insect bites/bedbugs</strong></td>
          <td>Grouped itchy bumps</td>
          <td>Linear “breakfast-lunch-dinner” pattern; bedside clues</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stasis dermatitis</strong></td>
          <td>Red-brown itchy shins/ankles</td>
          <td>Leg swelling/varicose veins; skin fragile</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Heat rash / Dry skin</strong></td>
          <td>Tiny bumps or flaky itch</td>
          <td>Hot weather, heavy clothing, frequent hot showers</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- First steps -->
  <section id="first-steps" class="section">
    <h2>First steps at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Spot the trigger</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>List new <strong>medicines</strong>, supplements, creams, soaps, detergents, plants, or foods.</li>
          <li>Note <strong>start date</strong>, body areas, itch vs pain, fever, and contacts with similar rash.</li>
          <li>Take clear photos daily to show changes.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <p class="kicker">Simple relief</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Cool compresses 10–15 minutes; lukewarm (not hot) showers.</li>
          <li>Fragrance-free moisturizer after bathing; cotton clothing; avoid scratching.</li>
          <li>Stop new topical products until reviewed; avoid home antibiotics unless advised.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Call your clinician</strong> for new/worsening rash, suspected drug cause, rash near eyes/genitals, or signs of infection (spreading redness, pus, fever).
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Gentle care -->
  <section id="care" class="section">
    <h2>Gentle skin care</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Use mild, fragrance-free cleanser; limit soap on dry areas.</li>
          <li>Thick emollient twice daily (cream/ointment); after handwashing too.</li>
          <li>Launder with dye/fragrance-free detergent; skip fabric softeners.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Short nails and nighttime cotton gloves can reduce skin damage from scratching.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Step</th><th scope="col">Purpose</th><th scope="col">Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Pattern recognition</td>
          <td>Distribution, symmetry, blisters, scale; medication timeline</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Point-of-care tests</strong></td>
          <td>Confirm infection/type</td>
          <td>KOH scraping for fungus; bacterial swab if drainage; dermoscopy</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs (selective)</strong></td>
          <td>Systemic clues</td>
          <td>CBC if fever, liver/kidney tests with suspected drug reaction</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Patch testing</strong></td>
          <td>Allergic contact</td>
          <td>Metals, fragrances, preservatives, topical antibiotics</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Biopsy / imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Unclear or severe cases</td>
          <td>Rule out autoimmune/bullous disease; eye exam for facial shingles</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment options</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Avoid the trigger</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Stop the offending product; for suspected medicine reactions, <strong>change only with clinician guidance</strong>.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Topicals</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Low–medium potency steroid creams for inflammatory rashes as prescribed.</li>
          <li>Antifungal creams for ringworm/athlete’s foot; avoid steroid-only on fungal rashes.</li>
          <li>Calcineurin inhibitors (selected areas) if steroid-sparing needed.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Systemic/other</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Antihistamines for itch (sedating ones at night if appropriate).</li>
          <li>Antivirals for shingles (best early); antibiotics if bacterial skin infection.</li>
          <li>Short steroid courses only when indicated and supervised.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        Eye involvement, mouth sores, or widespread blistering require urgent specialist care.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- ===== In-article AD (after Treatment) ===== -->
  

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Start date, spread, itch level (0–10), pain, fever.</li>
          <li>New products/medicines; exposures (plants, pets, travel, contacts).</li>
          <li>Response to moisturizers/creams; any side effects.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Keep a 1–2 week log with photos to share at appointments; it speeds diagnosis.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is my rash contagious?</p>
        <p>Eczema, drug rashes, and hives aren’t contagious. Fungal infections, scabies, and some bacterial rashes can be—seek guidance.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How long do hives last?</p>
        <p>Individual welts usually fade within 24 hours but new ones can appear. Persistent hives beyond a few weeks need evaluation.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can I use antibiotic cream?</p>
        <p>Topical antibiotics often cause contact allergy and aren’t needed for most rashes. Ask before using.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When do steroids help?</p>
        <p>They reduce inflammation in eczema/contact rashes when used correctly. Avoid on suspected fungal infections or delicate areas unless advised.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
