  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What black stools can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Black, <strong>tarry</strong>, sticky stools (called <em>melena</em>) often signal bleeding higher up in the digestive tract (esophagus, stomach, duodenum). In older adults, this can be serious—especially with blood thinners or ulcer risk.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Common bleeding causes:</strong> ulcers (often from NSAIDs), gastritis, esophagitis, varices, tumors, or bleeding after certain procedures. <strong>Non-bleeding causes:</strong> iron pills, bismuth (Pepto-Bismol), black/dark foods (blueberries, black licorice), activated charcoal, or supplements.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Black, tarry stools with <strong>dizziness, fainting, or weakness</strong></li>
        <li>Black stools plus <strong>vomiting blood</strong> or coffee-ground vomit</li>
        <li>Abdominal pain with <strong>rapid heartbeat</strong>, pale/clammy skin, or shortness of breath</li>
        <li>Black stools while taking <strong>blood thinners</strong> (e.g., warfarin, apixaban, clopidogrel)</li>
        <li>Known ulcer disease, liver disease/varices, or recent GI procedure with new black stools</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These may indicate significant bleeding and require urgent evaluation.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Look-alikes -->
  <section id="look-alikes" class="section">
    <h2>Harmless look-alikes</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Things that darken stool without bleeding">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Item</th><th scope="col">Why stool looks dark</th><th scope="col">What to do</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Iron supplements</strong></td>
          <td>Unabsorbed iron turns stool dark/green-black</td>
          <td>Usually safe; note color change. If tarry or symptoms appear, seek care.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Bismuth subsalicylate</strong> (e.g., Pepto-Bismol)</td>
          <td>Reacts with sulfur → black stool and tongue</td>
          <td>Benign color change; avoid excess use; watch for red-flags.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Dark foods/dyes</strong> (blueberries, beets + iron, black licorice, squid ink)</td>
          <td>Pigments can darken stool</td>
          <td>Color usually normalizes in 1–2 days after stopping.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Activated charcoal</strong></td>
          <td>Passes through unchanged, making stool black</td>
          <td>Expected effect; avoid unnecessary use.</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">If unsure whether it’s pigment vs. blood, treat as possible bleeding and contact your clinician.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Features: melena vs. dark stool -->

    <h2>How to tell melena from dark stool</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Differences between melena and benign dark stool">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Feature</th><th scope="col">Melena (likely bleeding)</th><th scope="col">Benign dark stool</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Appearance</strong></td>
          <td>Jet black, <strong>tarry/sticky</strong>, shiny; strong odor</td>
          <td>Dark brown/green-black; normal texture/odor</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Symptoms</strong></td>
          <td>Dizziness, fatigue, shortness of breath, chest discomfort</td>
          <td>No systemic symptoms</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Triggers</strong></td>
          <td>NSAIDs, ulcers, liver disease, anticoagulants</td>
          <td>Iron, bismuth, dark foods, charcoal</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stool tests</strong></td>
          <td>Occult blood test often positive</td>
          <td>Occult blood test negative</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Safe steps (if no red-flags)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Short trial at home</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Review any recent <strong>iron/bismuth/dark foods</strong>. Pause non-essential items for 24–48h.</li>
          <li>Hydrate (small, frequent sips). Avoid alcohol and NSAIDs.</li>
          <li>Choose simple foods; avoid very spicy/acidic meals for now.</li>
          <li>Monitor color/consistency of each bowel movement.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <p class="kicker">Call your clinician soon if</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Black stools persist beyond 24–48h after stopping pigments</li>
          <li>You develop fatigue, shortness of breath, or lightheadedness</li>
          <li>You take blood thinners or have a history of ulcers/liver disease</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small">This page is educational and not a diagnosis. Seek urgent care for the red-flags above.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds-caution" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>NSAIDs</strong> (ibuprofen, naproxen) and <strong>steroids</strong> can cause ulcers/bleeding—avoid unless your clinician says otherwise.</li>
          <li><strong>Blood thinners/antiplatelets</strong> (warfarin, apixaban, rivaroxaban, clopidogrel): report any black stools promptly.</li>
          <li><strong>Iron</strong> and <strong>bismuth</strong> can darken stools without bleeding; note timing and dose.</li>
          <li><strong>Acid reducers</strong> (PPIs/H2 blockers) may be advised for ulcer risk—only start/stop with clinician input.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        Keep a complete medication list (including OTCs and supplements) and bring it to appointments.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Diet -->
  <section id="diet" class="section">
    <h2>Food & hydration tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Helpful choices</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Small, frequent meals; avoid late-night heavy meals if reflux</li>
          <li>Gentle options during stomach irritation: bananas, rice, applesauce, toast, yogurt</li>
          <li>Adequate fluids to prevent constipation strain</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Limit/avoid for now</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Alcohol, very spicy/acidic foods, large doses of caffeine</li>
          <li>Foods/supplements with <strong>strong dark pigments</strong> while you’re clarifying the cause</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Step</th><th scope="col">Purpose</th><th scope="col">Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Assess bleeding risk and severity</td>
          <td>Medication review (NSAIDs, anticoagulants), vitals, abdominal and rectal exam</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs</strong></td>
          <td>Check anemia and clotting</td>
          <td>CBC (hemoglobin), iron studies if chronic; INR if on warfarin</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stool testing</strong></td>
          <td>Detect blood</td>
          <td>Fecal occult blood test / FIT</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Imaging/Endoscopy</strong></td>
          <td>Find bleeding source</td>
          <td>Upper endoscopy (EGD); sometimes colonoscopy or CT angiography</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Stop bleeding & prevent recurrence</td>
          <td>IV fluids, PPI therapy, endoscopic hemostasis; adjust/hold blood thinners as advised</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Plan depends on severity, comorbidities, and findings.</p>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When is black stool an emergency?</p>
        <p>When it’s tarry/sticky or you have dizziness, fainting, weakness, chest discomfort, shortness of breath, or you’re on blood thinners—seek urgent care.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can iron or Pepto-Bismol turn stools black?</p>
        <p>Yes. Iron and bismuth often darken stools without bleeding. If the stool is tarry or symptoms appear, treat as possible bleeding and get evaluated.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How long should I watch at home?</p>
        <p>If there are no red-flags and you recently took pigments, observe 24–48h after stopping them. If black stools persist or you feel unwell, call your clinician.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What tests might I need?</p>
        <p>Blood counts, stool occult blood, and often an upper endoscopy to find/treat the source. The plan depends on your risk factors and symptoms.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
