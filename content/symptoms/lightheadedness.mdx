  <!-- About -->
  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What lightheadedness can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Lightheadedness is feeling faint, woozy, or “about to pass out” (not a spinning sensation—that’s <strong>vertigo</strong>). In seniors, common causes include <strong>dehydration</strong>, <strong>orthostatic hypotension</strong> (BP drop when standing), <strong>heart rhythm problems</strong>, low blood sugar, anemia, infections, and <strong>medications</strong>.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Clues:</strong> symptoms when <em>standing up</em> or in heat suggest low BP/dehydration; episodes with <em>palpitations</em> suggest arrhythmia; with <em>black stools</em> or fatigue suggests anemia/bleeding; with <em>anxiety</em> may be hyperventilation.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Chest pain, <strong>shortness of breath</strong>, or a feeling of heart racing/pausing</li>
        <li>New <strong>one-sided weakness</strong>, trouble speaking, facial droop, severe sudden headache (possible stroke)</li>
        <li>Fainting or repeated near-fainting, especially with injury or while sitting/lying down</li>
        <li>Black/tarry stools, vomiting blood, or known <strong>GI bleeding</strong></li>
        <li>Fever with confusion, dehydration (very little urine), or severe vomiting/diarrhea</li>
        <li>Head injury while on blood thinners</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Do not drive yourself. These can indicate heart, brain, bleeding, or severe fluid/electrolyte problems.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Patterns -->
  <section id="patterns" class="section">
    <h2>Common patterns & causes</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Lightheadedness patterns and clues">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Pattern</th><th scope="col">More likely</th><th scope="col">Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>On standing up</strong></td>
          <td>Orthostatic hypotension, dehydration</td>
          <td>Drops in BP; worse in morning/after meals/with heat; on diuretics or tamsulosin</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Sudden, no warning</strong></td>
          <td>Heart rhythm issues</td>
          <td>Palpitations or pauses; may occur at rest or exertion</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>With exertion</strong></td>
          <td>Cardiac/valve disease, anemia</td>
          <td>Breathlessness, chest discomfort, fatigue, pale color</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>After new/changed meds</strong></td>
          <td>Medication side effect</td>
          <td>BP pills, diuretics, nitrates, Parkinson’s drugs, sedatives, anticholinergics</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>With tremor/sweats when hungry</strong></td>
          <td>Low blood sugar</td>
          <td>Diabetes meds (insulin/sulfonylureas); improves with carbohydrate</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Spinning sensation</strong></td>
          <td>Vertigo (inner ear)</td>
          <td>Room spinning, nausea; different pathway than lightheadedness</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <div class="note" style="margin-top:12px">
      <strong>Home orthostatic check (if safe):</strong> after 5 min lying, record BP/HR → stand, recheck at 1 and 3 min. A drop of ≥20 systolic or ≥10 diastolic with symptoms suggests orthostatic hypotension.
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Do now -->
  <section id="do-now" class="section">
    <h2>What to do right now</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If you feel lightheaded</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Sit or lie down immediately</strong>; elevate legs; avoid standing quickly</li>
          <li><strong>Hydrate</strong> with water or an oral rehydration solution; cool off if overheated</li>
          <li>If diabetic and suspect low sugar, take fast carbs (e.g., glucose tabs/juice) and recheck</li>
          <li>Do not drive or climb until symptoms fully resolve</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <p class="kicker">Call your clinician soon if</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Symptoms persist &gt;24–48 hours, recur frequently, or limit activity</li>
          <li>You recently started/changed medicines, or BP at home is low</li>
          <li>You have anemia history, black stools, or unintentional weight loss</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small">This page is educational and not a diagnosis. Seek emergency care for the warning signs above.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>If mild & stable</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Short-term steps</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Rise in stages: sit at bed edge 1–2 minutes before standing; tighten calves/fists</li>
          <li>Small, frequent meals; avoid heavy alcohol; limit very hot showers</li>
          <li>Consider <strong>compression stockings</strong> (knee- or thigh-high) if orthostatic symptoms</li>
          <li>Review home BP and heart rate morning/evening for several days</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Salt/fluid strategies</strong> can help orthostatic hypotension for some, but only if you are <em>not</em> on a fluid/salt restriction (heart/kidney failure). Ask your clinician first.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds-caution" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine notes & cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>BP meds & nitrates:</strong> may drop standing BP—timing/dose adjustments can help</li>
          <li><strong>Diuretics:</strong> can dehydrate and lower sodium/potassium</li>
          <li><strong>Tamsulosin/alpha-blockers:</strong> common cause of first-dose or morning lightheadedness</li>
          <li><strong>Parkinson’s meds, sedatives, anticholinergics, tricyclics:</strong> can worsen symptoms</li>
          <li><strong>Diabetes meds:</strong> insulin/sulfonylureas can cause low sugar—keep a plan for hypoglycemia</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        Bring an updated <strong>medication/supplement list</strong> to visits. <em>Do not</em> stop prescriptions on your own; ask about safer alternatives or dosing/timing changes.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Diet -->
  <section id="diet" class="section">
    <h2>Hydration & diet tips</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Helpful choices</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Water as main drink; consider <strong>ORS</strong> during illness/heat</li>
          <li>Balanced meals with complex carbs + protein to avoid sugar dips</li>
          <li>Fruits/veg with high water content (melon, citrus, cucumber)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Limit/avoid</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Excess alcohol and high-caffeine energy drinks</li>
          <li>Very large, high-carb meals that trigger post-meal drops</li>
          <li>Rapid chugging of plain water in huge amounts (risk of low sodium)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Prevention -->
  <section id="prevention" class="section">
    <h2>Prevention & safety</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Stand up in stages; flex calves before walking</li>
          <li>Hydrate, especially during illness/heat; don’t skip meals</li>
          <li>Ask about <strong>med timing</strong> (e.g., take tamsulosin at night)</li>
          <li>Use grab bars, good lighting, and assistive devices to prevent falls</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Avoid driving or high-risk activities until symptoms are under control and causes are addressed.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Date/time, position (standing/sitting) and activity at onset</li>
          <li>Hydration, meals, illness, heat exposure, alcohol/caffeine</li>
          <li>BP/HR readings (lying → standing) if available</li>
          <li>Palpitations, chest pain, breathlessness, black stools</li>
          <li>All medicines (name/dose/time) and recent changes</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        Bring this log to appointments—it speeds diagnosis and targeted treatment.
      </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is this the same as vertigo?</p>
        <p>No. Lightheadedness feels faint/woozy; vertigo feels like the room is spinning. Causes and treatments differ.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Could my blood pressure be too low?</p>
        <p>Yes—especially when standing, after meals, in heat, or with certain medicines. Home orthostatic checks can help your clinician.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When can I drive?</p>
        <p>When symptoms are fully resolved and the cause is assessed. Avoid driving after a faint/near-faint until cleared.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What quick fix helps?</p>
        <p>Sit or lie down with legs up, sip fluids, and cool off. If diabetic and shaky/sweaty, take fast carbs and recheck glucose.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
