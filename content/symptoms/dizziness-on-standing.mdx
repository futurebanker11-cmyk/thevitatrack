  <!-- About -->
  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What’s happening when you stand</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Standing suddenly can make blood pool in the legs. If the body doesn’t tighten blood vessels or raise heart rate fast enough, <strong>blood pressure drops</strong> and the brain gets less blood for a moment — causing <strong>light-headedness, dim vision, or near-fainting</strong>. This is called <strong>orthostatic hypotension</strong>.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Classic definition:</strong> drop in systolic BP ≥20 mmHg or diastolic ≥10 mmHg within 3 minutes of standing.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- ===== In-article AD ===== -->
  

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Fainting</strong> (loss of consciousness), head injury, or fall on blood thinners</li>
        <li><strong>Chest pain, breathlessness,</strong> new one-sided weakness, facial droop, or trouble speaking</li>
        <li><strong>Black or bloody stools</strong>, vomiting blood, or suspected internal bleeding</li>
        <li>Severe, persistent dizziness with <strong>new confusion</strong> or severe headache</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Possible heart rhythm issue, stroke/TIA, major bleed, pulmonary embolism, or heart attack.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes in later life</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Category</th><th scope="col">Examples</th><th scope="col">Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Low volume</strong></td>
          <td>Dehydration, blood loss, diarrhea/vomiting, over-diuresis</td>
          <td>Thirst, dry mouth, low urine, dark stools or known bleeding</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Medicines</strong></td>
          <td>BP pills (alpha-blockers, nitrates), diuretics, Parkinson’s meds, antidepressants, sedatives, alcohol</td>
          <td>Symptoms worse after dose changes or at night</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Autonomic/nerve</strong></td>
          <td>Diabetes neuropathy, Parkinson’s, amyloidosis</td>
          <td>Constipation, urinary retention, reduced sweating</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Heart rhythm/structural</strong></td>
          <td>Brady/tachyarrhythmias, aortic stenosis, cardiomyopathy</td>
          <td>Palpitations, chest pain, exertional dizziness</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Metabolic/other</strong></td>
          <td>Anemia, thyroid issues, B12 deficiency, infection</td>
          <td>Pale skin, fatigue, fever, weight change, numbness/tingling</td>
        </tr>

  <!-- First steps -->
  <section id="first-steps" class="section">
    <h2>Safe first steps (at home)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Stability first</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Stand up slowly:</strong> sit → dangle feet → <strong>pause 3 breaths</strong> → stand.</li>
          <li>Hold a counter or cane until steady; avoid sudden head turns.</li>
          <li>If dizzy, <strong>sit or lie down;</strong> raise legs on a pillow.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <p class="kicker">Hydration & salt (if allowed)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Small, frequent sips of water; rehydrate after illness/heat.</li>
          <li>Discuss salt targets with your clinician (different for heart/kidney disease).</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Quick counter-maneuvers</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Ankle pumps</strong> and calf squeezes before rising.</li>
          <li>Cross legs and <strong>tense thigh/buttock muscles</strong> for 30 seconds when light-headed.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Call your clinician soon</strong> if dizziness is new, frequent, causes near-falls, or follows a medicine change.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Home BP method -->
  <section id="bp-method" class="section">
    <h2>Home BP & pulse method (lying → standing)</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Step</th><th scope="col">How</th><th scope="col">What it shows</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>1) Rest lying</strong></td>
          <td>Lie flat 5 minutes. Measure <strong>BP & pulse</strong>.</td>
          <td>Baseline</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>2) Stand</strong></td>
          <td>Stand up; measure at <strong>1 minute</strong> and again at <strong>3 minutes</strong>.</td>
          <td>Immediate & sustained change</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Interpret</strong></td>
          <td>Drop ≥20 systolic or ≥10 diastolic within 3 minutes = orthostatic hypotension.</td>
          <td>Share readings with your clinician.</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Use a validated upper-arm cuff, arm supported at heart level, feet flat, no talking.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Prevention -->
  <section id="prevention" class="section">
    <h2>Daily prevention habits</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Rise-slow routine</strong> every morning; avoid jumping up at night.</li>
          <li>Eat smaller meals; very large meals can drop BP.</li>
          <li>Limit alcohol; keep caffeine earlier in the day.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Compression stockings</strong> (waist-high or abdominal binder) if advised.</li>
          <li><strong>Exercise calves</strong> daily: short walks, seated heel raises.</li>
          <li>Review meds regularly; avoid double-dosing BP pills.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Medicines -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine cautions & options</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Medicines that may worsen dizziness</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Alpha-blockers (for BPH), nitrates, high-dose BP meds</li>
          <li>Diuretics (water pills), sedatives/sleep aids, opioids</li>
          <li>Parkinson’s meds, some antidepressants</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Do not stop medicines on your own.</strong> Your clinician may adjust doses or timing (e.g., move doses to bedtime).
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When lifestyle isn’t enough</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Midodrine</strong> or <strong>fludrocortisone</strong> may be used in selected patients.</li>
          <li>Salt tablets or droxidopa in specific cases — specialist guided.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Time of day + what you were doing when dizzy</li>
          <li>BP/pulse lying → 1 min → 3 min standing (numbers + symptoms)</li>
          <li>Fluids, meals, illness (fever/diarrhea), new meds or dose changes</li>
          <li>Any near-falls/falls and circumstances</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is it normal to feel dizzy when I stand?</p>
        <p>Brief light-headedness can occur, but frequent or severe episodes <em>aren’t</em> normal—especially if you nearly fall.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Could my BP pills be the cause?</p>
        <p>Possibly. Several medicines can lower standing BP. Ask about dose timing or alternatives rather than stopping yourself.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do compression stockings really help?</p>
        <p>Yes for many people—waist-high styles or an abdominal binder reduce blood pooling. Fit and comfort matter.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When will this improve?</p>
        <p>Hydration and routines help within days; medication adjustments may take 1–2 weeks to assess. Keep a log and follow up.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
