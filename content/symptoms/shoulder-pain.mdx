  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What shoulder pain can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Pain can come from the <strong>rotator cuff tendons</strong>, <strong>bursa</strong>, <strong>joint arthritis</strong>, a <strong>frozen shoulder</strong> (stiff capsule), or from the <strong>neck</strong> (pinched nerve). Less common but important: <strong>fracture/dislocation</strong> after a fall and <strong>referred pain</strong> from the heart, lungs, or abdomen.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p>Clues: pain with reaching overhead or behind, night pain, weakness when lifting the arm, stiffness (loss of range), or tingling down the arm (often neck-related).</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Shoulder/arm pain with <strong>chest pressure, shortness of breath, sweating, or nausea</strong> (possible heart attack)</li>
        <li>After a fall: <strong>obvious deformity</strong>, severe pain, or inability to move/hold the arm (possible fracture/dislocation)</li>
        <li>Fever, red hot swollen shoulder, or feeling very unwell (possible joint infection)</li>
        <li>New weakness or numbness in the hand/arm after neck injury</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These need urgent care.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Cause</th><th scope="col">Typical features</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Rotator cuff tendinopathy/tear</strong></td>
          <td>Pain with lifting/overhead reach; night pain; weakness (tear)</td>
          <td>Common with age; often supraspinatus tendon</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Subacromial impingement / Bursitis</strong></td>
          <td>Painful arc (60–120°); worse reaching to the side or behind</td>
          <td>Often responds to posture and cuff strengthening</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Adhesive capsulitis (frozen shoulder)</strong></td>
          <td>Stiffness + pain; limited external rotation and reaching</td>
          <td>Stages: freezing → frozen → thawing; common with diabetes, thyroid disease</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Glenohumeral osteoarthritis</strong></td>
          <td>Deep joint pain, creaking, stiffness, loss of rotation</td>
          <td>Gradual onset; X-ray shows joint space loss</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Acromioclavicular (AC) joint arthritis</strong></td>
          <td>Tender top of shoulder; pain with cross-body reach</td>
          <td>Localized to AC joint; lifting/carrying aggravates</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Cervical radiculopathy</strong></td>
          <td>Neck pain; shooting pain/tingling down arm; worse with neck movement</td>
          <td>May have hand numbness/weakness; Spurling’s positive</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Polymyalgia rheumatica</strong></td>
          <td>Morning stiffness/pain in shoulders/hips; age >50</td>
          <td>Often elevated inflammatory markers; treatable</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Calcific tendinitis</strong></td>
          <td>Sudden severe pain; limited motion</td>
          <td>Calcium deposit in tendon; visible on X-ray</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- First steps -->
  <section id="first-steps" class="section">
    <h2>First steps at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Calm the pain</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Relative rest: avoid painful overhead/lifting for a few days; keep the arm gently moving below shoulder height.</li>
          <li>Ice 10–15 minutes 2–3×/day for flares; warm shower before exercises if stiff.</li>
          <li>Medicine safety: many seniors use <strong>acetaminophen</strong> first. <strong>Ask before NSAIDs</strong> if you have kidney, ulcer, heart, or BP issues.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <p class="kicker">Posture & setup</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Support the elbow on a pillow when sitting; avoid prolonged slumped posture.</li>
          <li>Keep frequently used items between waist and chest height.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Call your clinician</strong> if pain lasts &gt;2–3 weeks, limits sleep, or if you notice weakness, catching/locking, or significant stiffness.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Exercises -->
  <section id="exercises" class="section">
    <h2>Gentle exercises</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Phase 1 — Motion (daily)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Pendulums:</strong> Bend forward, relax the arm, gently swing small circles 20–30 seconds.</li>
          <li><strong>Table slides:</strong> Slide forearms forward on a table, stop before pain, hold 3–5 seconds × 10.</li>
          <li><strong>External rotation with stick:</strong> Elbows at side, use a stick/towel to gently turn hands outward × 10.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Phase 2 — Strength (3–4×/week)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Isometrics against a wall (gentle press out/in/forward/back) × 5–10 seconds each.</li>
          <li>Light band rows and external rotation once pain is controlled.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Mild stretch discomfort is OK; sharp pain is a stop sign. Progress gradually.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Step</th><th scope="col">Purpose</th><th scope="col">Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Localize pain; check motion & strength</td>
          <td>Painful arc, empty can, Hawkins/Kennedy, cross-body, neck tests</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>X-ray</strong></td>
          <td>Rule out fracture/arthritis; calcific deposits</td>
          <td>AP, outlet, AC views</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Ultrasound / MRI</strong></td>
          <td>Confirm rotator cuff tear/bursitis</td>
          <td>Useful if persistent weakness or surgery considered</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs (selected)</strong></td>
          <td>Inflammatory or systemic cause</td>
          <td>ESR/CRP for PMR; infection workup if fever/redness</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Injections</strong></td>
          <td>Reduce inflammation/pain to enable therapy</td>
          <td>Subacromial steroid; hydrodilatation in frozen shoulder</td>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment options</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Activity + PT</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Guided home program and physical therapy improve pain and function for most rotator cuff and impingement cases.</li>
          <li>For frozen shoulder: gradual stretching and pain control through phases.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Medicines</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Acetaminophen as first choice for many; topical NSAIDs may help localized pain.</li>
          <li>Oral NSAIDs only if appropriate—discuss kidney, stomach, heart, and BP risks.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Procedures</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Corticosteroid injections for bursitis/impingement (limit frequency).</li>
          <li>Surgery considered for large/full-thickness tears with loss of function or failure of rehab.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        Plans are individualized—consider other conditions (diabetes, heart, kidney) and medicine interactions.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Pain (0–10) morning/evening; sleep disturbance.</li>
          <li>Functional goals: reaching overhead, fastening bra/belt, behind-back reach.</li>
          <li>Triggers (lifting, overhead work) and response to exercises/medicine.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        A 2–3 week log helps fine-tune exercise progressions and timing of therapy or imaging.
      </div>
    </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I need an MRI right away?</p>
        <p>Not usually. Many shoulder problems improve with exercises and time. Imaging is considered if weakness persists, injury was severe, or rehab fails.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What’s the difference between frozen shoulder and rotator cuff?</p>
        <p>Frozen shoulder = <em>stiff</em> shoulder with limited motion in all directions. Rotator cuff issues = pain and weakness, usually with near-normal passive motion.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Should I use a sling?</p>
        <p>Short-term comfort is okay after injury, but prolonged sling use can worsen stiffness. Keep gentle motion going unless told otherwise.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When to see ortho?</p>
        <p>Significant weakness, recurrent dislocations, suspected large tears, or pain/function not improving after several weeks of good rehab.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
