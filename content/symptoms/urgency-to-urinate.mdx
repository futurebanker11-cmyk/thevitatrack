  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What urinary urgency can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Urgency is a sudden, hard-to-delay need to urinate. It commonly occurs with <strong>overactive bladder</strong>, <strong>urinary tract infection</strong>, <strong>prostate enlargement</strong> (men), <strong>pelvic floor weakness</strong>, <strong>vaginal estrogen loss</strong> after menopause, or <strong>medicines</strong> and foods that irritate the bladder.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p>Clues: burning or fever (infection), weak stream/straining (prostate blockage), leakage before reaching the toilet (urge incontinence), nighttime frequency, or triggers like caffeine and artificial sweeteners.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Fever, flank/back pain, feeling very unwell with urgency or burning (possible kidney infection/sepsis)</li>
        <li>Inability to pass urine with painful lower belly swelling (urinary retention)</li>
        <li>Blood in urine with clots, severe abdominal pain, or confusion/dehydration</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Prompt care prevents complications.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Cause</th><th scope="col">Typical features</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Urinary tract infection (UTI)</strong></td>
          <td>Urgency, burning, frequency; ± fever or flank pain</td>
          <td>More common with diabetes, incontinence, catheter use</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Overactive bladder (OAB)</strong></td>
          <td>Urgency ± urge incontinence; frequent small voids; nocturia</td>
          <td>Often without infection; bladder muscle overactivity</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>BPH / bladder outlet obstruction (men)</strong></td>
          <td>Weak stream, hesitancy, straining, incomplete emptying</td>
          <td>Urgency/frequency due to residual urine and irritation</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Genitourinary syndrome of menopause</strong></td>
          <td>Vaginal dryness, irritation, urgency/frequency, recurrent UTIs</td>
          <td>Improves with local vaginal estrogen (if appropriate)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Pelvic floor weakness</strong></td>
          <td>Leakage with cough/sneeze; urgency when near toilet</td>
          <td>Pelvic floor training helps both stress and urge symptoms</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Bladder irritants / medicines</strong></td>
          <td>Worse after caffeine, alcohol, spicy/acidic foods; diuretics</td>
          <td>Also artificial sweeteners; review meds (e.g., SGLT2 inhibitors)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Constipation</strong></td>
          <td>Full rectum presses the bladder → urgency</td>
          <td>Address fiber/fluids, activity, safe laxative plan</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Neurologic disease</strong></td>
          <td>Stroke, Parkinson’s, spinal issues with urgency/incontinence</td>
          <td>Specialist evaluation; tailored bladder plan</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Diabetes / high blood sugar</strong></td>
          <td>Excess urination and thirst; nighttime frequency</td>
          <td>Check glucose/A1C if symptoms suggest</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- First steps -->
  <section id="first-steps" class="section">
    <h2>First steps at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Hydration timing</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Steady sips daytime; reduce fluids 2–3 hours before bed.</li>
          <li>Limit evening caffeine/alcohol; try herbal tea or water.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Bladder irritant check</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Trial cutback of caffeine, citrus/acidic or spicy foods, artificial sweeteners for 1–2 weeks.</li>
          <li>Review diuretics and new medicines with your clinician.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <p class="kicker">Constipation care</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Daily fiber, gentle walking, and a stool plan reduce bladder pressure.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Call your clinician</strong> for burning, fever, blood in urine, new confusion, or if urgency persists &gt;2–3 weeks.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Bladder training & pelvic floor -->
  <section id="bladder-training" class="section">
    <h2>Bladder training & pelvic floor</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Bladder training (2–8 weeks)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Set a schedule (e.g., every 2 hours); go by the clock, not just urge.</li>
          <li>When urgency hits: <strong>stop, sit/stand still, deep breaths, pelvic floor squeeze</strong> for 5–10 seconds, then walk to the toilet.</li>
          <li>Increase interval by 15 minutes every few days as tolerated.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Pelvic floor basics</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Tighten the muscles you’d use to stop gas/urine. Hold 5 seconds, relax 5 seconds, ×10; repeat 3 times/day.</li>
          <li>Avoid straining; exhale during effort. Consider referral to pelvic floor therapy.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Many people see fewer urgent trips and less leakage with consistent training and irritant reduction.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Step</th><th scope="col">Purpose</th><th scope="col">Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Urinalysis ± culture</strong></td>
          <td>Rule out infection or blood</td>
          <td>Nitrites/leukocytes; culture-guided antibiotics if positive</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Post-void residual (PVR)</strong></td>
          <td>Check bladder emptying</td>
          <td>Bladder scan or catheter; high PVR suggests obstruction/weak detrusor</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Bladder diary</strong></td>
          <td>Track patterns and triggers</td>
          <td>Time/volume of voids, leakage, fluids, urgency episodes</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Pelvic/genital exam</strong></td>
          <td>Check atrophy, prolapse, prostate</td>
          <td>Consider vaginal atrophy, pelvic floor tone, prostate size</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs ± glucose</strong></td>
          <td>Look for contributing factors</td>
          <td>Glucose/A1C, kidney function, electrolytes</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Specialist tests (if needed)</strong></td>
          <td>Clarify complex cases</td>
          <td>Urodynamics, cystoscopy, imaging for stones/tumors</td>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment options</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Behavioral</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Bladder training schedule, pelvic floor therapy, evening fluid timing, and irritant reduction.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Medicines (selected)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>For OAB:</strong> β3-agonist (mirabegron, vibegron) or antimuscarinics (oxybutynin, solifenacin, etc.). Review side effects (dry mouth, constipation, confusion risk) and interactions.</li>
          <li><strong>Men with BPH:</strong> α-blocker (tamsulosin) ± 5-ARI (finasteride) if enlarged prostate.</li>
          <li><strong>Post-menopause:</strong> Low-dose <strong>vaginal estrogen</strong> (if appropriate) for urgency/UTI prevention.</li>
          <li><strong>Antibiotics</strong> only when UTI confirmed by testing.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Procedures (for select cases)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Bladder Botox, tibial or sacral nerve modulation if medicines/training fail.</li>
          <li>Prostate procedures for obstruction not responding to medicines.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        Plans are individualized—consider heart, kidney, cognition, constipation, and other medicines.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Bladder diary: time, amount, urgency/leak episodes for 3–7 days.</li>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is urgency always a UTI?</p>
        <p>No. Many cases are overactive bladder or irritants. Testing helps avoid unnecessary antibiotics.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How long does bladder training take?</p>
        <p>Often 2–8 weeks. Small, steady improvements add up; keep a schedule and diary.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Best drink choices?</p>
        <p>Water and non-caffeinated, non-acidic options. Limit evening intake and trial reducing caffeine and artificial sweeteners.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When to see a specialist?</p>
        <p>Persistent symptoms despite training/medicines, blood in urine, recurrent UTIs, retention, or neurologic conditions affecting the bladder.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
