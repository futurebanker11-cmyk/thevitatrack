  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What fainting can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> Fainting (syncope) is a brief loss of consciousness from <strong>reduced blood flow to the brain</strong>. In seniors, causes include heart rhythm problems, low blood pressure when standing, dehydration, medications, and reflex “vasovagal” spells.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Near-fainting</strong> (lightheaded, dim vision, weak) matters too—risk and evaluation are similar. Treat new or unexplained events as potentially serious until checked.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Fainting during <strong>exertion</strong> or while lying down</li>
        <li>Chest pain, shortness of breath, <strong>palpitations</strong>, or severe headache before/after</li>
        <li>New <strong>focal weakness</strong>, trouble speaking, facial droop (possible stroke/TIA)</li>
        <li>Major injury or <strong>head strike</strong> from the fall</li>
        <li>Known heart disease, heart failure, aortic stenosis, or an implanted device with new syncope</li>
        <li>Multiple episodes in 24 hours, prolonged confusion, or not returning to baseline quickly</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Do not drive yourself. Call emergency services for evaluation.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Patterns -->
  <section id="patterns" class="section">
    <h2>Common patterns & causes</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Syncope patterns and clues">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Pattern</th><th scope="col">More likely</th><th scope="col">Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Vasovagal (reflex)</strong></td>
          <td>Common & usually benign</td>
          <td>Trigger: pain, emotion, standing long; warning: nausea, warmth, sweating; quick recovery when lying down</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Orthostatic hypotension</strong></td>
          <td>Drop in BP when standing</td>
          <td>Dehydration, anemia, Parkinson’s, diabetes neuropathy; worsened by diuretics, BP meds, alcohol</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Cardiac arrhythmia</strong></td>
          <td>Fast/slow heart rhythms</td>
          <td>Sudden without warning; palpitations; may occur at rest or exertion; higher risk in heart disease</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Structural heart</strong></td>
          <td>Aortic stenosis, HCM, PE</td>
          <td>With exertion or breathlessness/chest pain; murmur history; leg swelling or clot risks</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Situational</strong></td>
          <td>Reflex with body actions</td>
          <td>Coughing, urinating (nocturnal), swallowing, after meals</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Look-alikes</strong></td>
          <td>Seizure, hypoglycemia</td>
          <td>Tongue bite, prolonged confusion, witnessed shaking; low blood sugar in diabetics</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Do now -->
  <section id="do-now" class="section">
    <h2>What to do right now</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If someone faints</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Lay flat</strong> with legs raised; loosen tight clothing</li>
          <li>Check breathing/pulse; if absent, begin CPR and call emergency</li>
          <li>If awake but dizzy, keep lying/sitting; <strong>do not stand quickly</strong></li>
          <li>Offer small sips of water only after fully alert and not nauseated</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <p class="kicker">If you fainted and are now alert</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Avoid driving, climbing, or operating machinery until evaluated</li>
          <li>Note triggers, position (standing/sitting), warning symptoms, and injuries</li>
          <li>Hydrate; skip alcohol; review your medicine list (see below)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small">This page is educational and not a diagnosis. Seek urgent care for red-flags above.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>If recovered and stable</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Short-term steps</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Hydration & salt</strong> (if not fluid/salt-restricted): water plus salty broth can support BP</li>
          <li>Rise slowly: sit at bedside 1–2 min before standing; clench calves and fists</li>
          <li>Compression: knee- or thigh-high <strong>compression stockings</strong> may help orthostatic symptoms</li>
          <li>Small, frequent meals; limit alcohol and very hot environments</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Call your clinician within 24–72 hours</strong> after any new fainting episode—even if you feel better—especially with heart disease, diabetes, or on multiple BP/diuretic meds.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds-caution" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine notes & cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Blood pressure drugs</strong> (alpha-blockers, nitrates, high-dose diuretics) can lower standing BP</li>
          <li><strong>Heart rhythm drugs</strong> and some <strong>antidepressants/antipsychotics</strong> may slow or destabilize rhythm</li>
          <li><strong>Diabetes medicines</strong> (insulin, sulfonylureas) can cause low sugar → faint-like episodes</li>
          <li><strong>Alcohol, sedatives</strong> increase falls and can worsen low BP</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        Bring an up-to-date <strong>medication list</strong> (including OTCs/herbals). <em>Do not</em> stop prescriptions without guidance—ask about timing/dose changes.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Step</th><th scope="col">Purpose</th><th scope="col">Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Identify cause & risk</td>
          <td>Orthostatic vitals (lying→standing BP/HR), heart & neuro exam, injury check</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>ECG & monitoring</strong></td>
          <td>Detect rhythm problems</td>
          <td>12-lead ECG; ambulatory monitor (Holter/patch/loop) for intermittent arrhythmias</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Labs</strong></td>
          <td>Look for contributors</td>
          <td>Electrolytes, glucose, blood count, troponin if cardiac concern</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Assess heart/brain when indicated</td>
          <td>Echocardiogram for valve/structure; head CT/MRI only if injury/neuro signs</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Provocative testing</strong></td>
          <td>Reproduce/confirm</td>
          <td>Tilt-table test for reflex/orthostatic syncope; carotid sinus massage in selected patients</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Prevent recurrence</td>
          <td>Fluids/salt, med adjustments, compression, pacemaker for certain slow rhythms, ablation/meds for fast rhythms</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Plan depends on severity, comorbidities, and test results.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Prevention -->
  <section id="prevention" class="section">
    <h2>Prevention & safety</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Stand up in stages; flex calves before walking</li>
          <li>Hydrate, especially during illness/heat; avoid skipping meals</li>
          <li>Learn counter-pressure maneuvers: leg crossing, squatting, squeezing a ball when warning symptoms start</li>
          <li>Keep living spaces <strong>fall-safe</strong>: good lighting, clear paths, grab bars</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        After any syncope, avoid <strong>driving</strong> and high-risk activities until a clinician clears you.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Date/time, position (standing/sitting/lying), and activity when it happened</li>
          <li>Warnings: nausea, sweating, dim vision, palpitations, chest pain</li>
          <li>Recovery time and any injuries</li>
          <li>Fluid intake, recent illness, missed meals, alcohol</li>
          <li>All medicines (name/dose/time) and recent changes</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring this log to appointments—it speeds diagnosis and safer prevention.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is fainting always serious?</p>
        <p>No—many episodes are benign (vasovagal). But in older adults, heart and medication causes are common, so new fainting should be evaluated.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can dehydration cause fainting?</p>
        <p>Yes. Illness, heat, and diuretics can lower blood pressure—hydrate and review medicines with your clinician.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What tests are most important?</p>
        <p>Orthostatic vitals and an ECG are basic. Depending on risk, you may need monitoring, an echo, or a tilt-table test.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When can I drive again?</p>
        <p>After clinician assessment. Avoid driving until cleared, especially if the cause is unclear or heart-related.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
