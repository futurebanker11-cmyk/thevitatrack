  <section id="about" class="section">
    <h2>What painful urination can mean</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">Overview</span> <strong>Dysuria</strong> (burning, pain, or discomfort when urinating) is often due to a <strong>urinary tract infection (UTI)</strong>, but can also result from <strong>bladder/urethral irritation</strong>, <strong>stones</strong>, <strong>prostatitis or enlarged prostate</strong> (in men), <strong>atrophic vaginitis/genitourinary syndrome of menopause</strong> (in women), <strong>catheter issues</strong>, or less commonly <strong>sexually transmitted infections</strong> (STIs) or interstitial cystitis.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Describe:</strong> onset, burning vs cramps, frequency/urgency, blood in urine, fever or back/flank pain, discharge, new sexual partner, recent procedures/catheters, and all current medicines.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Painful urination with <strong>fever, shaking chills</strong>, nausea/vomiting, or <strong>flank/back pain</strong> (possible kidney infection)</li>
        <li><strong>Confusion, weakness, or low blood pressure</strong> in an older adult with possible UTI (sepsis risk)</li>
        <li>Inability to pass urine, severe lower-abdomen pain/swelling (urinary retention)</li>
        <li><strong>Visible blood</strong> in urine (especially clots), or new severe pain in someone on blood thinners</li>
        <li>Recent urologic procedure or <strong>catheter not draining</strong> with pain/swelling/fever</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can indicate kidney infection, sepsis, obstruction/retention, bleeding, or catheter-related complications.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & clues</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Dysuria causes">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Cause</th><th>Typical features</th><th>Clues</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Bladder infection (cystitis)</strong></td>
          <td>Burning, urgency, frequent small voids; suprapubic ache</td>
          <td>Usually no vaginal/penile discharge; may see blood-tinged urine</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Kidney infection (pyelonephritis)</strong></td>
          <td>Fever, chills, nausea, flank/back pain</td>
          <td>Often with urinary symptoms; needs prompt medical care</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Prostatitis / prostate enlargement (men)</strong></td>
          <td>Perineal/low back pain, weak stream, frequency, nocturia</td>
          <td>Acute prostatitis may cause fever; chronic forms cause recurrent symptoms</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Atrophic vaginitis / GSM (women)</strong></td>
          <td>Dryness, irritation, painful intercourse, dysuria</td>
          <td>Often after menopause; improves with local estrogen (if appropriate)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stones / obstruction</strong></td>
          <td>Severe colicky flank-to-groin pain, blood in urine</td>
          <td>History of stones; may have nausea/vomiting</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Urethral irritation</strong></td>
          <td>Burning without infection</td>
          <td>Caffeine, alcohol, spicy/citrus foods, perfumed soaps/bubble bath</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>STIs (less common in seniors)</strong></td>
          <td>Dysuria with urethral or vaginal discharge</td>
          <td>New partner or unprotected sex; needs testing/treatment</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Interstitial cystitis / bladder pain syndrome</strong></td>
          <td>Bladder pain/pressure, urgency; worse with certain foods</td>
          <td>Negative cultures; improves after voiding</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Catheter-associated issues</strong></td>
          <td>Burning/spasms, cloudy urine, leakage around catheter</td>
          <td>Check kinks, bag position; infection risk higher</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Self-care -->
  <section id="self-care" class="section">
    <h2>Self-care that’s usually safe</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If no red flags</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Hydrate</strong> steadily (see below); void every 2–4 hours—don’t “hold it”</li>
          <li>Use a <strong>warm compress/heating pad</strong> over lower abdomen for cramps</li>
          <li>Avoid bladder irritants for a few days: caffeine, alcohol, cola, citrus, spicy foods, artificial sweeteners</li>
          <li>Switch to <strong>gentle, fragrance-free</strong> soaps; avoid bubble baths and douches</li>
          <li>After bowel movements: wipe front-to-back; consider a peri-bottle (warm water) for comfort</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call your clinician</strong> if symptoms last &gt;24–48 hours, recur, or include blood in urine, fever, flank pain, or if you have a catheter.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medication & OTC notes</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Phenazopyridine</strong> (OTC urinary analgesic) can reduce burning for <em>up to 2 days</em> while awaiting care. It <strong>turns urine orange</strong> and can stain—avoid with significant kidney disease unless advised.</li>
          <li><strong>Antibiotics</strong> are used only when infection is confirmed/suspected—complete the full course; cultures help target therapy.</li>
          <li><strong>Topical vaginal estrogen</strong> may reduce recurrent irritation/UTIs in post-menopausal women when appropriate.</li>
          <li><strong>Avoid anticholinergic bladder meds</strong> (unless prescribed) if you have trouble emptying—these can worsen retention and confusion in elders.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Cranberry products:</strong> may help some people prevent UTIs, but evidence is mixed and they can interact with <strong>warfarin</strong>. Ask your clinician first.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Hydration -->
  <section id="hydration" class="section">
    <h2>Hydration & bladder-friendly choices</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Aim for <strong>pale-yellow urine</strong> unless on fluid restriction; take small, frequent sips</li>
          <li>Warm water or diluted non-citrus herbal teas can be soothing</li>
          <li>If diabetic, monitor glucose more often during illness</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Pelvic floor relax:</strong> When starting to void, breathe out slowly and drop shoulders/jaw—tension can worsen burning.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>What clinicians may do</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Clinical evaluation for painful urination">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Step</th><th>Purpose</th><th>Examples</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>History & exam</strong></td>
          <td>Differentiate infection vs irritation/obstruction</td>
          <td>Abdomen/flank exam; pelvic exam (women) or prostate exam (men) when indicated</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Urinalysis & culture</strong></td>
          <td>Confirm UTI and guide antibiotics</td>
          <td>Check for white cells, nitrites, blood; send culture before antibiotics if possible</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Post-void residual</strong></td>
          <td>Assess emptying/retention</td>
          <td>Bladder scan ultrasound after voiding</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Imaging</strong></td>
          <td>Look for stones/obstruction</td>
          <td>Renal/bladder ultrasound or CT if severe or recurrent</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Targeted tests</strong></td>
          <td>Other causes</td>
          <td>STI testing when risk; urine cytology for persistent blood; cystoscopy for recurrent/atypical cases</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Treatment</strong></td>
          <td>Relieve symptoms & address cause</td>
          <td>Antibiotics if UTI, alpha-blockers for BPH, topical estrogen for GSM, stone management, catheter troubleshooting</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Plans depend on severity, age, kidney function, past cultures, and medication interactions.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Tracking -->
  <section id="tracking" class="section">
    <h2>What to track at home</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Onset, burning level, frequency/urgency, nighttime voids</li>
          <li>Fever, flank/back pain, <strong>visible blood</strong>, nausea/vomiting</li>
          <li>Fluids in vs urine out (color), any leakage or new incontinence</li>
          <li>Medication changes (including OTCs) and any recent procedures/catheters</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        Bring this log and a full medication/supplement list—helps select the right test and treatment.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with dignity</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Offer fluids regularly and prompt toileting every 2–4 hours</li>
          <li>Watch for <strong>confusion, falls, or decreased intake</strong>—common UTI clues in elders</li>
          <li>If a catheter is present, keep the bag <strong>below bladder level</strong>, tubing unkinked, and use clean technique for emptying</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> fever/flank pain, inability to urinate, visible blood, repeated vomiting, or worsening confusion/weakness.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I always need antibiotics?</p>
        <p>No. Burning from irritation or atrophic vaginitis may not be an infection. A urine test (and sometimes culture) clarifies when antibiotics help.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is blood in urine an emergency?</p>
        <p>Visible blood—especially with clots—should be evaluated promptly. Seek urgent care if it’s heavy or paired with pain, fever, or inability to urinate.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can dehydration cause burning?</p>
        <p>Yes. Concentrated urine irritates the urethra. Gentle, steady fluids often reduce symptoms unless your clinician has restricted fluids.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How long can I use phenazopyridine?</p>
        <p>Up to <strong>2 days</strong> for symptom relief while arranging care. It doesn’t treat infection and can stain urine and fabrics.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
