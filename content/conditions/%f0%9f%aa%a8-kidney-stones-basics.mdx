      <!-- 1 Basics & symptoms -->
      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What are kidney stones? Common symptoms <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>Kidney stones</strong> are hard crystals that form in the kidneys and can move into the ureter (the tube to the bladder). A stone can irritate or block the flow of urine, causing sudden flank or side pain that can spread to the groin.</p>
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Typical symptoms</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Sharp, wave-like pain in the back/side, lower belly, or groin</li>
                <li>Nausea or vomiting during pain episodes</li>
                <li>Blood in urine (pink, red, or tea-colored)</li>
                <li>Urgency or frequent urination if the stone is near the bladder</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Why stones form</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Low fluid intake → concentrated urine</li>
                <li>High salt intake (sodium) increases calcium in urine</li>
                <li>Diet high in oxalate (some nuts, spinach) in certain people</li>
                <li>Uric acid, infections, or rare genetic conditions</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 2 Red flags -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Red flags — when to go to urgent care or ER <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="note warn">
            <ul>
              <li><strong>Fever or chills</strong> with severe flank pain (possible infected blockage — emergency)</li>
              <li>Unable to pass urine, severe unrelenting pain, or repeated vomiting</li>
              <li>Pain with lightheadedness, fainting, or confusion</li>
              <li>Single kidney, known kidney disease, or recent kidney surgery with new severe pain</li>
            </ul>
            <p><strong>Don’t wait</strong> if you have fever with stone-like pain—seek emergency care.</p>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 3 Diagnosis -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> How doctors diagnose a stone <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Common tests</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Urinalysis (blood, infection markers, crystals)</li>
                <li>Blood tests (kidney function, uric acid—when indicated)</li>
                <li>Imaging: ultrasound (no radiation) or low-dose CT for clarity</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>What size means</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>≤ 5 mm</strong> often pass on their own</li>
                <li><strong>5–10 mm</strong> may pass or may need help</li>
                <li><strong>&gt; 10 mm</strong> usually need a procedure</li>
              </ul>
              <p class="muted">Location matters: stones near the bladder may pass more easily.</p>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 4 Pain control & passing -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Pain control, fluids, and helping a stone pass <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Pain control</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Follow your clinician’s plan (some prefer certain pain relievers for stones)</li>
                <li>Heat pad to the flank can ease muscle spasm</li>
                <li>Call if pain remains severe despite medication</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Fluids</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Steady sips through the day (unless you were told to restrict)</li>
                <li>Goal urine: pale yellow—avoid “dark apple juice” color</li>
                <li>If heart/kidney issues: ask for a safe fluid target first</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Medical expulsive therapy</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Some clinicians use a short course of a medicine that relaxes the ureter</li>
                <li>Best for stones in the lower ureter, size-dependent</li>
                <li>Report dizziness, lightheadedness, or side effects promptly</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Strain urine (a simple filter) to catch the stone; bring it for analysis to guide prevention.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 5 Stone types & diet -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Stone types & what to eat (simple rules) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Stone types and food tips">
            <table>
              <thead>
                <tr><th>Stone type</th><th>Limit</th><th>Helpful choices</th></tr>
              </thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr>
                  <td>Calcium oxalate (most common)</td>
                  <td>High-oxalate foods (e.g., spinach, almonds) in large amounts; high sodium</td>
                  <td>Normal <em>dietary</em> calcium with meals (dairy or fortified) binds oxalate; lemon/lime for citrate; drink water</td>
                </tr>
                <tr>
                  <td>Uric acid</td>
                  <td>Purine-heavy foods in excess (some red meats, organ meats)</td>
                  <td>More vegetables/fiber, adequate fluids; some need urine alkalinization (clinician-guided)</td>
                </tr>
                <tr>
                  <td>Struvite (infection stones)</td>
                  <td>—</td>
                  <td>Treat and prevent UTIs; procedures often required to clear stone</td>
                </tr>
                <tr>
                  <td>Cystine (rare, inherited)</td>
                  <td>—</td>
                  <td>High fluid goals; specialized medications—urology guidance</td>
                </tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
          <div class="two" style="margin-top:12px">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Universal prevention</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Fluids:</strong> enough to keep urine pale (ask for your safe target)</li>
                <li><strong>Salt:</strong> reduce sodium—salty packaged foods and restaurant meals add up</li>
                <li><strong>Balanced calcium:</strong> don’t cut all calcium; include normal portions with meals</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Extras that help many</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Lemon or lime in water (citrate can protect against stones)</li>
                <li>Steady activity and regular meals—avoid long fasts with dehydration</li>
                <li>Review supplements (high-dose vitamin C or D may be an issue for some)</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 6 24-hour urine & labs -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> 24-hour urine & lab follow-up (why it matters) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>A <strong>24-hour urine test</strong> measures volume and minerals to tailor prevention. Common targets your team may discuss:</p>
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="24-hour urine focus points">
            <table>
              <thead><tr><th>Focus</th><th>Plain meaning</th></tr></thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr><td>Urine volume</td><td>Higher volume dilutes stone-forming minerals</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Calcium, oxalate, uric acid</td><td>Too much raises stone risk; diet or meds may adjust</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Citrate</td><td>Natural inhibitor—more citrate usually protects</td></tr>
                <tr><td>pH</td><td>Acidic urine favors uric acid stones; alkalinization may help (if advised)</td></tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>

      <!-- 7 Procedures -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> When a procedure is needed <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Procedures are used for larger stones, stuck stones, severe symptoms, or infection behind a blockage. Your urology team chooses based on size, location, and your health.</p>
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Procedure overview">
            <table>
              <thead><tr><th>Option</th><th>What it does</th><th>Notes</th></tr></thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr>
                  <td>ESWL (shock wave)</td>
                  <td>Breaks stones into passable pieces from outside the body</td>
                  <td>Best for certain kidney/upper ureter stones; may need follow-up</td>
                </tr>
                <tr>
                  <td>Ureteroscopy (laser)</td>
                  <td>Scope via bladder to the stone; laser fragments and pieces removed</td>
                  <td>Common; a temporary stent may be placed to aid drainage</td>
                </tr>
                <tr>
                  <td>PCNL</td>
                  <td>Small back incision to access and remove large stones</td>
                  <td>Used for big/complex stones; hospital stay likely</td>
                </tr>
                <tr>
                  <td>Emergency drainage</td>
                  <td>Stent or nephrostomy for infected obstruction</td>
                  <td><strong>Life-saving priority</strong>—clear infection and urine flow first</td>
                </tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 Home care & safety -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Home care & safety checklist <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Do</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Steady fluids unless told otherwise; aim for pale urine</li>
                <li>Use heat for comfort; rest during pain waves</li>
                <li>Take prescribed meds exactly as directed</li>
                <li>Strain urine to capture the stone for analysis</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Call promptly if</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Fever/chills or burning urine with severe pain</li>
                <li>Unable to urinate, or pain stays severe</li>
                <li>Worsening nausea/vomiting or signs of dehydration</li>
              </ul>
            </div>

      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> FAQs & next steps <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">How long does it take to pass a small stone?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Some pass within days; others take a few weeks. Location, size, and your anatomy all matter. Your team guides expectations and follow-up imaging if needed.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Should I stop all calcium?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Not usually. Normal dietary calcium with meals can actually <em>reduce</em> certain stones by binding oxalate in the gut. Your clinician will advise if supplements are appropriate.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Do lemon or citrate drinks help?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">For many people, citrate is protective. Flavoring water with lemon or lime is a simple way to add citrate—useful alongside adequate fluids and salt reduction.</p>
            </div>
