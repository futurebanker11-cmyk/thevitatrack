  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What it is</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> Tinnitus is the perception of sound without an external source—often ringing, buzzing, hissing, clicking, or a heartbeat-like pulse. It can be constant or come and go, and may affect one or both ears.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Good to know:</strong> For many, improving hearing and reducing stress makes tinnitus far less intrusive—even if the sound doesn’t disappear completely.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>Urgent red-flags</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card danger">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Sudden hearing loss</strong> with tinnitus (especially one-sided)</li>
          <li><strong>New one-sided tinnitus</strong> or tinnitus with <strong>asymmetrical hearing loss</strong></li>
          <li><strong>Pulsatile tinnitus</strong> (whooshing in time with heartbeat), especially with headache, vision changes, or neuro symptoms</li>
          <li>Tinnitus after <strong>head injury</strong> or with severe vertigo, facial weakness, or ear drainage</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="small">Seek same-day/urgent care—some causes need rapid treatment to protect hearing or rule out vascular issues.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Ear pain + fever</strong> or swollen, red outer ear needs prompt evaluation for infection.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Cause</th><th scope="col">Clues</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Age-related hearing loss</strong> (presbycusis)</td>
          <td>Gradual hearing difficulty, especially in crowds</td>
          <td>Very common; <strong>hearing aids</strong> often reduce tinnitus intrusiveness</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Noise exposure</strong></td>
          <td>After concerts, machinery, firearms</td>
          <td>Use hearing protection; avoid further loud noise</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Earwax (cerumen) buildup</strong></td>
          <td>Fullness, hearing change, itch</td>
          <td>Safe removal helps; avoid cotton swabs</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Ototoxic medicines</strong></td>
          <td>Starts after a new drug or dose increase</td>
          <td>Examples include high-dose aspirin/NSAIDs, certain antibiotics, chemo, loop diuretics—review with clinician</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Ménière’s disease</strong></td>
          <td>Episodic vertigo, ear fullness, fluctuating hearing</td>
          <td>ENT evaluation; salt/caffeine moderation may help</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>TMJ or neck muscle tension</strong></td>
          <td>Worse with jaw clench or neck strain</td>
          <td>Dental/physio support can reduce symptoms</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Evaluation -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>How it’s evaluated</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>History & ear exam:</strong> noise, infections, wax, jaw/neck issues, medicines</li>
          <li><strong>Hearing test (audiogram):</strong> baseline for both ears</li>
          <li><strong>Asymmetry or one-sided tinnitus:</strong> may need <strong>MRI</strong> of internal auditory canals (rule out rare causes)</li>
          <li><strong>Pulsatile tinnitus:</strong> consider vascular imaging per clinician (CTA/MRA/ultrasound)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Bring a list</strong> of current medicines and supplements—some can aggravate tinnitus; do not stop prescribed drugs without guidance.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Relief -->
  <section id="relief" class="section">
    <h2>Relief strategies that help</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Improve hearing</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Hearing aids</strong> (with or without built-in maskers) reduce effortful listening and often make tinnitus less noticeable</li>
          <li>Treat <strong>earwax</strong> or middle-ear issues when present</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Sound therapy</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Low-level background sound: fan, soft music, nature noise</li>
          <li>Apps or sound machines at night to reduce the contrast of silence</li>
          <li>Avoid excessive volume; goal is gentle blending, not covering</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Cognitive & behavioral</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>CBT-based programs</strong> improve coping and reduce distress</li>
          <li>Relaxation training, mindfulness, paced breathing</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>What usually doesn’t help:</strong> routine antibiotics, steroids (unless sudden hearing loss), or random supplements. Target the cause and the reaction to the sound.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Sleep & stress -->
  <section id="sleep-stress" class="section">
    <h2>Sleep & stress toolkit</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Wind-down routine: dim lights, screens off, light stretch</li>
          <li>Consistent bedtime/wake time; cool, dark bedroom with a soft <strong>sound layer</strong></li>
          <li>Limit late caffeine/nicotine; moderate alcohol (can worsen sleep and tinnitus for some)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Daytime reset:</strong> brief walks, neck/shoulder mobility, jaw relaxation; manage sustained noise exposure with breaks and protection.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds & supplements -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medicines & supplement cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Avoid self-starting</strong> sedatives or high-dose NSAIDs for tinnitus</li>
          <li>Discuss ototoxic risks when starting new medicines (e.g., certain antibiotics, chemo, loop diuretics)</li>
          <li>Supplements such as <em>ginkgo</em>, <em>zinc</em>, or <em>magnesium</em> show <strong>mixed/limited evidence</strong>; interactions are possible</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card small">
        <strong>Goal:</strong> reduce impact and improve quality of life. Combine hearing optimization, sound therapy, and coping skills.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Will tinnitus damage my hearing?</p>
        <p>Tinnitus is a <em>symptom</em>, not a cause of damage. Protect hearing from loud noise and review medicines that may worsen it.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Why is it worse at night?</p>
        <p>Silence makes the sound stand out. A soft, steady background sound can help the brain tune it down.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do hearing aids help?</p>
        <p>Yes for many—amplifying external sound and improving speech clarity often reduces tinnitus intrusiveness.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When is imaging needed?</p>
        <p>One-sided tinnitus or asymmetrical hearing loss, pulsatile tinnitus, or neurologic signs typically prompt further imaging per clinician.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
