      <!-- 1 What it is -->
      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What does heart-related swelling look like? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <p><strong>Leg swelling (edema)</strong> from the heart often shows up as puffiness around the ankles, lower legs, or feet. Pressing a thumb into the skin may leave a temporary dent (<em>pitting</em>). Shoes or socks feel tight by evening.</p>
              <ul>
                <li>Usually affects <strong>both legs</strong> (can be uneven).</li>
                <li>May be worse after long sitting or standing; improves overnight.</li>
                <li>Can come with <strong>weight gain</strong>, shortness of breath, or waking at night gasping.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Why the heart matters:</strong> If the heart pumps weakly or is stiff (heart failure), fluid may build up in the legs and sometimes lungs. Salt intake and certain meds can make swelling worse.
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 2 Red flags -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Red flags: when to call urgently <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Sudden swelling in one leg</strong> with pain/redness (possible clot) — urgent evaluation.</li>
            <li><strong>Chest pain</strong>, fainting, new <strong>severe breathlessness</strong>, or blue lips — emergency.</li>
            <li><strong>Fast weight gain</strong> (for example, ≥2 kg / 4–5 lb in 2–3 days) with worse swelling or breathlessness — call your clinician promptly.</li>
          </ul>
          <p class="note warn"><strong>If in doubt, seek emergency care.</strong> It’s safer to be checked.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

        <div class="content">

      <!-- 4 Daily plan -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Daily plan: small steps that help <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Move & elevate</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Short walks after meals (5–10 min) to engage the calf muscle pump.</li>
                <li>Feet-up breaks during the day (ankles above hips if safe).</li>
                <li>Ankle circles and toe raises while seated; avoid crossing legs long periods.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Salt & fluids</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Taste first, then season; check labels (soups, sauces, deli meats).</li>
                <li>If you have heart failure, follow your clinician’s salt and daily fluid targets.</li>
                <li>Avoid large late-evening drinks if night urination is a problem.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Daily tracking</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Weigh every morning after bathroom; note sudden gains.</li>
                <li>Log swelling (mild/moderate/marked), breathlessness, and diuretic doses.</li>
                <li>Bring your log to appointments; trends guide safe adjustments.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 5 Compression basics -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Compression basics (read before buying) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Check with your clinician</strong> first if you have severe arterial disease, severe heart failure, or significant neuropathy.</li>
            <li><strong>Gentle levels</strong> often used initially (e.g., 15–20 mmHg). Put on in the morning before swelling builds.</li>
            <li><strong>Fit matters:</strong> measure ankle/calf circumference; avoid roll-down bands that constrict.</li>
            <li>Take breaks if numb, painful, or toes discolor.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 6 What your clinician may check -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> What your clinician may check <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Exam:</strong> heart sounds, lung fluid, jugular venous pressure, pitting edema.</li>
            <li><strong>Tests:</strong> blood tests (kidney/liver, electrolytes), BNP/NT-proBNP, ECG, chest X-ray.</li>
            <li><strong>Echocardiogram:</strong> heart pumping strength and valve function.</li>
            <li><strong>Venous ultrasound</strong> if a clot is suspected.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 7 Treatment overview -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Treatment overview (heart-related) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Medicines</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Diuretics</strong> (“water pills”) to remove extra fluid; dosing and timing are individualized.</li>
                <li>Heart failure medicines (for example, ACEi/ARB/ARNI, beta-blocker, mineralocorticoid receptor blocker, SGLT2 inhibitor) when indicated.</li>
                <li>Review other drugs that may worsen swelling (certain calcium channel blockers, NSAIDs, thiazolidinediones).</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Non-medicine steps</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Salt awareness and portion planning; dietitian referral when helpful.</li>
                <li>Compression, walking plan, elevation, and sleep optimization.</li>
                <li>Procedure/surgery only if a specific structural problem is found.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Never change doses on your own. Call your clinician if swelling or weight rises quickly, or if you feel dizzy, very tired, or short of breath.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 Travel & sitting -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Travel & long sitting <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Walk the aisle every hour on flights; pump ankles while seated.</li>
            <li>Avoid tight bands at the knee/thigh; consider gentle compression if approved.</li>
            <li>Keep a small water bottle; avoid heavy salty snacks before/during travel.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 9 Caregiver notes -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Caregiver notes <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Help with a <strong>daily weight log</strong> and medication checklist.</li>
            <li>Prep low-salt meals; keep a list of salty “surprise” foods on the fridge.</li>
            <li>Encourage short, frequent walks and safe elevation after activities.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 10 FAQs + Multiplex -->
      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can swelling be only from salt?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Salt can worsen fluid retention, but persistent swelling deserves an evaluation to rule out heart, kidney, liver, venous, or medication causes.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Are compression socks safe if I have heart failure?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Often yes at gentle levels, but check with your clinician first—especially if you have severe heart failure or arterial disease. Fit and comfort matter.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Which medicines can make swelling worse?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Calcium channel blockers (some types), NSAIDs, and certain diabetes medicines can contribute. Never stop on your own—ask about safe alternatives.</p>
            </div>
