  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What OSA is & why it matters</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> <strong>Obstructive sleep apnea (OSA)</strong> is when the throat repeatedly narrows or collapses during sleep, causing <em>pauses in breathing</em> and drops in oxygen. It fragments sleep and raises risks for daytime sleepiness, high blood pressure, heart rhythm problems, and falls.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Good to know:</strong> Effective treatment—most often <strong>CPAP/APAP</strong>—can improve alertness, blood pressure, and quality of life at any age.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to get urgent help</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Severe sleepiness while <strong>driving</strong> or operating machinery</li>
        <li>Witnessed pauses in breathing with choking and inability to stay awake</li>
        <li>Worsening chest pain, new irregular heartbeat, or fainting</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Do not drive if excessively sleepy. Seek urgent care for concerning heart or breathing symptoms.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>Diagnosis: home vs lab sleep test</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Common pathways</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Home sleep apnea test (HSAT):</strong> Simple sensors at home; best for moderate–severe OSA suspicion without major lung/neuromuscular disease.</li>
          <li><strong>Polysomnography (lab):</strong> Full overnight test; preferred if heart/lung disease, suspected other sleep disorders, or HSAT is inconclusive.</li>
          <li>Results include <em>AHI</em> (apnea–hypopnea index) and oxygen levels to guide treatment.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Tip:</strong> Bring your medication list and bedtime routine notes to the sleep clinic. Ask when and how results will be shared.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment overview</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="OSA treatments">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Approach</th><th>How it helps</th><th>Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>CPAP/APAP</strong></td>
          <td>Keeps airway open with gentle air pressure</td>
          <td>Most effective for OSA; masks and pressures tailored to comfort and fit</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Oral appliance</td>
          <td>Advances the jaw slightly to open airway</td>
          <td>Best for mild–moderate OSA or CPAP-intolerant; fitted by trained dentist</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Positional therapy</td>
          <td>Reduces supine (back) sleeping apneas</td>
          <td>Side-sleep aids, vibration trainers; combine with other therapies</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Weight & lifestyle</td>
          <td>Less tissue crowding, better sleep quality</td>
          <td>Weight loss if appropriate, limit alcohol/sedatives, regular sleep schedule</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Procedures</td>
          <td>Upper-airway surgery; hypoglossal nerve stimulation (selected)</td>
          <td>For carefully chosen patients after evaluation; discuss benefits/risks</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>

  <!-- CPAP How-to -->
  <section id="cpap" class="section">
    <h2>CPAP/APAP setup & comfort</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">First 2–4 weeks</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Mask fit first:</strong> Try nasal cushion vs nasal pillows vs full-face. Aim for minimal leaks and comfort.</li>
          <li><strong>Ramp & pressure:</strong> Use ramp-to-pressure to fall asleep easier; ask about APAP if pressure varies nightly.</li>
          <li><strong>Humidifier:</strong> Start mid-level. Increase if dryness/congestion; add heated tubing to reduce “rainout.”</li>
          <li><strong>Clean basics:</strong> Daily cushion wipe; weekly soap-and-water for mask/tubing; replace filters as recommended.</li>
          <li><strong>Track:</strong> Check nightly hours, mask leak, and residual AHI in the app/reader; share with your clinic.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Troubleshooting:</strong> Mouth dryness → chin strap or full-face mask. Persistent leaks → refit or different size. Claustrophobia → wear mask while reading to acclimate.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Alternatives -->
  <section id="alternatives" class="section">
    <h2>Alternatives & extras</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If CPAP is hard</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Oral appliance:</strong> Custom mandibular advancement device via dental sleep specialist</li>
          <li><strong>Positional therapy:</strong> Side-sleep training devices and pillows</li>
          <li><strong>Nasal care:</strong> Saline rinse, allergy treatment to improve airflow</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Follow-up matters:</strong> Recheck symptoms and data after any change. Some devices need repeat sleep testing to verify benefit.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Safety -->
  <section id="safety" class="section">
    <h2>Driving & surgery safety</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Safety considerations">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Situation</th><th>Why it matters</th><th>What to do</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td>Driving</td>
          <td>Untreated OSA ↑ crash risk from sleepiness</td>
          <td>Do not drive if drowsy. Use treatment nightly; schedule breaks on long trips.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Surgery & anesthesia</td>
          <td>Sedatives/opioids can worsen apnea post-op</td>
          <td>Tell the team you have OSA. Bring CPAP to hospital; ask about monitoring overnight.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>New meds</td>
          <td>Sedatives, alcohol, opioids suppress breathing</td>
          <td>Review doses and timing; avoid mixing with bedtime alcohol.</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Will CPAP help right away?</p>
        <p>Many feel better within days to weeks as sleep consolidates. Give yourself time to adapt and adjust settings with your team.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can I treat OSA without CPAP?</p>
        <p>Some can with oral appliances, positional therapy, weight loss, or selected procedures. Effectiveness varies; monitoring is key.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I need CPAP if my snoring is mild?</p>
        <p>Snoring alone isn’t OSA. Testing clarifies risk. Treat if apneas/oxygen drops are significant or symptoms/conditions are present.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What if my nose is always stuffy?</p>
        <p>Use humidification, saline rinses, and treat allergies or structural issues. A comfortable nose makes CPAP much easier.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
