      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> Overview: what your thyroid does <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Your thyroid is a small gland in your neck that makes hormones (T4 and T3). These hormones set the body’s “speed”—affecting heart rate, temperature, digestion, mood, skin, hair, and energy. Too little hormone is <strong>hypothyroidism</strong> (slowed-down), and too much is <strong>hyperthyroidism</strong> (sped-up).</p>
          <div class="note tip"><strong>Senior focus:</strong> Thyroid problems can look like “just aging.” If you notice new fatigue, feeling cold or hot, bowel changes, or heart rhythm changes, testing can be helpful.</div>

      <!-- 2 Symptoms: Hypo vs Hyper -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Symptoms — Hypo vs Hyper <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Hypothyroidism (low)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Tired, slowed thinking, low mood</li>
                <li>Feeling cold, dry skin, hair thinning</li>
                <li>Constipation, weight creeping up</li>
                <li>Slower heart rate; high cholesterol</li>
                <li>In seniors: can appear as low energy, memory fog, or depression</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Hyperthyroidism (high)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Anxiety, shakiness, heat intolerance</li>
                <li>Unintentional weight loss, more frequent stools</li>
                <li>Fast/irregular heartbeat (palpitations, possible AFib)</li>
                <li>Sweating, trouble sleeping, muscle weakness</li>
                <li>In seniors: sometimes just shortness of breath, fatigue, or heart rhythm changes</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 3 Tests -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Tests: TSH, Free T4/T3, and antibodies <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>TSH</strong> (from the pituitary) tells your thyroid to make hormone. If thyroid is low, TSH rises; if thyroid is high, TSH drops. <strong>Free T4</strong> and sometimes <strong>Free T3</strong> show actual hormone levels. Antibody tests (like <em>TPO</em>, <em>Tg</em>, and <em>TSI/TRAb</em>) help confirm autoimmune causes (Hashimoto’s or Graves’).</p>
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Usual lab patterns (simplified)">
            <table>
              <thead><tr><th>Pattern</th><th>TSH</th><th>Free T4</th><th>Notes</th></tr></thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr><td>Primary hypothyroidism</td><td>High</td><td>Low/low-normal</td><td>Often Hashimoto’s (TPO+)</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Subclinical hypothyroidism</td><td>High</td><td>Normal</td><td>Repeat in weeks; treat based on symptoms/age</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Primary hyperthyroidism</td><td>Low/undetectable</td><td>High</td><td>Often Graves’ (TSI+), toxic nodules</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Subclinical hyperthyroidism</td><td>Low</td><td>Normal</td><td>Higher AFib/fracture risk in older adults</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Medication effect</td><td>Varies</td><td>Varies</td><td>Biotin can distort labs; pause 48–72h (ask clinician)</td></tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Lab ranges differ by lab. Your clinician interprets results with symptoms, age, heart health, and medicines.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 4 Treatment overview -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Treatment overview (hypo & hyper) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Hypothyroidism</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Levothyroxine (T4):</strong> standard therapy. Dose is individualized; start low and go slow in seniors or those with heart disease.</li>
                <li>Recheck labs ~6–8 weeks after a dose change (TSH takes time to settle).</li>
                <li>Target range may be slightly different in older adults to avoid over-treatment.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Hyperthyroidism</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Antithyroid medicines</strong> (e.g., methimazole) to reduce hormone production.</li>
                <li><strong>Beta-blockers</strong> for symptom control (tremor/palpitations), if appropriate.</li>
                <li><strong>Radioactive iodine</strong> or <strong>surgery</strong> in selected cases; long-term follow-up required.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 5 Medication timing & interactions -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Medication timing & interactions (important!) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box">
            <h3>Levothyroxine tips</h3>
            <ul>
              <li>Take on an empty stomach with water, same time daily (morning before breakfast <em>or</em> bedtime 3–4 hours after last meal).</li>
              <li><strong>Separate by 4 hours</strong> from calcium, iron, multivitamins, magnesium, and some heartburn meds—these block absorption.</li>
              <li>Keep brand/generic consistent if possible; if changed, recheck labs.</li>
              <li>Biotin supplements can distort thyroid tests—ask about pausing 48–72 hours before labs.</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Always bring an up-to-date medication list to appointments.</p>

      <!-- 6 Food, iodine & supplements -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Food, iodine, and supplements <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Balanced approach</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Most people get enough iodine from iodized salt and foods; <strong>avoid excess iodine</strong> unless prescribed.</li>
                <li>Selenium from foods (fish, eggs, nuts) is usually sufficient; avoid high-dose supplements unless advised.</li>
                <li>Soy foods and high-fiber meals are fine—just keep levothyroxine separate in time for absorption.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Be cautious with</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>“Thyroid support” supplements—some contain hormone or high iodine.</li>
                <li>Biotin—can skew labs; discuss timing with your clinician.</li>
                <li>Seaweed/kelp tablets—can be very high in iodine.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>

      <!-- 7 Seniors & special cases -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Seniors & special cases <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Start low, go slow:</strong> Thyroid dosing changes gradually to avoid heart strain or bone loss.</li>
            <li><strong>Subclinical hyperthyroidism</strong> in older adults can raise AFib and fracture risk—often treated even if Free T4 is normal.</li>
            <li><strong>Bone & heart:</strong> Over-treatment raises fracture and arrhythmia risk; under-treatment can worsen cholesterol and energy.</li>
            <li><strong>Other illnesses/meds:</strong> Steroids, amiodarone, lithium, and severe illness can alter thyroid tests—interpret with clinical context.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 8 Daily living -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Daily living & follow-up plan <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Routine</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Take levothyroxine the same way daily; set a phone reminder.</li>
                <li>Keep a short symptom log (energy, sleep, bowels, heart rate).</li>
                <li>Recheck labs after dose changes (usually 6–8 weeks).</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Movement & sleep</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Gentle daily walks; light strength and balance work</li>
                <li>Regular bedtime; cool, dark room; morning light exposure</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Appointments</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Bring medication list and supplements to every visit</li>
                <li>Ask about target TSH range for <em>you</em></li>
                <li>Report palpitations, dizziness, or mood changes early</li>
              </ul>
            </div>

      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">What TSH should I aim for?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Targets are individualized. Many adults feel well with TSH in the lab’s normal range, but older adults or those with heart disease may use a slightly different target to avoid over-treatment. Ask your clinician.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can I take levothyroxine with coffee?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Coffee can reduce absorption. Take levothyroxine with water on an empty stomach and wait at least 30–60 minutes before coffee or breakfast, or take it at bedtime 3–4 hours after your last meal.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Do I need iodine supplements?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Most people get enough iodine from everyday foods and iodized salt. Extra iodine can worsen some thyroid problems. Only use if your clinician recommends it.</p>
            </div>
