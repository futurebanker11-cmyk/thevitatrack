      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What is insulin resistance? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Insulin is a hormone that helps move glucose (sugar) from your blood into your cells for energy. With <strong>insulin resistance (IR)</strong>, your cells don’t respond as well, so your body needs <em>more</em> insulin to do the same job. Over time, the pancreas may struggle to keep up, leading to higher blood sugars, <strong>prediabetes</strong>, and sometimes <strong>type 2 diabetes</strong>. IR also travels with other risks—abdominal weight, high triglycerides, low HDL (“good”) cholesterol, and high blood pressure.</p>

          <div class="note tip"><strong>Good news:</strong> Sensitivity can improve. Gentle movement after meals, weight around the waist coming down a little, better sleep, and medication when appropriate all help your body use insulin more effectively.</div>

      <!-- 2 Signs & labs -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Signs and common lab clues <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <ul>
                <li>Weight carried around the waist (larger waist size)</li>
                <li>Higher fasting glucose or A1C in the prediabetes range</li>
                <li>High triglycerides; low HDL (“good” cholesterol)</li>
                <li>Blood pressure on the higher side</li>
                <li>Skin tags or darker velvety skin at the neck/armpits (acanthosis nigricans)</li>
                <li>Family history of type 2 diabetes or gestational diabetes in the past</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Typical lab ranges that hint at insulin resistance">

      <!-- 3 Daily plan -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Daily plan to improve sensitivity <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Move after meals</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>5–10 minute walk</strong> after breakfast, lunch, and dinner helps sugar move into muscles.</li>
                <li>Light chores count: easy gardening, hallway laps, or chair marches.</li>
                <li>Balance & strength a few days a week: sit-to-stand sets, wall push-ups, light hand weights if safe.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Sleep & stress</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Regular bedtime; cool, dim room; short wind-down routine</li>
                <li>5–10 minutes of slow breathing or gentle stretches daily</li>
                <li>Limit late caffeine and alcohol; review snoring or possible sleep apnea with your clinician</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Medication + monitoring</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Take meds exactly as prescribed; bring a list to visits</li>
                <li>Track a few fasting and post-meal readings if advised</li>
                <li>Look for trends, not single numbers—share a short log</li>
              </ul>
            </div>

      <!-- 4 Food pattern -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Food pattern that helps (simple and flexible) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Build your plate</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Half plate veggies/fruit</strong> you digest well (fresh, frozen, or lightly cooked)</li>
                <li><strong>One quarter protein:</strong> fish, poultry, beans/lentils, eggs, or tofu</li>
                <li><strong>One quarter smart carbs:</strong> oats, whole grains, sweet potato, corn, beans, or fruit</li>
                <li>Use olive/canola oil; choose unsalted nuts/seeds</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Smart swaps</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Soda → water or sparkling water with lemon</li>
                <li>White bread → whole grain or smaller portion</li>
                <li>Large dessert nightly → fruit + yogurt a few nights</li>
                <li>Heavy takeout → home meals with herbs, lemon, and less salt</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">If you have kidney, heart failure, or digestive conditions, your portions and choices may differ—ask for a personalized plan.</p>

      <!-- 5 Weight & waist -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Waist & weight: small changes, real impact <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Even a <strong>5–7% weight reduction</strong> (for example, 8–12 lb for someone at 170 lb) can improve insulin sensitivity and lower the risk of type 2 diabetes. Aim for gentle progress, not crash diets. Pair steady meals with light daily movement and better sleep.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Weigh once weekly at the same time (morning after bathroom)</li>
            <li>Use a waist measurement monthly to watch central fat changes</li>
            <li>Choose habits you can keep: shorter walks, smaller plates, more vegetables</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 6 Medicines -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Medications often used for insulin resistance <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Metformin:</strong> lowers liver glucose output and improves sensitivity; may reduce appetite for some. Common side effect: stomach upset—ask about slow-release and take with food.</li>
            <li><strong>GLP-1 receptor agonists:</strong> help fullness, reduce appetite, and improve glucose; some show heart/kidney benefits. Discuss nausea tips and whether this class is right for you.</li>
            <li><strong>SGLT2 inhibitors:</strong> increase sugar loss in urine; have heart/kidney benefits in certain people. Ask about hydration and infection precautions.</li>
            <li><strong>Others:</strong> individualized choices depend on A1C, kidney/liver function, heart history, and cost/access.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note"><strong>Safety first:</strong> Bring an updated med list to visits. Don’t change doses on your own. Report dizziness, dehydration, or new symptoms promptly.</div>
        </div>
      </details>

              </ul>

          <p class="muted">If you’re new to activity or have joint/heart issues, get clearance and start slowly. Use supportive shoes and safe spaces.</p>

      <!-- 8 Special situations -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Special situations (seniors, heart, kidney, fatty liver) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Age 65+:</strong> Avoid dizziness and falls. Rise slowly. If you lose appetite or weight unintentionally, call your clinician.</li>
            <li><strong>Heart disease:</strong> Ask if GLP-1 RA or SGLT2 options fit your history; manage blood pressure and lipids.</li>
            <li><strong>Kidney disease:</strong> Targets and medicines may change; confirm safe pain relievers and hydration goals.</li>
            <li><strong>Fatty liver:</strong> Gentle weight loss and movement can help; limit alcohol and review meds that affect the liver.</li>
          </ul>

        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can insulin resistance improve without weight loss?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Yes. Short walks after meals, better sleep, and medication when appropriate can improve sensitivity even before weight changes. Weight around the waist often changes later.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">What’s the best diet?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">The best plan is the one you can keep. A balanced plate with vegetables/fruit, lean proteins, and smart carbs—plus less salt and sugary drinks—works for many. Personalize for kidney, heart, or digestive issues.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Do I need a CGM?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Not everyone needs one. CGM can help if you’re adjusting medicines, have hypoglycemia risk, or want to see how meals/activities affect you. Discuss cost and coverage with your clinician.</p>
