  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What PAD is & why it matters</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> <strong>Peripheral artery disease</strong> happens when arteries in the legs (sometimes arms) narrow from plaque, limiting blood flow. This can cause leg pain when walking and, in severe cases, <em>non-healing wounds</em> or tissue loss.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Good to know:</strong> PAD is a <em>circulation</em> problem and a warning sign for heart attack and stroke. Treating risk factors helps your legs <em>and</em> your heart.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to get urgent help</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Foot pain at <strong>rest</strong>, especially at night when legs are elevated</li>
        <li>Cold, pale/blue toes; <strong>sudden</strong> severe leg pain, numbness, or weakness</li>
        <li>Non-healing foot ulcers, blackened skin, or spreading infection</li>
        <li>New loss of pulses in foot or rapidly worsening swelling/redness</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can indicate <strong>critical limb ischemia</strong> or acute arterial blockage. Seek emergency care and avoid walking on an infected/ulcerated foot.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Signs -->
  <section id="signs" class="section">
    <h2>Common symptoms</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Leg pain, cramp, or tightness with walking that eases with rest (<strong>claudication</strong>)</li>
          <li>Calves most common; sometimes buttock, hip, or thigh pain</li>
          <li>Slow-healing cuts on feet/toes; shiny skin, hair loss, thick nails</li>
          <li>Cool feet, weak pulses; nighttime foot pain relieved by dangling legs</li>
          <li>Men: erectile dysfunction (pelvic artery involvement)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>Diagnosis & tests</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Common evaluations</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Ankle–Brachial Index (ABI):</strong> Compare ankle and arm blood pressure</li>
          <li><strong>Toe pressures/TcPO₂:</strong> Helpful in diabetes or calcified vessels</li>
          <li><strong>Duplex ultrasound</strong> to map blood flow; <strong>CT/MR angiography</strong> if procedures considered</li>
          <li>Foot exam: pulses, skin temperature, wounds, footwear assessment</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Numbers to know:</strong> Many clinics consider ABI < 0.90 as PAD; < 0.40 often severe. Your clinician interprets results with symptoms.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment overview</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="PAD treatment options">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Approach</th><th>Examples</th><th>Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td>Lifestyle foundation</td>
          <td>Smoking cessation, walking program, foot care</td>
          <td>Cornerstones for all PAD stages</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Medicines</td>
          <td><strong>Antiplatelet</strong> (e.g., aspirin or clopidogrel), <strong>statin</strong>, BP & diabetes meds</td>
          <td>Lower risk of heart attack/stroke and limb events; take as prescribed</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Symptom relief</td>
          <td>Cilostazol (not for heart failure)</td>
          <td>May increase walk distance; discuss pros/cons and contraindications</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Procedures</td>
          <td>Angioplasty/stent, atherectomy, bypass surgery</td>
          <td>Consider if lifestyle-limiting symptoms despite program/meds, or for non-healing ulcers/critical ischemia</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Wound care</td>
          <td>Offloading, specialized dressings, infection treatment</td>
          <td>Early referral to limb-salvage/wound clinic if ulcers present</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>

  <!-- Walking -->
  <section id="walking" class="section">
    <h2>Supervised/home walking program</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How it works (12+ weeks)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Warm up 5 minutes. Walk at a pace that brings on <strong>moderate</strong> calf discomfort in 3–5 minutes.</li>
          <li>Rest until pain resolves, then <strong>walk again</strong>. Repeat cycles for 30–45 minutes total.</li>
          <li>Goal: 3–5 sessions/week. Gradually increase walking time and reduce rest breaks.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Tip:</strong> Slight discomfort is expected and safe in PAD training. <em>Stop</em> for chest pain, severe breathlessness, or foot wounds.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Foot care -->
  <section id="footcare" class="section">
    <h2>Foot-care checklist</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Daily habits</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Check tops/soles of feet and between toes for blisters, cracks, or color change</li>
          <li>Keep feet clean and dry; moisturize <em>not</em> between toes</li>
          <li>Wear cushioned socks; well-fitting shoes with a wide toe box</li>
          <li>Never walk barefoot; break in new shoes slowly</li>
          <li>Toenails straight across; see podiatry for thick nails or calluses</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>When to call:</strong> Any foot wound that doesn’t improve after 2–3 days, signs of infection (redness, warmth, drainage), or sudden color/temperature change.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Will walking make PAD worse?</p>
        <p>No. A structured walk–rest program is a proven way to <strong>improve</strong> symptoms and walking distance. Expect manageable calf discomfort that eases with rest.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I need a stent?</p>
        <p>Not always. Many people improve with medicines, smoking cessation, and supervised exercise. Procedures are for persistent lifestyle-limiting symptoms or limb-threatening issues.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Which medicines are typical?</p>
        <p>Usually an <strong>antiplatelet</strong> (like aspirin or clopidogrel) and a <strong>statin</strong>, plus BP/diabetes control. Your clinician chooses based on your history.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What’s the biggest risk I can control?</p>
        <p><strong>Stopping smoking.</strong> Combine counseling with approved medications for best success.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
