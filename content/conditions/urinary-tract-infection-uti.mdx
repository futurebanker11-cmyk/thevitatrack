  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What is a UTI?</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> A urinary tract infection is bacterial growth in the urinary system—bladder (<strong>cystitis</strong>) or kidneys (<strong>pyelonephritis</strong>). Older adults can have <strong>typical urinary symptoms</strong> or sometimes subtle changes like confusion or weakness—especially with dehydration.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Important:</strong> Bacteria in the urine <em>without symptoms</em> (asymptomatic bacteriuria) usually <strong>does not need antibiotics</strong>, except in specific situations (e.g., before certain urologic procedures). Treat the person, not the urine alone.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to seek urgent care</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card danger">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Fever</strong>, shaking chills, or vomiting</li>
          <li><strong>Back/side (flank) pain</strong> with fever (possible kidney infection)</li>
          <li><strong>Confusion, new agitation, or severe weakness</strong> with urinary symptoms</li>
          <li><strong>Low blood pressure</strong>, fast heartbeat, or feeling faint</li>
          <li>Continuous symptoms despite antibiotics, or inability to keep fluids down</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="small">These may indicate severe infection or dehydration—seek emergency evaluation.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>High-risk groups:</strong> people with kidney stones, urinary blockages, catheters, recent urinary procedures, or immunosuppression.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Symptoms -->
  <section id="symptoms" class="section">
    <h2>Typical symptoms</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Burning or pain during urination</li>
          <li>Urgency, frequent trips, waking at night to urinate</li>
          <li>Lower belly pressure or discomfort</li>
          <li>Cloudy, strong-smelling, or bloody urine</li>
          <li>Incontinence worsening from baseline</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card small">
        <p class="kicker">Kidney infection (pyelonephritis) clues</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Fever, chills</li>
          <li>Back/side (flank) pain</li>
          <li>Nausea/vomiting</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Causes & risks -->
  <section id="causes-risks" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & risks</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Factor</th><th scope="col">Why it matters</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Dehydration</strong></td>
          <td>Concentrated urine favors bacterial growth</td>
          <td>Regular fluids unless restricted; monitor color (pale yellow goal)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Post-menopause</strong></td>
          <td>Lower estrogen alters vaginal/urinary microbiome</td>
          <td><strong>Topical vaginal estrogen</strong> can reduce recurrent UTIs (if appropriate)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Urinary retention / prostate enlargement</strong></td>
          <td>Incomplete emptying leaves residual urine for bacteria</td>
          <td>Check post-void residual; treat BPH/obstruction</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Catheters</strong></td>
          <td>Foreign surface for biofilm; higher infection risk</td>
          <td>Use only when necessary; maintain closed drainage; hygiene</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Diabetes</strong></td>
          <td>Higher glucose can support bacterial growth</td>
          <td>Glucose control helps prevention</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Kidney stones or structural issues</strong></td>
          <td>Blockage and stasis</td>
          <td>May need imaging/urology input</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Diagnosis -->
  <section id="diagnosis" class="section">
    <h2>How UTIs are diagnosed</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Symptoms + urine tests</strong> (dipstick and urinalysis) guide initial treatment</li>
          <li><strong>Urine culture</strong> confirms the organism and helps pick the antibiotic</li>
          <li>For recurrent or complicated cases, clinicians may check <strong>post-void residual</strong>, kidney/bladder <strong>ultrasound</strong>, or refer to urology</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Asymptomatic bacteriuria:</strong> positive urine tests without symptoms usually <em>don’t</em> need antibiotics (exceptions exist). Treating unnecessarily can cause side effects and resistance.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment & symptom relief</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Antibiotics</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Choice depends on <strong>local resistance</strong>, allergies, kidney function, and culture results</li>
          <li>Take exactly as prescribed and <strong>finish the course</strong> unless advised otherwise</li>
          <li>Report side effects (rash, severe diarrhea, tendon pain) promptly</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Hydration & pain relief</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Drink fluids (unless restricted); avoid dehydration</li>
          <li>Short-term urinary analgesics may be used if clinician approves</li>
          <li>Avoid bladder irritants (excess caffeine, alcohol, spicy foods) during recovery</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card small">
        <p class="kicker">Kidney infection</p>
        <p>Often needs longer antibiotics; sometimes IV therapy or brief hospitalization—follow clinician advice closely.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Follow-up:</strong> If symptoms persist after 48–72 hours of treatment, contact your clinician—culture results may suggest a different antibiotic.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Prevention -->
  <section id="prevention" class="section">
    <h2>Prevention & daily habits</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Fluids:</strong> steady water intake unless on fluid restriction</li>
          <li><strong>Timed voiding:</strong> don’t hold urine for long periods; empty before sleep</li>
          <li><strong>Wipe front to back</strong>; gentle hygiene without harsh soaps</li>
          <li><strong>Urinate after sexual activity</strong></li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Vaginal estrogen (topical)</strong> can reduce recurrent UTIs in postmenopausal women (if appropriate)</li>
          <li>Discuss <strong>cranberry, D-mannose, probiotics</strong>—evidence varies; may help some</li>
          <li>Review medicines that may cause dry mouth/dehydration or retention</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Recurrent UTI -->
  <section id="recurrent" class="section">
    <h2>Recurrent UTI plan</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When is it “recurrent”?</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>≥2 infections in 6 months or ≥3 in 1 year</li>
          <li>Keep a record of symptoms, cultures, and antibiotics used</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Options your clinician may consider</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Targeted prevention: <strong>vaginal estrogen</strong>, address retention/BPH, treat constipation</li>
          <li>Self-start or post-exposure antibiotics in select cases</li>
          <li>Low-dose nightly prophylaxis for limited periods when benefits outweigh risks</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Catheter care -->
  <section id="catheter" class="section">
    <h2>Catheter-related care</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Use catheters only when necessary; consider intermittent catheterization if appropriate</li>
          <li>Maintain a <strong>closed drainage</strong> system; keep the bag below bladder level</li>
          <li>Daily gentle cleaning around the catheter; hand hygiene before/after handling</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Do not treat</strong> cloudy or odorous urine alone in catheter users—use <em>symptoms</em> plus tests to guide treatment.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Does confusion always mean UTI in seniors?</p>
        <p>No. Confusion has many causes (dehydration, medicines, stroke, infection elsewhere). If urinary symptoms are absent, clinicians often look broadly before treating.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can I prevent UTIs with cranberry or D-mannose?</p>
        <p>Evidence is mixed; some benefit for selected people. They can interact with medicines—discuss before starting.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When should I recheck?</p>
        <p>If symptoms persist after 48–72 hours on antibiotics, or sooner if you feel worse. Recurrent UTIs warrant a prevention plan.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Should I keep testing urine after I feel better?</p>
        <p>Routine “test of cure” isn’t usually needed for simple bladder infections unless symptoms return or you’re high-risk.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
