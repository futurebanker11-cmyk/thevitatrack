        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">!</span> Urgent signs — act now <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>Call emergency services immediately</strong> for chest pain/pressure, shortness of breath, jaw/arm pain, fainting, or if heartburn feels different or worse than usual. Don’t assume it’s “just reflux.”</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Vomiting blood or coffee-ground material; black or tarry stools</li>
            <li>Trouble or pain with swallowing; food getting stuck</li>
            <li>Unintentional weight loss, persistent vomiting, anemia, severe belly pain</li>
            <li>New hoarseness, chronic cough, or nighttime choking with weight loss</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 2 What GERD is -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What GERD is & common symptoms <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <p><strong>GERD</strong> (gastroesophageal reflux disease) happens when stomach contents move up into the esophagus (the swallowing tube). It can cause heartburn (burning in the chest), sour taste, regurgitation, throat clearing, cough, hoarseness, or trouble swallowing.</p>
              <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="GERD vs non-GERD causes (examples)">
                <table class="responsive">
                  <thead><tr><th>Common in GERD</th><th>Need a different workup</th></tr></thead>
                  <tbody>
                    <tr><td data-col="Common in GERD">Heartburn after meals, bending, or lying flat</td><td data-col="Need a different workup">Crushing chest pain with sweating/short breath</td></tr>
                    <tr><td data-col="Common in GERD">Sour regurgitation; bitter taste in mouth</td><td data-col="Need a different workup">Pain with swallowing; food sticking</td></tr>
                    <tr><td data-col="Common in GERD">Hoarseness or cough in the morning</td><td data-col="Need a different workup">Black stools, vomiting blood, weight loss</td></tr>
                  </tbody>
                </table>
              </div>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip"><strong>Good news:</strong> Small changes in timing, posture, and meals often reduce symptoms within days to weeks.</div>
          </div>

      <!-- 3 Quick relief -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Quick relief steps (right now) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ol>
            <li><strong>Posture:</strong> Sit up straight; avoid bending/lying down for at least 2–3 hours after meals.</li>
            <li><strong>Sips:</strong> Small sips of water; avoid chugging carbonated drinks.</li>
            <li><strong>Loosen:</strong> Loosen tight belts/waistbands.</li>
            <li><strong>Antacid:</strong> If allowed by your clinician, a simple antacid may help occasional symptoms. Check drug interactions.</li>
          </ol>

      <!-- 4 Daily routine -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Daily routine that calms reflux <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Meal timing:</strong> Last meal or snack ≥3 hours before bed.</li>
            <li><strong>Portions:</strong> Smaller meals; avoid “over-full.”</li>
            <li><strong>Weight & movement:</strong> Gentle walks after meals (5–15 minutes) can help.</li>
            <li><strong>Med review:</strong> Ask about pills that can worsen reflux (e.g., some pain, bone, or blood pressure meds) or irritate the esophagus if taken without water.</li>
            <li><strong>Hydration:</strong> Water through the day; limit fizzy drinks.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 5 Foods & drinks -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Foods & drinks: what to try, what to limit <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Often easier</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Oatmeal, whole-grain toast, brown rice</li>
                <li>Bananas, melon, applesauce, cooked vegetables</li>
                <li>Lean proteins: fish, chicken/turkey, beans, tofu</li>
                <li>Low-fat dairy (if tolerated), olive/canola oil</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Common triggers</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Large/fatty meals, fried foods</li>
                <li>Tomato sauces, citrus, chocolate, mint</li>
                <li>Onions/garlic for some people</li>
                <li>Coffee and alcohol (try limits or earlier timing)</li>
                <li>Carbonated beverages</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Triggers vary. Use the log to find <em>your</em> patterns rather than cutting everything.</p>

      <!-- 6 Sleep & positioning -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Sleep & body positioning <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Head-of-bed up:</strong> Raise the head of the bed 6–8 inches with blocks, or use a foam <em>wedge</em>. Extra pillows alone usually don’t help.</li>
            <li><strong>Sleep on your left side</strong> (often reduces reflux compared with right side).</li>
            <li><strong>Night meds:</strong> If you take acid medicines, ask about best timing relative to bedtime and meals.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 7 Medicines -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Medicines (overview & safety) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Medicine options">
            <table class="responsive">
              <thead><tr><th>Type</th><th>How it helps</th><th>Notes</th></tr></thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr>
                  <td data-col="Type">Antacids</td>
                  <td data-col="How it helps">Neutralize acid quickly</td>
                  <td data-col="Notes">Short-term relief; can affect other meds. Some contain sodium or aluminum/magnesium—ask if you have kidney or heart issues.</td>
                </tr>
                <tr>
                  <td data-col="Type">H2 blockers</td>
                  <td data-col="How it helps">Reduce acid for hours</td>
                  <td data-col="Notes">May be used for night symptoms. Interactions and kidney dosing may apply in older adults.</td>
                </tr>
                <tr>
                  <td data-col="Type">PPIs</td>
                  <td data-col="How it helps">Strong acid suppression</td>
                  <td data-col="Notes">Often taken <em>before breakfast</em>. Discuss duration and step-down plan; long use is individualized.</td>
                </tr>
                <tr>
                  <td data-col="Type">Pro-kinetics / others</td>
                  <td data-col="How it helps">Help stomach emptying in select cases</td>
                  <td data-col="Notes">Specialist-guided; watch for side effects and interactions.</td>
                </tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
          <p class="muted"><strong>Safety:</strong> Bring a complete list of medicines/supplements. Some drugs (e.g., certain pain meds) can irritate the esophagus or worsen reflux if taken without water or before lying down.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 When to see the doctor -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> When to get evaluated; tests <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Symptoms ≥2–3 times/week despite lifestyle changes</li>
            <li>Need for frequent OTC meds; rebound when stopping</li>
            <li>Red flags: trouble swallowing, bleeding, weight loss, anemia</li>
            <li>Prior ulcers, esophageal strictures, or Barrett’s esophagus</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Common tests</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Upper endoscopy (looks at esophagus/stomach)</li>
                <li>pH or impedance testing (measures reflux)</li>
                <li>Swallow study or esophageal motility test if needed</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Prep for the visit</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>2-week symptom/food log, list of medicines & supplements</li>
                <li>Note what helps/worsens (timing, positions, foods)</li>
              </ul>
            </div>

      <!-- 9 Caregivers -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Caregivers & family: simple support <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Arrange meals earlier in the evening; set reminders to avoid lying down after eating.</li>
            <li>Help elevate head-of-bed safely; ensure a left-side-sleep wedge is comfortable.</li>
            <li>Keep a shared log of symptoms, triggers, and what helps.</li>
            <li>Review medicine timing and refills; encourage water with pills and staying upright afterward.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 10 Myths -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Myths to ignore <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>“Milk cures heartburn.”</strong> It may soothe briefly but can stimulate acid later.</li>
            <li><strong>“If I feel better, I can stop meds suddenly.”</strong> Some medicines need a step-down plan—ask your clinician.</li>
            <li><strong>“All heartburn is harmless.”</strong> Ongoing symptoms or red flags deserve evaluation.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">How long to try lifestyle changes before meds?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">If symptoms are mild, a 2–4 week trial of timing/portion/position changes is reasonable. If symptoms persist ≥2–3 times per week or affect sleep or daily life, talk with your clinician.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Is nighttime reflux worse?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">It can be. Elevating the head of the bed and avoiding late meals help. Nighttime symptoms deserve a plan with your clinician.</p>
            </div>
