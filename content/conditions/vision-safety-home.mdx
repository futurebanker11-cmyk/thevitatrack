  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What it is & why it matters</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> <strong>Urinary incontinence</strong> is leaking urine you can’t fully control. It is common—and <em>treatable</em>. The right mix of habits, pelvic floor training, and, when needed, medicines or procedures can significantly reduce leaks.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Good to know:</strong> A <em>bladder diary</em> (3 days of time/amounts, leaks, triggers) helps pinpoint what works for you and guides your clinician.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to get urgent help</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Inability to pass urine with painful lower belly swelling</li>
        <li>Blood in urine, fever, flank pain, or burning with urination</li>
        <li>New severe back weakness/numbness with bladder/bowel issues</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can signal <strong>urinary retention</strong>, infection (UTI/kidney), stones, or nerve emergencies. Seek urgent care.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <section id="types" class="section">
    <h2>Types of incontinence</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Types of urinary incontinence">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Type</th><th>Clues</th><th>What helps</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td>Stress</td>
          <td>Leaks with cough, laugh, sneeze, lifting</td>
          <td>Pelvic floor training, weight loss if needed, pessary, slings (selected)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Urge / Overactive bladder</td>
          <td>Sudden urge, frequent trips, nighttime urination</td>
          <td>Bladder training, pelvic floor “quick squeezes,” limit bladder irritants, certain meds</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Mixed</td>
          <td>Features of stress and urge</td>
          <td>Start with habits/pelvic floor; add meds if urge persists</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Overflow</td>
          <td>Dribbling, weak stream, feeling not empty; often with enlarged prostate or nerve issues</td>
          <td>Address blockage (e.g., BPH treatment), check post-void residual, avoid constipation</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Functional</td>
          <td>Leaks due to mobility, vision, or cognitive barriers</td>
          <td>Timed voiding, easy-to-remove clothing, clear path to toilet, bedside commode</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>

  <!-- Self-check -->
  <section id="screen" class="section">
    <h2>Simple self-check</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Ask yourself (past 2 weeks)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>How many leaks per day/week? What was happening each time?</li>
          <li>Do you rush to the bathroom with little warning?</li>
          <li>Do you wake ≥2× at night to urinate?</li>
          <li>Any burning, blood, or pain (UTI/stone clues)?</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="small">Track in a 3-day diary (time, amount, leaks, triggers, pads used). Bring to your visit.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Hydration target:</strong> Unless you’re on fluid restrictions, aim for pale-yellow urine. Concentrated urine can irritate the bladder.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Plan -->
  <section id="plan" class="section">
    <h2>Bladder-friendly daily plan</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Core habits</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Timed voiding:</strong> Go every 2–3 hours while awake; don’t wait until urgent</li>
          <li><strong>Bladder training:</strong> When urge hits, pause → sit/stand still → do 5 quick pelvic squeezes → urge should ease → walk to toilet</li>
          <li><strong>Evening routine:</strong> Limit fluids 2–3 hours before bed; avoid caffeine after noon and alcohol in the evening</li>
          <li><strong>Constipation care:</strong> Fiber, fluids, movement; straining worsens leaks</li>
          <li><strong>Weight & movement:</strong> Even modest weight loss can reduce stress leaks; walk most days</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Bathroom setup:</strong> Night light, clear path, raised seat/grab bars if needed; clothing that’s easy to remove (Velcro/elastic).
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Pelvic floor -->
  <section id="pelvic" class="section">
    <h2>Pelvic floor basics</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Kegel how-to</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Tighten the muscles you’d use to stop gas or urine; avoid tightening belly/thighs</li>
          <li>Hold 3–5 seconds, relax 3–5 seconds; repeat 10 times → do 3 sets/day</li>
          <li>Add <strong>“quick flicks”</strong> (1-second squeezes) to calm sudden urges</li>
          <li>Consistency: improvements usually show in 4–8 weeks</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Get guidance:</strong> Pelvic floor physical therapy or continence nurses can check technique and tailor a program. For severe prolapse, ask about a <strong>pessary</strong>.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Meds -->
  <section id="meds" class="section">
    <h2>Medicines & procedures (talk to your clinician)</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Treatment options">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Option</th><th>Helps with</th><th>Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td>Topical vaginal estrogen</td>
          <td>Urgency, recurrent UTIs in post-menopausal women</td>
          <td>Local, low systemic absorption; not the same as pills/patches</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Bladder relaxers</td>
          <td>Urge/overactive bladder</td>
          <td><strong>Antimuscarinics</strong> (e.g., oxybutynin, solifenacin) may cause dry mouth/constipation/confusion in some; <strong>β3-agonists</strong> (e.g., mirabegron, vibegron) may raise BP—monitor</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Alpha-blockers (men)</td>
          <td>BPH-related symptoms</td>
          <td>Can lower blood pressure → dizziness; take care with falls</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Bulking agents / slings</td>
          <td>Stress incontinence</td>
          <td>Urogynecology/urology procedures; discuss durability and risks</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Botox (bladder)</td>
          <td>Refractory overactive bladder</td>
          <td>Can cause temporary urinary retention; may need self-catheterization</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Neuromodulation</td>
          <td>Urge incontinence</td>
          <td>Tibial nerve stimulation (in-office/home) or sacral nerve stimulation (implant)</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Products -->
  <section id="products" class="section">
    <h2>Products & helpful tech</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Make life easier</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Absorbent pads/underwear sized correctly; change regularly to protect skin</li>
          <li>Moisture barrier creams to prevent irritation; gentle cleanser (no harsh wipes)</li>
          <li>Bed & chair protectors, bedside commode or urinal for nights</li>
          <li>Phone reminders for timed voiding; leak/void tracking apps</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Skin care:</strong> Clean and dry promptly after leaks; treat rashes early. Consider breathable products to reduce moisture.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support with dignity</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Offer bathroom prompts every 2–3 hours and before outings/bed</li>
          <li>Use clothing that’s easy to remove; ensure path/lighting to toilet</li>
          <li>Document triggers, pad counts, and skin integrity; share at visits</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Call the clinician for:</strong> Burning or fever, new confusion (possible UTI), blood in urine, sudden retention, or worsening nighttime accidents.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is leakage a normal part of aging?</p>
        <p>No—common, but <strong>not</strong> inevitable. Most people improve with habits, pelvic floor training, and targeted treatments.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Should I drink less water?</p>
        <p>Don’t dehydrate. Spread fluids earlier in the day, cut evening/caffeinated drinks, and aim for pale-yellow urine unless your clinician advises restrictions.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How long until I see progress?</p>
        <p>With daily pelvic floor work and bladder training, many notice improvement in 4–8 weeks. Keep a diary to track wins.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When should I see a specialist?</p>
        <p>Persistent leaks despite 6–8 weeks of habits, recurrent UTIs, blood in urine, retention, severe prolapse, or suspected neurologic issues.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
