          <h2 style="margin-top:12px">Quick facts</h2>
          <div class="quickfacts" style="margin-top:8px">
            <div><strong>Low is not always bad:</strong> Many people feel fine with “naturally low” pressure.</div>
            <div><strong>Symptoms matter most:</strong> dizziness, blurred vision, weakness, or fainting are red flags.</div>
            <div><strong>Standing drops are common after 60:</strong> this is called <em>orthostatic hypotension</em>.</div>
            <div><strong>Prevention helps:</strong> rise slowly, hydrate, small meals, and ask about medication timing.</div>

      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> What counts as “low”? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>For many adults, <strong>less than 90/60</strong> is considered low. But the key question is: <em>how do you feel?</em> If you’re symptom-free, a lower number may be normal for you. If you feel dizzy or weak—especially on standing—speak with your clinician.</p>
          <table aria-label="Low BP basics">
            <thead><tr><th>Situation</th><th>What it means</th><th>Notes</th></tr></thead>
            <tbody>
              <tr><td>Reading &lt; 90/60 but you feel fine</td><td>May be normal</td><td>Track trends; keep hydrated and rise slowly.</td></tr>
              <tr><td>Reading &lt; 90/60 with symptoms</td><td>Concerning</td><td>Call your clinician; review meds/fluids; watch for illness.</td></tr>
              <tr><td>Drop when standing</td><td>Orthostatic hypotension</td><td>Fall risk rises; see “Standing test” section.</td></tr>
            </tbody>
          </table>
          <p class="muted">Some conditions (heart failure, kidney disease) change targets—follow your clinician’s plan.</p>

      <!-- Standing test -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Do-it-yourself standing test (orthostatic) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <ol>
                <li><strong>Rest 5 minutes seated.</strong> Feet on the floor, back supported. Check BP and pulse.</li>
                <li><strong>Stand up carefully.</strong> Use a counter for balance.</li>
                <li><strong>Recheck at 1 minute and 3 minutes.</strong></li>
              </ol>
              <p><strong>Result is “orthostatic”</strong> if the top number drops by <strong>≥20</strong>, or the bottom by <strong>≥10</strong>, within 3 minutes and you feel dizzy.</p>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip"><strong>Safety first:</strong> If you get light-headed, sit back down. Keep a chair nearby, and don’t do this test alone if you’ve fainted before.</div>
          </div>

      <!-- Daily plan -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Daily plan to prevent dips <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Rise-slow routine</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Sit up, <strong>dangle feet</strong> 30–60 seconds, then stand.</li>
                <li><strong>3-breath pause:</strong> deep breaths before walking.</li>
                <li>Use handrail or counter in the morning.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Fluids & meals</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Water goal:</strong> small sips through the day (ask your clinician if you have heart/kidney limits).</li>
                <li><strong>Smaller meals:</strong> large, heavy meals can drop BP—try 4–5 lighter ones.</li>
                <li><strong>Limit alcohol;</strong> ask about moderate caffeine if it helps symptoms.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Compression & activity</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Compression socks/stockings</strong> (knee-high 15–20 mmHg to start) after clinician approval.</li>
                <li>Gentle walks; avoid standing still for long periods.</li>
                <li>Heat can drop BP: stay cool, rise slowly from hot showers.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- Foods & salt -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Foods, salt & drinks (use wisely) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>May help raise BP gently</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Regular water intake; an extra glass before activity if approved.</li>
                <li>Balanced meals with protein + complex carbs to avoid post-meal dips.</li>
                <li>Small amount of extra salt <em>only if your clinician approves</em>.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Be cautious / avoid</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Big carb-heavy meals (can cause after-meal hypotension).</li>
                <li>Alcohol (drops BP and causes dehydration).</li>
                <li>Added salt if you have heart failure or kidney disease—<strong>follow your plan</strong>.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">If you have fluid or sodium restrictions, do <strong>not</strong> change them without medical advice.</p>

      <!-- Medications -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Medicines that can lower BP <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Review your list with your clinician or pharmacist. Some drugs can lower BP or worsen standing drops, especially when combined.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Blood-pressure pills (diuretics, ACEi/ARB, beta-blockers, calcium channel blockers).</li>
            <li>Alpha-blockers (for prostate or BP), nitrates, some Parkinson’s drugs.</li>
            <li>Certain antidepressants, sedatives, opioid pain meds.</li>
            <li>High-dose diabetes meds causing low sugar (can feel like low BP).</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note"><strong>Do not stop medicines suddenly.</strong> Ask about timing (e.g., taking some at night), dose changes, or alternatives.</div>
        </div>
      </details>

        <div class="content">
          <p>Log date/time, position (lying/sitting/standing), top/bottom numbers, pulse, and a short note (meal, hot shower, missed dose). Track morning, before lunch, and evening for a few days to spot patterns.</p>

      <!-- Special situations -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Special notes (65+, diabetes, neuropathy, illness) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Age 65+:</strong> Main goal is <em>no falls</em>. Focus on rise-slow routine and hydration plan.</li>
            <li><strong>Diabetes/autonomic neuropathy:</strong> standing drops are common—ask about compression, medication timing, and mealtime strategies.</li>
            <li><strong>Illness, vomiting, diarrhea, fever:</strong> dehydration lowers BP—seek advice early.</li>
            <li><strong>Heat exposure & hot showers:</strong> can drop BP—cool environment, sit while drying/dressing.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- Myths -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Common myths <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>“Low numbers are always bad.”</strong> Not if you feel well—symptoms guide action.</li>
            <li><strong>“Salt fixes everything.”</strong> Not safe for everyone; follow your clinician’s advice.</li>
            <li><strong>“Only young people faint.”</strong> Standing drops are common after 60—and preventable.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- Caregivers -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> Caregivers & family <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Help set up safe routines: hydration reminders, smaller meals, compression socks in the morning (if approved), clutter-free paths, and a shower chair or grab bars. Keep a simple log and bring it to appointments.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Is 90/60 dangerous?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Not always. If you feel well and stay steady, it may be your normal. If you’re dizzy or faint, call your clinician.</p>

            <h3 itemprop="name">What helps fast if I feel light-headed?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Sit or lie down, elevate legs, sip water, and breathe slowly. If symptoms persist or you faint, seek urgent care.</p>
