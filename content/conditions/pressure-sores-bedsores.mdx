  <!-- What it is -->
  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What it is</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> A pressure sore happens when skin and underlying tissue are damaged by <strong>unrelieved pressure</strong>, shear, or friction—often over bony areas in people who sit or lie in one position for long periods. Early action prevents deeper wounds and infection.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Key idea:</strong> Rotate positions on a schedule, use proper cushions/mattress, keep skin clean and dry but moisturized, and support protein and calorie needs.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- ===== In-article AD ===== -->
  

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>Urgent red-flags</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Fever, chills, or feeling unwell with a wound</li>
        <li>Rapidly spreading redness, warmth, bad odor, or pus</li>
        <li>Black/purple skin that’s quickly worsening or new severe pain</li>
        <li>Visible bone/tendon or deep crater; signs of sepsis (confusion, fast heart rate)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Seek immediate care—deep infection and <strong>osteomyelitis</strong> can occur.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Risk -->
  <section id="risk" class="section">
    <h2>Who is at risk</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Limited mobility, wheelchair/bed-bound</li>
          <li>Diabetes, vascular disease, anemia, malnutrition</li>
          <li>Incontinence, moisture, or sweating</li>
          <li>Older age, thin skin, prior pressure injuries</li>
          <li>Sedating medicines; dementia or poor awareness of pain/position</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Risk scoring:</strong> Tools like the Braden Scale (sensory, moisture, activity, mobility, nutrition, friction/shear) guide prevention plans.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- ===== In-article AD (after Risk) ===== -->
  

  <!-- Sites -->
  <section id="sites" class="section">
    <h2>Common sites</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Lying on the back</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Back of head, shoulder blades</li>
          <li>Elbows, spine, tailbone/sacrum</li>
          <li>Heels</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Side-lying & sitting</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Hips/greater trochanters, knees/ankles (bony contact)</li>
          <li>Ischial tuberosities (“sitting bones”), sacrum/coccyx</li>
          <li>Heels and toes, especially with sheets pulling</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Staging -->
  <section id="staging" class="section">
    <h2>Staging (1–4, unstageable, deep tissue)</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Pressure injury staging">
      <thead><tr><th>Stage</th><th>Skin findings</th><th>Notes</th></tr></thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stage 1</strong></td>
          <td>Intact skin with <strong>non-blanchable redness</strong> over a bony area; may feel warm/firm/painful</td>
          <td>Earliest warning—offload pressure urgently</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stage 2</strong></td>
          <td>Partial-thickness loss of skin (epidermis/dermis); shallow open ulcer or blister</td>
          <td>Keep clean, moist-healing dressings, protect from shear</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stage 3</strong></td>
          <td>Full-thickness skin loss; fat may be visible; <em>no</em> exposed bone/tendon</td>
          <td>May need debridement; infection risk rises</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stage 4</strong></td>
          <td>Full-thickness tissue loss with exposed <strong>bone, tendon, or muscle</strong></td>
          <td>High infection risk; urgent specialist care</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Unstageable</strong></td>
          <td>Base covered by slough/eschar; depth unknown</td>
          <td>Debridement (when appropriate) reveals true stage</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Deep Tissue</strong></td>
          <td>Purple/maroon discoloration or blood-filled blister</td>
          <td>From pressure/shear; can progress quickly</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Prevention -->
  <section id="prevention" class="section">
    <h2>Prevention checklist</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Turn & offload</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Reposition at least <strong>every 2 hours in bed</strong>; every 15–30 minutes in chair (brief weight shifts)</li>
          <li><strong>Heels off the bed</strong>—use pillows/heel protectors</li>
          <li>Use <strong>30° side-lying</strong> (not directly on the hip)</li>
          <li>Keep head of bed as low as safely possible to reduce shear</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Surfaces & cushions</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>High-quality foam or <strong>alternating-pressure</strong> mattress when high risk or existing sores</li>
          <li>Wheelchair cushion (gel/air/contour) fitted by therapist</li>
          <li>Don’t sit on donut cushions—can worsen pressure</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Skin routine</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Inspect skin daily (sacrum, heels, hips); check for non-blanching redness</li>
          <li>Cleanse gently; pat dry; apply <strong>barrier cream</strong> where moisture exposed</li>
          <li>Moisturize dry skin; avoid vigorous rubbing over bony areas</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Movement & fit</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Adjust splints/oxygen tubing/catheters to avoid pressure points</li>
          <li>Keep sheets smooth; avoid wrinkles and “friction burn”</li>
          <li>Encourage small, frequent mobility as able; physical therapy referral</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Moisture -->
  <section id="moisture" class="section">
    <h2>Moisture & incontinence care</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Prompt cleanse after urine/stool; use pH-balanced cleansers</li>
          <li>Apply <strong>zinc oxide or dimethicone</strong> barrier creams each change</li>
          <li>Breathable briefs; avoid plastic underpads directly on skin</li>
          <li>Consider fecal/urine diversion devices (clinician-guided) for severe cases</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Yeast rash care:</strong> Bright red with satellite spots suggests candidiasis—ask about antifungal plus barrier layering.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- ===== In-article AD (mid page) ===== -->
  

  <!-- Nutrition -->
  <section id="nutrition" class="section">
    <h2>Nutrition for healing</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Target adequate <strong>calories and protein</strong> (protein most meals/snacks)</li>
          <li>Hydration plan; consider <strong>dietitian</strong> for tailored needs</li>
          <li>Vitamin C and zinc only if deficient or advised—avoid high doses without guidance</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Clues of malnutrition:</strong> unintentional weight loss, muscle loss, poor appetite—address early for better wound outcomes.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Wound care options</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Dressings (match to wound)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Foam</strong> for moderate drainage; cushioning</li>
          <li><strong>Hydrocolloid</strong> for shallow, low-drainage wounds (not infected)</li>
          <li><strong>Alginate</strong> for heavy drainage; needs cover dressing</li>
          <li><strong>Hydrogel</strong> for dry wounds/eschar (when appropriate)</li>
          <li><strong>Silver/iodine</strong> dressings short-term if high bioburden (clinician guided)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Debridement</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Autolytic</strong> (occlusive dressings), <strong>enzymatic</strong> ointments</li>
          <li><strong>Conservative sharp</strong> and surgical debridement by trained clinicians</li>
          <li>Stable, dry heel eschar may be left intact unless infection—specialist decides</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Advanced options</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Negative pressure wound therapy</strong> (vacuum-assisted) for select deep wounds</li>
          <li>Consult wound clinic for stalled wounds or complex cases</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Antibiotics:</strong> Use for clinical infection, not for colonization. Culture after appropriate cleaning/debridement if needed—guided by clinician.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Pain -->
  <section id="pain" class="section">
    <h2>Pain & comfort</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Time oral pain medicine <strong>before dressing changes</strong></li>
          <li>Moist dressings reduce pain vs. dry gauze</li>
          <li>Pressure relief cushions and gentle positioning supports</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card small">
        Related: <a href="/conditions/chronic-pain">Chronic Pain</a> · <a href="/conditions/balance-problems-falls">Balance Problems & Falls</a> · <a href="/conditions/diabetes-type-2">Diabetes (Type 2)</a>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Set a written <strong>turning schedule</strong>; use phone alarms</li>
          <li>Log wound size, drainage, odor, pain; bring to visits</li>
          <li>Use draw sheets/lift devices—avoid dragging the skin</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>When to call:</strong> new non-blanching redness, increased drainage/odor, fevers, or decline in alertness—don’t wait.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How fast can a sore develop?</p>
        <p>High-risk skin can break down within hours under constant pressure—especially heels and sacrum. That’s why scheduled turning matters.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is redness always bad?</p>
        <p>If a red area <strong>doesn’t blanch</strong> (stay white briefly when pressed) or feels warm/firm, it’s an early injury—offload right away.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I need a special mattress?</p>
        <p>People at moderate to high risk or with existing sores often benefit from high-quality foam or alternating-pressure surfaces—get fitted advice.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Should I use antibiotic ointment?</p>
        <p>Not routinely. Choose dressings matched to drainage; reserve antibiotics for clear infection under clinician guidance.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
