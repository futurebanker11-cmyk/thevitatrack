  <!-- What is -->
  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What is hypothyroidism?</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> The thyroid gland makes hormones that help set your body’s “speed.” When levels are low, many systems slow down—energy, heart rate, digestion, skin/hair, and thinking can be affected. Treatment replaces the missing hormone.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Good to know:</strong> Symptoms may be subtle in older adults and overlap with other conditions. Blood tests confirm the diagnosis and guide dosing.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Symptoms -->
  <section id="symptoms" class="section">
    <h2>Typical symptoms (older adults)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Low energy, feeling cold, weight gain or difficulty losing weight</li>
          <li>Dry skin, hair loss, brittle nails</li>
          <li>Constipation, slower digestion</li>
          <li>Muscle cramps, joint aches</li>
          <li>Low mood, slowed thinking, memory complaints</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">

  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Cause</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Hashimoto’s thyroiditis</strong></td>
          <td>Autoimmune; gradual gland damage; antibodies often present</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Thyroid surgery or radioactive iodine</strong></td>
          <td>Past treatment for nodules, cancer, or hyperthyroidism</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Medicines</strong></td>
          <td>Amiodarone, lithium, immune/targeted therapies; high iodine exposure</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Pituitary or hypothalamic disease</strong></td>
          <td>Central hypothyroidism (low/normal TSH with low Free T4)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Iodine deficiency/excess</strong></td>
          <td>Less common with iodized salt; excess can also impair function</td>
        </tr>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>How it’s diagnosed (TSH, Free T4)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Primary hypothyroidism</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>TSH is high and Free T4 is low (or low-normal)</li>
          <li>“Subclinical”: TSH high with normal Free T4; decisions depend on symptoms, age, heart risk, and TSH level</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Central hypothyroidism</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>TSH low/normal with low Free T4 → pituitary/hypothalamic cause</li>
          <li>Needs specialist evaluation; TSH not reliable to monitor therapy</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small" style="margin-top:8px">Other checks may include thyroid antibodies, lipid panel, sodium, anemia screen, and medication review.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment basics (levothyroxine)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What it is</p>
        <p>Levothyroxine (T4) is identical to the main thyroid hormone your body produces. Your tissues convert some T4 to T3 as needed.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Starting doses (older adults)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Lower starting doses are common, especially with heart disease</li>
          <li>Slow titration helps avoid palpitations or angina</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <p class="kicker">Brand vs generic</p>
        <p>Consistency matters. If the manufacturer changes, repeat labs after the switch to confirm dose still fits.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <p><strong>Do not self-adjust.</strong> Over-replacement can raise the risk of atrial fibrillation and bone loss.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Timing & Interactions -->
  <section id="timing" class="section">
    <h2>How to take your dose (timing & interactions)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Timing</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Take on an <strong>empty stomach</strong> with water, same time each day</li>
          <li>Wait at least <strong>30–60 minutes before breakfast</strong> (or take at bedtime 3+ hours after last meal)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Common blockers (separate by 4 hours)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Calcium, iron, magnesium, multivitamins</li>
          <li>Cholestyramine, sucralfate, some fiber supplements</li>
          <li>Soy or very high-fiber meals can reduce absorption</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Medicine interactions (examples)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Warfarin (INR can change), digoxin, insulin/oral diabetes meds</li>
          <li>Proton pump inhibitors and certain antidepressants may alter levels</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        If you miss a dose, you can usually take it when remembered that day. Do not double up without guidance.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Dose adjust -->
  <section id="dose-adjust" class="section">
    <h2>Dose adjustments & follow-up</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Recheck timing</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>TSH (and Free T4 when indicated) about <strong>6–8 weeks</strong> after any dose change</li>
          <li>Once stable, labs every 6–12 months or if symptoms change</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When to re-test sooner</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>New medicines that interact (iron/calcium started, PPI changes)</li>
          <li>New symptoms (palpitations, weight loss, insomnia) suggesting over-replacement</li>
          <li>Significant weight change, pregnancy planning, or acute illness</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- ===== In-article AD (near bottom) ===== -->
  

  <!-- Special -->
  <section id="special" class="section">
    <h2>Special situations</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Heart disease</p>
        <p>Start low and go slow to avoid angina or rhythm problems. Report chest pain, racing heart, or new shortness of breath.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Bone health</p>
        <p>Too much thyroid hormone increases bone loss. Ensure dosing keeps TSH in target; keep up calcium/vitamin D if advised.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Central hypothyroidism</p>
        <p>TSH is not reliable to monitor; dosing follows Free T4 and symptoms under specialist care.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Myxedema coma (rare emergency)</p>
        <p>Severe hypothyroidism with confusion, low temperature, and low blood pressure. Call emergency services immediately.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can I take levothyroxine with coffee?</p>
        <p>Coffee can reduce absorption if taken together. Take the pill with water and wait at least 30 minutes before coffee.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Why do I still feel tired on treatment?</p>
        <p>Other conditions (sleep apnea, anemia, depression, low activity, meds) can contribute. Check labs and look for other causes.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is natural desiccated thyroid better?</p>
        <p>Most guidelines prefer levothyroxine alone. Combination or desiccated products are individualized and need careful monitoring.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I need to avoid iodine salt?</p>
        <p>Normal iodized salt is generally fine; avoid high-dose supplements unless prescribed.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
