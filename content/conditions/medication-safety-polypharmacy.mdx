  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What polypharmacy is & why it matters</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> <strong>Polypharmacy</strong> means taking many medications (often 5+), including prescriptions, over-the-counter pills, vitamins, and herbs. In older adults, multiple drugs can interact and raise the risk of falls, confusion, bleeding, kidney problems, and hospital visits.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Good to know:</strong> <em>Never</em> stop or change a medicine without your clinician’s advice. Safer plans often come from a slow, supervised <strong>deprescribing</strong> process—removing meds that no longer help or cause harm.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to get urgent help</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Severe allergic reaction: swelling of face/lips, trouble breathing, hives</li>
        <li>Fainting, chest pain, severe confusion, new seizures, or falls with injury</li>
        <li>Black/tarry stools, vomiting blood, or severe abdominal pain</li>
        <li>Very low blood sugar symptoms (sweats, shaking, confusion, collapse)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Call emergency services or go to urgent care now. Bring all your medicines or a clear list.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Signs -->
  <section id="signs" class="section">
    <h2>Common signs of medication problems</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>New dizziness, drowsiness, or unsteady walking</li>
          <li>Confusion, memory changes, or hallucinations</li>
          <li>Upset stomach, constipation, diarrhea, or appetite loss</li>
          <li>Bruising/bleeding easily; swelling of legs; sudden weight change</li>
          <li>Low mood, anxiety, or sleep reversal after starting a new medicine</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

  <!-- High-risk meds -->
  <section id="risks" class="section">
    <h2>High-risk meds & combinations</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="High-risk drugs and combinations">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Category</th><th>Examples</th><th>Why risky (older adults)</th><th>Safer steps</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td>Sedatives & sleep aids</td>
          <td>Benzodiazepines (diazepam, lorazepam), “Z-drugs” (zolpidem)</td>
          <td>Falls, confusion, memory problems, car crashes</td>
          <td>Sleep hygiene, CBT-I; discuss tapering; non-drug options first</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Strong pain meds</td>
          <td>Opioids; combo with benzodiazepines</td>
          <td>Over-sedation, slowed breathing, constipation, falls</td>
          <td>Use lowest dose/shortest time; avoid opioid + benzo combo</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Anticholinergics</td>
          <td>Diphenhydramine, certain bladder & allergy meds</td>
          <td>Confusion, constipation, dry mouth, urinary retention</td>
          <td>Ask for alternatives with lower anticholinergic burden</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Blood thinners</td>
          <td>Warfarin, DOACs; plus NSAIDs (ibuprofen), aspirin (duplicate)</td>
          <td>Bleeding risk, dangerous interactions, food/drug conflicts</td>
          <td>One prescriber monitors; avoid duplicate NSAIDs; INR or follow-ups</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Diabetes meds</td>
          <td>Insulin, sulfonylureas (glyburide)</td>
          <td>Low blood sugar → falls, confusion</td>
          <td>Safer agents where possible; clear meals/monitoring plan</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Kidney-cleared meds</td>
          <td>Metformin, certain antibiotics, digoxin</td>
          <td>Build-up if kidneys slow → toxicity</td>
          <td>Dose adjust for eGFR; check kidney tests periodically</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Herb/OTC interactions</td>
          <td>St. John’s wort, ginkgo; decongestants; antacids</td>
          <td>Reduce drug levels or raise BP; block absorption</td>
          <td>Always list all supplements; check with pharmacist</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Brown-bag -->
  <section id="screen" class="section">
    <h2>Brown-bag self-check</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Once every 3–6 months</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Put <strong>all</strong> meds, vitamins, and herbs in a bag (include eye/ear drops, creams, inhalers).</li>
          <li>Bring to your pharmacist or clinician. Ask: “Which can be lowered or stopped?”</li>
          <li>Look for duplicates (two allergy meds), outdated drugs, or confusing schedules.</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="small">Keep one updated list with drug name, dose, reason, and who prescribed it.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Deprescribing:</strong> Stopping meds is <em>medical treatment</em>. It’s done slowly with monitoring to avoid withdrawal or rebound symptoms.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Daily plan -->
  <section id="daily-plan" class="section">
    <h2>Daily medication-safety plan</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Organize</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>One pharmacy</strong> if possible; one prescriber to coordinate</li>
          <li><strong>Weekly pillbox</strong> (AM/PM). Refill the same day each week.</li>
          <li>Use a <strong>printed schedule</strong> on the fridge; bring to visits.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Safety steps</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Ask for <strong>lowest effective dose</strong> and shortest duration.</li>
          <li>Before new meds: “Any <strong>interactions</strong> with my current list?”</li>
          <li>Report new dizziness, confusion, falls, or bleeding <strong>right away</strong>.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <div class="note" style="margin-top:12px">Never share medicines. Store in original labeled bottles; keep a travel copy of your list.</div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tech -->
  <section id="tech" class="section">
    <h2>Pillboxes & reminder tech</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Make it easy</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Vibrating reminders, phone alarms, or smart pill caps</li>
          <li>Large-print labels; color coding (morning = yellow, night = blue)</li>
          <li>Automatic refills and synchronized pickup dates</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Travel tip:</strong> Keep meds in carry-on with a copy of prescriptions. Use a small pillbox for day trips; set a one-tap phone reminder.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Community -->
  <section id="community" class="section">
    <h2>Pharmacist & community help</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What to ask a pharmacist</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>“Which of my meds are <strong>high-risk</strong> for falls or confusion?”</li>
          <li>“Any interactions with <strong>OTC or supplements</strong> I’m taking?”</li>
          <li>“Can my schedule be <strong>simplified</strong> (once-daily options)?”</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Access & cost</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Ask about generic options and patient assistance programs.</li>
          <li>Request <strong>90-day supplies</strong> for stable long-term meds.</li>
          <li>Home delivery or blister packs can reduce errors.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support & observe</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Attend visits with the med list; ask about deprescribing opportunities.</li>
          <li>Watch for new drowsiness, confusion, or unsteady walking.</li>
          <li>Track bowel movements and appetite after med changes.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Reduce errors</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Pre-fill a weekly pillbox; double-check against the schedule.</li>
          <li>Lock up high-risk meds (opioids, sedatives) if others visit the home.</li>
          <li>Use one shared calendar for refill dates and lab checks.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How many meds are “too many”?</p>
        <p>There’s no single number, but risks rise around <strong>5 or more</strong>. Focus on whether each drug is <em>still needed</em>, effective, and safe together.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is it safe to stop on my own?</p>
        <p><strong>No.</strong> Some meds need a <strong>slow taper</strong> to avoid withdrawal or rebound. Ask your clinician for a plan.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do vitamins/herbs count?</p>
        <p>Yes. List <strong>all</strong> supplements and OTC products—many interact with prescriptions or affect blood tests and surgery.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What’s the fastest safety win?</p>
        <p>Keep <strong>one master list</strong>, use <strong>one pharmacy</strong>, and book a <strong>brown-bag review</strong> to remove duplicates and risky combos.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
