      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What is nocturia? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>Nocturia</strong> means waking at night to pass urine. Once a night can be normal for many adults—especially with evening drinks—but waking <strong>two or more times</strong> often affects sleep, energy, and fall risk.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Common contributors include late fluids, salt-related swelling that “drains” overnight, diabetes, sleep apnea, prostate enlargement (BPH), overactive bladder, and some medicines.</li>
            <li>Good news: small habit shifts and timing changes help many people within 1–2 weeks.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 2 Quick fixes -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> First steps to try this week <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Fluids & timing</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Spread drinks through the day; <strong>reduce big drinks 2–3 hours before bed</strong>.</li>
                <li>Limit evening caffeine (tea/coffee/cola) and alcohol—both can increase urine.</li>
                <li>If thirsty at night, take small sips, not a full glass.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Salt & swelling</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>High-salt meals pull water into tissues—your body “dumps” it overnight.</li>
                <li>Keep legs up for 30–60 minutes in the early evening; consider <strong>compression socks</strong> (ask your clinician).</li>
                <li>Walk 5–10 minutes after dinner to help fluid shift before bedtime.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Bladder habits</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Go once right before bed (don’t “hover” peeing many times—can irritate bladder).</li>
                <li>Calm breathing if you wake—sometimes urge fades within a minute.</li>
                <li>Avoid straining; relax shoulders, feet on the floor, lean slightly forward.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 3 Why it happens -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Common causes by pattern <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Fluid shift overnight</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Leg swelling in the day (heart, veins, salt) returns to the bloodstream when lying down → kidneys make more urine.</li>
                <li>Often improved with earlier leg elevation, compression, and salt awareness.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Bladder/prostate & other triggers</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>BPH (men):</strong> slower stream, incomplete emptying → frequent trips.</li>
                <li><strong>Overactive bladder:</strong> sudden urge, small amounts.</li>
                <li><strong>Diabetes / high glucose:</strong> pulls water into urine.</li>
                <li><strong>Sleep apnea:</strong> hormone shifts increase urine at night.</li>
                <li><strong>Diuretics:</strong> late dose timing pushes night output.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Your clinician may ask about timing, volume, swelling, snoring, and medication list to pinpoint the mix.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 4 Medication timing -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Medication timing & checks <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Diuretics (“water pills”):</strong> if taken late day, ask if you can move to morning or early afternoon.</li>
            <li><strong>Evening caffeine & alcohol:</strong> reduce or cut off by late afternoon/early evening.</li>
            <li><strong>Diabetes meds & glucose:</strong> review targets; high evening glucose raises urine volume.</li>
            <li><strong>BPH medicines:</strong> alpha-blockers or others may help night symptoms—ask your clinician.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 5 Evening routine -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> A sleep-friendly evening routine <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>2–3 hours before bed</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Finish most fluids for the day; only small sips after this.</li>
                <li>Legs up (or light compression) 30–60 minutes.</li>
                <li>Short, easy walk after dinner; avoid heavy exercise late.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>1 hour before bed</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Bathroom once before lights out.</li>
                <li>Limit screens; dim lights; cool, dark room with safe night light.</li>
                <li>Calm breath: in through the nose 4 counts, out 6–8 counts.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 7 Pelvic floor & bladder training -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Pelvic floor & bladder training <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Pelvic floor basics</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Tighten the muscles you’d use to stop gas or urine; hold 3–5 sec; relax 5–10 sec; repeat 10 times.</li>
                <li>Practice 2–3 sets daily; avoid holding breath or squeezing thighs/abs.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Bladder training</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>If you can, delay by 1–3 minutes when urge hits; use slow breathing or distraction.</li>
                <li>Gradually lengthen daytime intervals; calmer bladder often helps the night too.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 When to seek care -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> When to call your clinician (red flags) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="note warn">
            <ul>
              <li>Pain/burning, fever, or blood in urine</li>
              <li>Marked swelling of legs/face or sudden weight gain</li>
              <li>Very large night volumes despite limiting fluids</li>
              <li>New bedwetting, severe urgency, or inability to pass urine</li>
              <li>Loud snoring, gasping, or daytime sleepiness (possible sleep apnea)</li>
            </ul>
            <p><strong>Emergency:</strong> severe back/side pain with fever, confusion, chest pain, or severe breathlessness—call emergency services.</p>
          </div>
