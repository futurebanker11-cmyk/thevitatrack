      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What does “protein in urine” mean? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Healthy kidneys keep most protein in your blood. When the filters are stressed or damaged, small amounts of protein—often <strong>albumin</strong>—leak into urine. This is called <strong>proteinuria</strong> or <strong>albuminuria</strong>. It can be <em>temporary</em> (illness, exercise, dehydration) or a sign of <strong>chronic kidney disease (CKD)</strong>.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Don’t panic over one test.</strong> Your clinician often repeats a urine test to confirm.</li>
            <li><strong>Risk is about the whole picture:</strong> urine albumin, eGFR, blood pressure, diabetes control, and symptoms.</li>
          </ul>

        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> How protein is tested (dipstick, ACR, 24-hr) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Common tests</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Dipstick (screening):</strong> quick, but can miss small leaks or be affected by urine concentration.</li>
                <li><strong>Urine ACR:</strong> <em>Albumin-to-Creatinine Ratio</em> on a spot sample. Best routine test to track small leaks.</li>
                <li><strong>24-hour urine:</strong> adds detail in special cases (e.g., high levels, unclear results).</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Best sample:</strong> first morning urine, avoid testing during fever, heavy exercise, or menstruation, and repeat if unsure.
            </div>

      <!-- 3 ACR ranges -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Understanding ACR ranges (A1–A3) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>ACR</strong> (albumin/creatinine ratio) is often reported as <strong>mg/g</strong> (US) or <strong>mg/mmol</strong> (SI). The categories below are widely used. Your lab’s exact phrasing may vary.</p>
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="ACR categories table">
            <table>
              <thead><tr><th>Category</th><th>ACR (mg/g)</th><th>ACR (mg/mmol)</th><th>What it suggests</th></tr></thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr><td><strong>A1</strong> (normal to mildly increased)</td><td>&lt; 30</td><td>&lt; 3</td><td>Usually normal; track if other risk factors</td></tr>
                <tr><td><strong>A2</strong> (moderately increased)</td><td>30–300</td><td>3–30</td><td>Early leak; needs BP, diabetes & kidney-protective plan</td></tr>
                <tr><td><strong>A3</strong> (severely increased)</td><td>&gt; 300</td><td>&gt; 30</td><td>Higher risk; specialist input often helpful</td></tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Confirm persistent albuminuria with repeat testing over 3 months unless levels are very high.</p>

      <!-- 4 Causes -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Common causes: temporary vs persistent <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Temporary (often settle)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Fever, infection, dehydration</li>
                <li>Vigorous exercise within 24–48 hours</li>
                <li>UTI, menstrual contamination of sample</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Persistent (monitor/treated)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Diabetes-related kidney disease</li>
                <li>Hypertension (long-term high BP)</li>
                <li>CKD from other causes (immune, genetic, prior injury)</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Good news:</strong> Early treatment can reduce albumin leak and protect kidneys and heart over time.
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 5 Treatment basics -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Treatment basics (protect your filters) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Medicines often used</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>ACE inhibitors / ARBs:</strong> lower pressure inside kidney filters and reduce albumin leak. A small creatinine bump can be expected at start—usually monitored.</li>
                <li><strong>SGLT2 inhibitors:</strong> in diabetes or CKD, can reduce albuminuria and slow decline in kidney function.</li>
                <li>Statins & BP medicines as needed for cardiovascular protection.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Daily habits that help</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Blood pressure:</strong> follow your personalized target; home checks help.</li>
                <li><strong>Salt:</strong> “taste first, then season.” Swap herbs/lemon for some salt.</li>
                <li><strong>Diabetes:</strong> aim for steady A1C (as advised); short post-meal walks.</li>
                <li><strong>NSAIDs caution:</strong> ask before taking ibuprofen/naproxen.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>

      <!-- 6 When to repeat & track -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> When to repeat tests & how to track <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Confirm persistence:</strong> repeat ACR over weeks to months (often 2 of 3 positive over ≥3 months).</li>
            <li><strong>After illness/exercise:</strong> wait until fully recovered and hydrated before retesting.</li>
            <li><strong>Track alongside eGFR:</strong> changes together guide treatment intensity.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 7 Red flags -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Red flags — seek care promptly <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="note warn">
            <ul>
              <li>Foamy urine with <strong>swelling</strong> of legs/face or sudden weight gain</li>
              <li><strong>Blood in urine</strong>, fever, severe flank or back pain</li>
              <li>Very high ACR with rising creatinine, or you feel unwell</li>
            </ul>
            <p><strong>Emergency symptoms</strong> (chest pain, severe breathlessness, confusion) — call emergency services.</p>
          </div>

      </details>

      <!-- 8 Special notes for seniors -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Special notes for older adults (65+) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Hydration:</strong> small sips through the day help avoid dehydration bumps; ask if you’re on fluid limits.</li>
            <li><strong>Falls/dizziness:</strong> report light-headedness when starting or increasing BP medicines.</li>
            <li><strong>Polypharmacy:</strong> bring a complete medicine + supplement list to visits.</li>
          </ul>

        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Prepare for your appointment + FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <h3>Prepare</h3>
          <ul>
            <li>Bring <strong>medication list</strong> (include OTC pain pills, supplements).</li>
            <li>Have <strong>recent labs</strong> (ACR, eGFR, creatinine) and when you were last ill/exercised hard.</li>
            <li>Ask: “Do I need ACE/ARB or SGLT2? What’s my BP target? When should I recheck ACR?”</li>
          </ul>

          <h3>FAQs</h3>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h4 itemprop="name">Can proteinuria be reversed?</h4>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Yes—if caused by temporary triggers (illness, dehydration, heavy exercise), it often settles. Persistent albuminuria can improve with BP control, diabetes care, ACE/ARB or SGLT2, and salt awareness.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h4 itemprop="name">Do I need a nephrologist?</h4>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Consider referral for A3 levels, rapid change, uncertain cause, or CKD stage 3b+; your primary clinician will guide timing.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h4 itemprop="name">Is the dipstick enough?</h4>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Dipsticks are a screen. ACR is the preferred test to confirm and monitor small leaks.</p>

    const span=document.getElementById('acrValOut');
    const FACTOR=8.84; // mg/mmol -> mg/g

    function convert(){
      const x=parseFloat(v.value);
      if(!isFinite(x)||x<0){ out.style.display='none'; return; }
      let res,label;
      if(from.value==='mgg' && to.value==='mgmmol'){ res=x/FACTOR; label=res.toFixed(2)+' mg/mmol'; }
      else if(from.value==='mgmmol' && to.value==='mgg'){ res=x*FACTOR; label=res.toFixed(0)+' mg/g'; }
      else { res=x; label=x+' '+(to.value==='mgg'?'mg/g':'mg/mmol'); }
      span.textContent='Converted: '+label;
      out.style.display='flex';
    }
    go && go.addEventListener('click', convert);
  })();
