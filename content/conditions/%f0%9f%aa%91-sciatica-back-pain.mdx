      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> Overview: sciatica vs. simple back strain <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>Sciatica</strong> means irritation of the sciatic nerve, usually from a disc bulge or spinal narrowing. Pain can shoot from the low back through the buttock and down the leg, sometimes with numbness or tingling. <strong>Simple back strain</strong> is soreness in the muscles or ligaments of the back without nerve symptoms.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Typical sciatica signs:</strong> sharp or burning pain down one leg, worse when sitting or coughing; possible foot/toe weakness.</li>
            <li><strong>Back strain signs:</strong> dull ache across lower back, stiff after rest, improves with gentle movement.</li>
          </ul>

        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Red flags: when to seek urgent care <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Red flags vs routine care">
            <table>
              <thead><tr><th>Get urgent care now</th><th>Routine (call clinician)</th></tr></thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr><td>New bowel/bladder loss or numbness in the “saddle” area</td><td>Pain >4–6 weeks despite self-care</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Severe weakness in foot or leg (foot drop)</td><td>Fever, weight loss, or pain at night</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Back pain after a fall or accident</td><td>History of cancer, osteoporosis, or long-term steroids with new pain</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Unbearable, worsening pain with illness signs</td><td>Recurring flares affecting daily life</td></tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
          <div class="note warn" style="margin-top:10px"><strong>Emergency symptoms (rare):</strong> sudden loss of bladder/bowel control, progressive leg weakness, or numbness in the groin area — go to emergency care.</div>

      <!-- 3 First 1–2 weeks -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> First 1–2 weeks: safe self-care <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Movement</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Short, frequent walks (3–10 minutes, several times daily).</li>
                <li>Avoid long bed rest; change positions every 30–45 minutes.</li>
                <li>Try gentle pelvic tilts and knee-to-chest (one leg at a time) if comfortable.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Comfort</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Ice or heat 10–15 minutes to the sore area (whichever feels better).</li>
                <li>Pillow between knees when side-lying; under knees when on your back.</li>
                <li>Use a small rolled towel at the low back when sitting.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">If pain steadily worsens or leg weakness appears, contact your clinician sooner.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 4 Gentle movement plan -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Gentle movement plan (step-by-step) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Daily (5–10 min)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Paced walking on flat ground.</li>
                <li>Ankle pumps, gentle hamstring stretch (towel behind thigh).</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Strength 3×/wk</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Chair sit-to-stands (2×8–10 reps).</li>
                <li>Abdominal bracing: breathe out, gently tighten belly for 5–8 sec.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Nerve glide (if advised)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Seated: slowly extend knee and flex/point ankle; stay below pain.</li>
                <li>Stop if sharp symptoms shoot past the knee.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 5 Posture & setups -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Posture & setups: chair, desk, and car <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Chair</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Feet flat; hips/knees ~90°. Use a lumbar roll at belt line.</li>
                <li>Sit back to the chair; avoid slouching on the edge.</li>
              </ul>
              <h3>Desk</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Screen top near eye level; keyboard close; elbows ~90°.</li>
                <li>Stand and stretch every 30–45 minutes.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Car</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Seat closer with slight recline; small pillow behind low back.</li>
                <li>Pause every 45–60 minutes to walk briefly.</li>
              </ul>
              <div class="note tip"><strong>Rule of thirds:</strong> alternate sitting, standing, and light walking across the hour.</div>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 6 Sleep positions -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Sleep positions that calm the back <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Side-lying:</strong> pillow between knees, small towel at waist gap.</li>
            <li><strong>Back-lying:</strong> pillow under knees to relax the spine.</li>
            <li>Avoid deep twisting when turning; move shoulders and hips together.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 7 Medicines -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Pain relief: medicines (plain guidance) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Acetaminophen (paracetamol):</strong> often first choice; follow total daily limit on the label/clinician advice.</li>
            <li><strong>NSAIDs</strong> (e.g., ibuprofen, naproxen): can help but may affect stomach, kidneys, and blood pressure—ask if safe for you.</li>
            <li><strong>Topicals:</strong> diclofenac gel, lidocaine patches for focal areas.</li>
            <li><strong>Nerve pain options (by prescription):</strong> may include certain antidepressants or anticonvulsants if sciatica persists.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note warn"><strong>Safety:</strong> Avoid combining multiple pain medicines without guidance. If on blood thinners, have kidney disease, or stomach ulcers, consult before NSAIDs.</div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 Imaging & tests -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Imaging: do you need an X-ray or MRI? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Most new back pain</strong> improves in a few weeks without imaging.</li>
            <li><strong>MRI</strong> is considered if strong leg weakness, red flags, or pain doesn’t improve after conservative care.</li>
            <li><strong>X-ray</strong> checks for fractures if trauma, osteoporosis, or age-related risk.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 9 Procedures & surgery -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Procedures & surgery (who might benefit) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Epidural steroid injection:</strong> can reduce nerve inflammation from disc bulge or stenosis; relief varies.</li>
            <li><strong>Microdiscectomy:</strong> may help a confirmed disc herniation with persistent leg pain/weakness.</li>
            <li><strong>Laminectomy:</strong> for spinal stenosis when walking distance is limited by leg pain/numbness.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 10 Prevention -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> Prevention & long-term back care <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Keep walks regular; add light strength for hips, core, and legs.</li>
            <li>Lift with legs close to the body; avoid twisting while carrying.</li>
            <li>Maintain a healthy weight and stop smoking if applicable.</li>
            <li>Treat osteoporosis if present to reduce fracture risk.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 11 Caregivers -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">11</span> Caregivers: helpful support ideas <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Set up a comfortable chair with lumbar support and a footrest if feet dangle.</li>
            <li>Place commonly used items at waist level to avoid bending/twisting.</li>
            <li>Walk together after meals; celebrate gradual progress instead of big leaps.</li>
          </ul>

        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">How long does sciatica usually last?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Many flares improve within 2–6 weeks with movement and symptom care. Some cases take longer or need targeted therapy or procedures.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Should I avoid all bending?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">You don’t have to avoid normal life. Bend by hinging at the hips with a straight back, keep items close, and avoid twisting while lifting.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Do I need an MRI right away?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">No, unless red flags are present. Imaging is considered if symptoms persist, worsen, or there’s significant weakness.</p>
