  <section id="what-is-afib" class="section">
    <h2>What is AFib?</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> AFib is an <strong>irregular heartbeat</strong> (arrhythmia) that starts in the top chambers of the heart (atria). It can lead to blood clots and increases the risk of <strong>stroke</strong> and heart failure if not managed.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> AFib is common in older adults. It’s linked with an estimated <strong>five-fold higher stroke risk</strong>; many people don’t notice symptoms at first.</p>
      <p><strong>Types you might hear:</strong> paroxysmal (comes and goes), persistent, long-standing persistent, or permanent. Your plan may differ by type.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>New or worsening <strong>chest pain</strong> or pressure</li>
        <li><strong>Shortness of breath</strong> at rest, or trouble speaking</li>
        <li>Fainting, confusion, <strong>weakness on one side</strong>, face droop, or trouble speaking (possible stroke)</li>
        <li>Very fast or irregular pulse with dizziness that doesn’t settle</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can be emergencies. Call your local emergency number right away.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Symptoms -->
  <section id="symptoms" class="section">
    <h2>Common symptoms</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Heart <strong>palpitations</strong> (fluttering, racing, skipping)</li>
          <li><strong>Fatigue</strong> or reduced exercise ability</li>
          <li><strong>Shortness of breath</strong>, especially with activity or when lying down</li>
          <li>Dizziness or light-headedness, sometimes fainting</li>
          <li>Chest discomfort</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="small">Some people have <strong>no symptoms</strong> (silent AFib).</p>
      </div>

    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Risk</th><th scope="col">What it means</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Stroke</strong></td>
          <td>Blood clots can form in the atria and travel to the brain. AFib is linked with about a <strong>5× higher stroke risk</strong>.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Heart failure</strong></td>
          <td>The heart may not pump as well over time, especially with fast rates.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Quality-of-life issues</strong></td>
          <td>Fatigue, breathlessness, anxiety about palpitations; some have no symptoms yet remain at risk.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Shared risk factors</strong></td>
          <td>Age, high blood pressure, diabetes, obesity, sleep apnea, thyroid problems.</td>

  <!-- Diagnosis -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>How AFib is diagnosed</h2>
    <div class="card">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Physical exam & history</strong> (pulse, blood pressure, symptoms)</li>
        <li><strong>ECG</strong> (electrocardiogram) — confirms irregular rhythm</li>
        <li><strong>Monitoring</strong> (Holter, patch, or wearable) if AFib comes and goes</li>
        <li><strong>Blood tests</strong> (thyroid, electrolytes), <strong>echocardiogram</strong> for structure/function</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Your clinician chooses tests based on symptoms and risk.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatments -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment options (overview)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">1) Lower stroke risk</p>
        <p>Many people with AFib benefit from <strong>blood thinners (anticoagulants)</strong>. Clinicians use stroke-risk scores (like <em>CHA<sup>2</sup>DS<sup>2</sup>-VASc</em>) and bleeding-risk tools to decide. Never start/stop these medicines without medical advice.</p>
        <a class="cta-ghost" href="#">See Health Calculators</a>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">2) Control heart rate</p>
        <p>Medicines (e.g., beta-blockers or rate-limiting calcium-channel blockers) often slow the heart so you feel better and protect the heart muscle.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">3) Restore/keep normal rhythm</p>
        <p>Options include cardioversion (a controlled shock), anti-arrhythmic medicines, or <strong>catheter ablation</strong>. Your team will weigh benefits/risks and your preferences.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">4) Treat the causes</p>
        <p>Manage blood pressure, diabetes, weight, sleep apnea; limit alcohol; review thyroid and other triggers. These steps reduce AFib burden and improve outcomes.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small note" style="margin-top:12px">This page is educational and not a substitute for medical care. Plans are individualized.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Daily care -->
  <section id="daily-care" class="section">
    <h2>Daily care: small steps that help</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Movement</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Most seniors benefit from <strong>gentle daily movement</strong> (walking, cycling, tai chi). Build up slowly.</li>
          <li>Stop and seek care if you get chest pain, severe breathlessness, or feel faint.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Caffeine & alcohol</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Some people notice palpitations with excess caffeine or energy drinks — consider cutting back.</li>
          <li>Alcohol can trigger AFib; many guidelines suggest limiting or avoiding.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Sleep & stress</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Good sleep, regular bedtime, and treating <strong>sleep apnea</strong> can reduce AFib burden.</li>
          <li>Try calm breathing or short walks after meals.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Salt, fluids & weight</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Heart-healthy eating (more plants, less salt) supports blood pressure and swelling control.</li>
          <li>If your clinician set fluid/salt targets (heart failure, kidney issues), follow those.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>

    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Questions -->
  <section id="questions" class="section">
    <h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>
    <div class="card">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>What is my <strong>stroke risk</strong> and do I need a blood thinner?</li>
        <li>Is <strong>rate control</strong> or <strong>rhythm control</strong> better for me right now?</li>
        <li>Could I be a candidate for <strong>ablation</strong> or cardioversion?</li>
        <li>Do I need tests for <strong>sleep apnea</strong> or thyroid problems?</li>
        <li>Which symptoms mean I should call urgently?</li>
        <li>How often should I check my <strong>blood pressure</strong> and pulse at home?</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>AFib — quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can AFib go away?</p>
        <p>Some episodes stop on their own, especially in paroxysmal AFib. Even when you feel normal, stroke risk can remain—keep to your plan.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How is stroke risk estimated?</p>
        <p>Clinicians often use <em>CHA<sup>2</sup>DS<sup>2</sup>-VASc</em>. Higher scores usually mean stronger need for anticoagulation.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is AFib life-threatening?</p>
        <p>AFib increases risks of stroke and heart failure but those risks can be <strong>greatly reduced</strong> with treatment and healthy routines.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do wearables help?</p>
        <p>Home devices and some watches can detect irregular pulse and prompt medical checks. Always confirm with clinical testing.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
