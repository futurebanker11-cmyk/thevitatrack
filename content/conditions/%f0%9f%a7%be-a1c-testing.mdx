      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> A1C in plain words <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>A1C</strong> is a lab test showing your average blood sugar over the last ~3 months. It looks at sugar attached to red blood cells. It’s steady—so one day of “good” or “bad” eating doesn’t move it much. Your personal target depends on age, other conditions, and safety concerns like dizziness or low sugars.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Higher A1C → higher average glucose</strong> and higher risk for eye, kidney, nerve, and heart problems over time.</li>
            <li><strong>Lowering A1C safely</strong> comes from repeatable routines: food pattern, short post-meal walks, better sleep, and medicines when needed.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 4 How often to test -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> How often should I test? (A1C & home checks) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>A1C:</strong> usually every 3–6 months; sooner if treatment changes or numbers are far from goal.</li>
            <li><strong>Home finger-stick (if advised):</strong> before breakfast and 1–2 hours after meals, a few days each month, to learn patterns. Focus on <em>repeatable meals</em> and portion sizes.</li>
            <li><strong>Continuous Glucose Monitor (CGM):</strong> some seniors benefit (trend view, alarms). Ask your clinician about fit, training, and insurance.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note tip"><strong>Use testing to learn—not to worry.</strong> Compare similar meals and note what keeps your after-meal reading steadier.</div>

      <!-- 5 Targets -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Targets are personal (safety first) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Targets vary by age, fall risk, heart/kidney health, and medicines. Many clinicians aim for an A1C that lowers risk yet avoids lows or dizziness. If you ever have <strong>low sugars</strong> (shaking, sweating, confusion), call your clinician—goals or meds may need adjusting.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 6 Why A1C can mislead -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> When A1C can be off (and what to do) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Recent blood loss, anemia, kidney disease, or certain conditions</strong> can make A1C less accurate.</li>
            <li>If A1C and finger-stick/CGM don’t match, your clinician may use <strong>fructosamine</strong> or rely more on home patterns.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note">Bring your meter/CGM data and meal notes. Matching the story to the number helps guide treatment.</div>

      <!-- 7 How to test at home well -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> How to test at home (simple routine) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ol>
            <li>Wash hands with warm water and dry (no food/sugar residue).</li>
            <li>Use a fresh strip; lance the side of the fingertip (less sore).</li>
            <li>Record time, reading, and a short note (meal, walk, meds).</li>
            <li>For learning, check <strong>before</strong> a meal and <strong>1–2 hours after</strong> the first bite.</li>
          </ol>

      <!-- 8 Improving A1C -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> How to lower A1C safely (habits that work) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Plate Method</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Half plate veggies; quarter protein; quarter smart carbs</li>
                <li>Limit sugary drinks; choose water/tea</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Move after meals</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Walk 5–10 minutes after eating</li>
                <li>Light strength 2–3×/week; practice balance safely</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Sleep & meds</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Regular bedtime; cool, dark room</li>
                <li>Take medicines as prescribed; report side effects</li>
              </ul>
            </div>

      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Medicines & monitoring <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Your prescriber chooses medicines based on A1C, heart/kidney health, weight, and safety. Common options include metformin and, when appropriate, other agents. Never start/stop on your own—ask about timing, side effects, and how to monitor.</p>
