  <!-- What it is -->
  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What it is</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> Shingles is a <strong>reactivation</strong> of the varicella-zoster virus (the same virus that causes chickenpox), leading to a <strong>painful, blistering rash</strong> usually on one side of the body or face. Age and weaker immunity raise the risk and severity.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Good to know:</strong> Starting <strong>antivirals within 72 hours</strong> of rash onset shortens illness and may reduce nerve pain afterwards.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>Urgent red-flags</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Rash or pain on the <strong>face, nose, or around the eye</strong> (can threaten vision)</li>
        <li><strong>Ear pain</strong> with facial weakness or hearing changes (possible Ramsay Hunt)</li>
        <li>Severe headache, confusion, weakness, or neck stiffness</li>
        <li>Spreading redness, pus, fever, or rapidly worsening pain (possible infection)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Seek urgent care—eye and ear involvement are emergencies.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Symptoms & phases -->
  <section id="symptoms" class="section">
    <h2>Symptoms & phases</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Prodrome (1–3 days before rash)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Burning, tingling, or sharp pain in a band on one side</li>
          <li>Skin sensitivity, mild fever, headache, or fatigue</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Rash phase (7–10 days)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Red patches → clusters of fluid-filled blisters on one side</li>
          <li>Blisters crust over in 7–10 days; pain can be significant</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Healing & nerve pain</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Crusts fall off over 2–4 weeks</li>
          <li>Some develop <strong>postherpetic neuralgia (PHN)</strong>—nerve pain that lasts after the rash clears</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Risk factors:</strong> older age, weakened immunity, stress/illness, certain medicines (steroids, chemo), prior shingles.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Contagious -->
  <section id="contagious" class="section">
    <h2>Am I contagious?</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Shingles can spread the <strong>chickenpox virus</strong> to someone who has never had chickenpox or the vaccine—through direct contact with blister fluid.</li>
          <li>Cover the rash; avoid touching/scratching; wash hands often.</li>
          <li>Avoid close contact with <strong>pregnant people, newborns, and people with weak immunity</strong> until all blisters have crusted.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Not contagious</strong> before the rash appears or after all lesions are fully crusted and dry.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment (timing matters)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Antivirals (start ASAP)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Valacyclovir, acyclovir, or famciclovir</strong> ideally within 72 hours of rash onset</li>
          <li>May still help later if new blisters are appearing or if eye/ear is involved</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Pain control</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Cool compresses, acetaminophen/NSAIDs if appropriate</li>
          <li>For stronger pain: consider clinician-guided options (e.g., <strong>gabapentin/pregabalin</strong>, short course of certain agents)</li>
          <li>Topicals: <strong>lidocaine</strong> gel/patch after crusting; avoid irritating creams on open blisters</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Steroids:</strong> Sometimes used <em>with</em> antivirals for severe pain/nerve inflammation; must be clinician-directed (not for everyone).
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When to recheck</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>No improvement in 48–72 hours on antivirals</li>
          <li>New spreading redness, pus, fever, or confusion</li>
          <li>Any face/eye/ear symptoms—same-day care</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Rash care -->
  <section id="rash-care" class="section">
    <h2>Rash & skin care</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Keep rash <strong>clean, dry, and covered</strong>; loose cotton clothing</li>
          <li>Cool wet compresses 15–20 minutes a few times daily</li>
          <li>Non-stick dressings if blisters weep; change gently</li>
          <li>Avoid thick ointments on fresh blisters; after crusting, light moisturizers can ease itch</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

  <!-- PHN -->
  <section id="phn" class="section">
    <h2>Postherpetic neuralgia (PHN)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What it is</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Nerve pain that persists for ≥90 days after the rash</li>
          <li>More common with older age and severe acute pain</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Options that help</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Gabapentin / pregabalin</strong> (dose cautiously; watch for dizziness/sedation)</li>
          <li><strong>Duloxetine</strong> or certain <strong>TCAs</strong> (e.g., nortriptyline at night) when appropriate</li>
          <li><strong>Topical 5% lidocaine</strong> patches; capsaicin patch/cream in select cases</li>
          <li>Sleep routine, gentle movement, stress reduction techniques</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Eye & ear -->
  <section id="eyes-ears" class="section">
    <h2>Eye & ear involvement</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card danger">
        <p class="kicker">Ophthalmic shingles</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Rash on the forehead, nose tip, eyelid, or around the eye</li>
          <li>Eye pain, redness, blurred vision, or light sensitivity</li>
          <li><strong>Urgent same-day eye care</strong> to protect vision</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card danger">
        <p class="kicker">Ramsay Hunt syndrome (ear)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Ear pain/rash with <strong>facial weakness</strong>, taste loss, or hearing changes</li>
          <li>Needs prompt specialist care for best recovery</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Prevention -->
  <section id="prevention" class="section">
    <h2>Prevention (Shingrix)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Shingrix</strong> (recombinant zoster vaccine) is recommended for most adults <strong>50+</strong>, including many who previously had shingles.</li>
          <li>Two doses, 2–6 months apart. Ask your clinician about timing after an acute episode.</li>
          <li>Helps prevent shingles and <strong>postherpetic neuralgia</strong>.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>After exposure:</strong> Keep rash covered and avoid high-risk contacts until crusted. Hand hygiene lowers spread.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How long am I contagious?</p>
        <p>From the start of blisters until they all crust and dry. Keep them covered and avoid high-risk contacts during this period.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can shingles happen more than once?</p>
        <p>Yes. Recurrence is possible. Vaccination reduces the chance and severity of future episodes.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do antivirals help after 72 hours?</p>
        <p>They work best early, but may still help if new blisters are appearing or if the face/eye/ear is involved—seek care.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Which pain treatment is safest for me?</p>
        <p>It depends on your health and medicines. Options include acetaminophen/NSAIDs (if safe), gabapentin/pregabalin, duloxetine, TCAs, and topical lidocaine. Start low, go slow.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
