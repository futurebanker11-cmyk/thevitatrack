        <div class="content">
          <div class="quickfacts">
            <div><strong>LDL (“bad”):</strong> carries cholesterol that can build plaque in arteries. Lower is usually better.</div>
            <div><strong>HDL (“good”):</strong> helps carry cholesterol away. Higher is generally better.</div>
            <div><strong>Triglycerides:</strong> a type of fat from extra calories/sugars; high levels raise risk.</div>
            <div><strong>Goals are personal:</strong> diabetes, kidney disease, prior heart/brain events, and age change targets.</div>

        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Plate pattern</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Half plate veggies/fruit,</strong> quarter lean protein (fish, beans, poultry), quarter whole grains.</li>
                <li><strong>Fiber helps LDL:</strong> oats, barley, beans, lentils, apples, berries.</li>
                <li>Swap butter for <strong>olive or canola oil</strong>.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Smart swaps</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Choose <strong>fish 2×/week</strong> (salmon, sardines) for omega-3s.</li>
                <li>Unsalted nuts (handful), seeds, avocado for healthy fats.</li>
                <li>Pick low-sodium sauces; watch processed meats and bakery items.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Move & routine</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Walk 10–20 min</strong> after meals if able.</li>
                <li>Simple strength: chair stands, wall push-ups, hand-grip squeezes.</li>
                <li>Consistent sleep supports healthier choices and lipids.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 5 Medications -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Medications (statins & alternatives) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Most people with higher cardiovascular risk benefit from medication <em>plus</em> lifestyle changes. Talk to your prescriber about the mix that fits your risk and preferences.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Statins</strong> (e.g., atorvastatin, rosuvastatin): lower LDL; proven to reduce heart attack/stroke risk.</li>
            <li><strong>Ezetimibe</strong>: lowers LDL by reducing absorption; sometimes added to a statin or used if statins not tolerated.</li>
            <li><strong>PCSK9 inhibitors</strong> (injections): powerful LDL lowering for very high risk or familial hypercholesterolemia.</li>
            <li><strong>Triglyceride therapies</strong> (omega-3 Rx, fibrates) if triglycerides are very high; goal is to reduce pancreatitis risk.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note"><strong>Side effects?</strong> Muscle aches, weakness, or new symptoms—call your clinician. Never stop suddenly without advice.</div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 6 Monitoring -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Monitoring & follow-up (how often to check) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>After starting or changing meds:</strong> recheck lipids in ~6–12 weeks, then every 3–12 months per plan.</li>
            <li>Ask about <strong>non-fasting vs fasting</strong> labs and whether to hold any meds before tests.</li>
            <li>Track weight/waist, blood pressure, and activity—these support cholesterol control.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 7 Special situations -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Special notes (65+, diabetes, kidney, heart disease) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Age 65+:</strong> emphasize safety, simple routines, and drug-interaction checks.</li>
            <li><strong>Diabetes:</strong> LDL goals are often lower; pair with A1C and BP targets.</li>
            <li><strong>Chronic kidney disease:</strong> confirm medicine choices/doses and potassium interactions.</li>
            <li><strong>Prior heart attack/stroke/stent:</strong> intensive LDL lowering is typically recommended—ask for your number.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 Myths -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Common myths <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>“Thin people can’t have high cholesterol.”</strong> Genetics and diet still matter.</li>
            <li><strong>“If I take a statin, food doesn’t matter.”</strong> Lifestyle + meds protects best.</li>
            <li><strong>“Eggs are always bad.”</strong> Context matters; overall pattern is more important than a single food.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>

              <p itemprop="text">Targets vary. If you’ve had heart/brain events or have diabetes/kidney disease, your clinician may set a lower LDL goal. Ask for your personal target.</p>

              <p itemprop="text">Many panels are accurate without fasting, but some clinics still prefer fasting. Follow your lab’s instructions.</p>
