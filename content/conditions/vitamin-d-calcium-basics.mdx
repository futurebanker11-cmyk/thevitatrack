  <!-- Why they matter -->
  <section id="why-matter" class="section">
    <h2>Why they matter</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Vitamin D</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Helps absorb calcium and supports <strong>bone strength</strong></li>
          <li>Important for <strong>muscle function</strong> and balance (fall prevention)</li>
          <li>Supports immune and overall health</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Calcium</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Builds and maintains bones/teeth</li>
          <li>Needed for <strong>muscle contraction</strong> and normal heart rhythm</li>
          <li>Too little → bone loss; too much from pills → kidney stone risk in some</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Targets -->
  <section id="targets" class="section">
    <h2>Daily targets (ages 50+)</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Nutrient</th><th scope="col">Typical target</th><th scope="col">Upper limit (avoid regularly)</th><th scope="col">Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Vitamin D</strong> (cholecalciferol)</td>
          <td>800–1,000 IU/day (20–25 μg)</td>
          <td>4,000 IU/day (100 μg)</td>
          <td>Personalize to blood level (25-OH D). Some need more; recheck after changes.</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Calcium</strong> (diet + pills)</td>
          <td>Women 50+: 1,200 mg/day; Men 50–70: 1,000 mg/day; Men 71+: 1,200 mg/day</td>
          <td>2,000 mg/day (total)</td>
          <td>Prefer foods first; split supplements (≤600 mg per dose) if needed.</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
    <p class="small">Targets vary with kidney disease, stones, osteoporosis meds, or other conditions—follow your clinician’s plan.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Food sources -->
  <section id="foods" class="section">
    <h2>Best food sources</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Calcium-rich foods (approximate mg/serving)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Dairy: yogurt (250–300), milk (300), cheese (200–300)</li>
          <li>Fortified plant milks/juices (200–350)</li>
          <li>Tofu set with calcium (200–400), canned salmon/sardines with bones (200–325)</li>
          <li>Dark greens (collards, kale, bok choy) (100–250)</li>
          <li>Sesame/tahini, almonds (small amounts add up)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Vitamin D foods</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Fatty fish: salmon, mackerel, sardines</li>
          <li>Egg yolks</li>
          <li>Fortified milk/plant milks, some cereals and yogurts</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <div class="note" style="margin-top:12px">
      <strong>Foods first:</strong> Aim to meet most calcium from food; add a small supplement only if the total falls short.
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Sun -->
  <section id="sun" class="section">
    <h2>Smart sun exposure</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Brief morning or late-afternoon sun on arms/legs can contribute to vitamin D</li>
          <li>Use sun protection to prevent skin damage; darker skin or limited sun often requires dietary D or supplements</li>
          <li>In winter or with minimal sun, most older adults need supplemental vitamin D</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Balance:</strong> target safe, short exposures—never burn.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- ===== In-article AD (after Sun) ===== -->
  

  <!-- Supplements -->
  <section id="supplements" class="section">
    <h2>Supplements: when & how</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Vitamin D (D3)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Common daily doses: 800–2,000 IU; personalize to blood level and season</li>
          <li>Take with or without food; consistency matters</li>
          <li>Recheck 25-OH D about 8–12 weeks after a dose change</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Calcium</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Calculate diet first; supplement only the <strong>gap</strong> to your target</li>
          <li><strong>Calcium carbonate:</strong> take with meals; <strong>calcium citrate:</strong> with or without food (better if on acid-reducing meds)</li>
          <li>Split doses ≤600 mg elemental calcium at a time</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        <strong>Kidney stones or CKD:</strong> discuss exact targets and pill type with your clinician before starting.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Interactions -->
  <section id="interactions" class="section">
    <h2>Medicine interactions & cautions</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Calcium can reduce absorption of <strong>thyroid pills (levothyroxine)</strong>, some <strong>antibiotics</strong>, and <strong>osteoporosis meds</strong>—separate by several hours</li>
          <li>High-dose vitamin D may affect <strong>diuretics</strong> or conditions causing high calcium—use supervised plans</li>
          <li>Antacids or acid-reducing meds may lower calcium carbonate absorption—consider calcium citrate</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        Keep a current <strong>medication/supplement list</strong> and bring it to appointments.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- ===== In-article AD (after Interactions) ===== -->
  

  <!-- Deficiency & toxicity -->
  <section id="deficiency" class="section">
    <h2>Deficiency & toxicity</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Signs of low levels</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Vitamin D: bone pain, muscle weakness, frequent falls, low mood (non-specific)</li>
          <li>Calcium (low blood calcium): cramps, tingling, in severe cases spasms—needs urgent medical evaluation</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Too much (rare but important)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>High vitamin D → high blood calcium: thirst, frequent urination, nausea, confusion</li>
          <li>Excess calcium pills → constipation, kidney stone risk in some</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card danger">
        <strong>Red-flags:</strong> confusion, vomiting, severe weakness, or abnormal heartbeat—seek urgent care.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Testing -->
  <section id="testing" class="section">
    <h2>Testing & follow-up</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Vitamin D status: <strong>25-hydroxyvitamin D</strong> blood test</li>
          <li>Recheck 8–12 weeks after starting or changing dose</li>
          <li>Check kidney function and calcium if taking higher doses or with CKD/stones</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card ok">
        <strong>Targets are individualized:</strong> osteoporosis therapy, kidney disease, or malabsorption often need tailored plans.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Food or pills for calcium?</p>
        <p>Food first. Add only enough supplement to reach your daily target, split into smaller doses.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Best time to take vitamin D?</p>
        <p>Any time you’ll remember. Daily or weekly plans can work—stay consistent and recheck levels.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can I take calcium with my thyroid pill?</p>
        <p>Separate by several hours—calcium blocks absorption of levothyroxine.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Does more vitamin D mean stronger bones?</p>
        <p>More isn’t always better. Target an appropriate blood level and combine with adequate calcium, protein, and movement.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
