  <!-- What is -->
  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What is earwax buildup?</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> Earwax (cerumen) protects the ear canal. Buildup occurs when wax <strong>doesn’t clear out</strong> and blocks the canal, often causing muffled hearing, fullness, itching, or feedback with hearing aids.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> Blocked ears can worsen communication and balance. Removal usually gives immediate relief.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>Urgent warning signs</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Severe pain</strong>, fever, or drainage that smells bad</li>
        <li>Sudden hearing loss, spinning dizziness, or facial weakness</li>
        <li>After head injury: blood/fluid from ear</li>
        <li>Known <strong>eardrum perforation</strong> or ear surgery history</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These need prompt medical assessment.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

    <h2>Common symptoms</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Muffled hearing</strong> or a blocked, “underwater” feeling</li>
          <li><strong>Fullness</strong>, pressure, itching, or mild earache</li>
          <li>Ringing (tinnitus) or hearing aid <strong>feedback/whistling</strong></li>
          <li>Worse after shower or swimming (wax swells with water)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

  <!-- Causes -->
  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Why it happens (older adults)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Wax & skin changes</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Wax gets <strong>drier and thicker</strong> with age</li>
          <li>Slower skin migration outward from the eardrum</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Blockers</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Hearing aids</strong>, earbuds, cotton swabs push wax inward</li>
          <li>Narrow or bendy canals (anatomy), hair in the canal</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Home care -->
  <section id="home" class="section">
    <h2>Safe at-home care</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Softening drops (choose one)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Mineral oil</strong> or <strong>baby oil</strong>: 3–5 drops once daily for 3–5 days</li>
          <li><strong>Carbamide peroxide</strong> OTC: per label (usually 5–10 drops, 1–2×/day up to 4 days)</li>
          <li><strong>Saline</strong> or <strong>glycerin</strong>-based drops if sensitive</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="small">Lie on your side 5–10 minutes after drops to let them work.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Gentle rinse (only if eardrum is normal)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>After softening, a <strong>warm (body-temp) saline</strong> bulb-syringe rinse may help</li>
          <li><strong>Stop</strong> if pain, dizziness, or ringing occurs</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="small">If you have <strong>eardrum perforation, ear tubes, or ear surgery</strong> history, avoid rinsing and see a clinician.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        Many feel relief after a few days of softening drops; stubborn wax may still need clinic removal.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- What not to do -->
  <section id="dont" class="section">
    <h2>What NOT to do</h2>
    <div class="card">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>No <strong>cotton swabs</strong>, keys, hairpins — they push wax deeper and can injure the ear</li>
        <li>Avoid <strong>ear candling</strong> (ineffective, burn/injury risk)</li>
        <li>Don’t use <strong>cold</strong> or very <strong>hot</strong> water (dizziness, injury)</li>
        <li>Don’t rinse if you have ear pain, drainage, prior eardrum issues, or tubes</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Hearing aids -->
  <section id="hearing-aids" class="section">
    <h2>Hearing aids & earwax</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Clean <strong>wax guards</strong> and <strong>domes</strong> regularly (per device guide)</li>
          <li>Power off aids before inserting drops; wipe shells daily</li>
          <li>Consider <strong>monthly</strong> wax checks if you’re prone to blockage</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        Feedback/whistling, sudden muffling, or one aid “going quiet” often means a <strong>wax blockage</strong> — check tips and guards first.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>How clinicians check</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Ear exam</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Otoscopy</strong> to look for wax, infection, or a perforation</li>
          <li>Assessment of canal shape, skin, and eardrum</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Hearing</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Simple office hearing screen; full <strong>audiology</strong> if needed</li>
          <li>Post-removal hearing usually improves immediately if wax was the cause</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Removal options</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Microsuction (clinic)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Wax is removed under direct view with a small suction tube</li>
          <li>Good if you have eardrum issues, diabetes, or narrow canals</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Manual removal</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Loop/curette under magnification; quick for firm wax near the opening</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Irrigation (clinic)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Warm water/saline with controlled pressure; not used if perforation suspected</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        Choice depends on your ear history and exam. Tell the clinician about <strong>ear surgeries</strong>, infections, or perforations.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Prevention -->
  <section id="prevention" class="section">
    <h2>Prevention & upkeep</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>If prone to buildup, use <strong>mineral oil</strong> 2–3 drops weekly after shower</li>
          <li>Schedule a quick <strong>ear check every 6–12 months</strong>, or sooner with hearing aids</li>
          <li>Dry outer ear gently with a towel; do not insert swabs</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card small">
        Related: <a href="#">Hearing Loss</a> · <a href="#">Balance Problems & Falls</a> · <a href="#">Dry Ears vs. Dry Skin</a>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- When to call -->
  <section class="section">
    <h2>When to contact a clinician</h2>
    <div class="card">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>No improvement after 3–5 days of softening drops</li>
        <li>Severe pain, drainage, fever, or sudden hearing change</li>
        <li>Use hearing aids and symptoms recur often</li>
        <li>History of ear surgery, perforation, or frequent infections</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How long to use softening drops?</p>
        <p>Usually 3–5 days. If not improved, book a removal visit.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is olive oil okay?</p>
        <p>Yes for softening in many people. If irritation occurs, switch to mineral oil or saline-based drops.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I need antibiotics?</p>
        <p>Not for simple wax. Antibiotic drops are only for bacterial infections diagnosed by a clinician.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Why does it come back?</p>
        <p>Age-related changes, canal anatomy, and hearing aids push wax inward. Regular maintenance helps prevent blockages.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
