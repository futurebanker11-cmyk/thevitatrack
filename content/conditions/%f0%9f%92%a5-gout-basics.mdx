      <!-- 2 What is gout -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What is gout? Common symptoms <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <p><strong>Gout</strong> happens when uric acid in the blood builds up and forms sharp crystals in a joint—often the big toe, midfoot, ankle, or knee. Flares are sudden, very painful, red, hot, and swollen. Pain is often worst at night and with even a bedsheet touching the joint.</p>
              <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Typical symptoms">
                <table class="responsive">

                    <tr>
                      <td data-col="Phase">Flare</td>
                      <td data-col="What you may notice">Severe pain, warmth, redness, swelling, shiny skin</td>
                    </tr>
                    <tr>
                      <td data-col="Phase">Between flares</td>
                      <td data-col="What you may notice">No pain, but crystals may still be present</td>
                    </tr>
                    <tr>
                      <td data-col="Phase">Chronic</td>
                      <td data-col="What you may notice">Tophi (chalky bumps), joint stiffness/damage</td>
                    </tr>
                  </tbody>
                </table>
              </div>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Kidney link:</strong> High uric acid can also lead to kidney stones and may worsen kidney function. Controlling uric acid protects both joints and kidneys.

      <!-- 3 Flare first aid -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Fast relief during a flare (first 24–48 hours) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ol>
            <li><strong>Rest & raise</strong> the joint (elevation). Avoid weight-bearing if foot/ankle is affected.</li>
            <li><strong>Cold packs</strong> 10–15 minutes at a time, cloth barrier to protect skin.</li>
            <li><strong>Medicines:</strong> Many people use anti-inflammatories, colchicine, or steroids for flares—<em>only</em> as prescribed by your clinician, especially if you have kidney, heart, or stomach issues.</li>
            <li><strong>Hydrate</strong> with water unless on fluid restriction. Limit alcohol fully during flares.</li>
          </ol>

      <!-- 4 Triggers -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Common triggers (fix the root cause) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Diet:</strong> Large portions of red meat, organ meats, some seafood (anchovies, sardines), and beer/spirits</li>
            <li><strong>Alcohol:</strong> Especially beer and binge drinking</li>
            <li><strong>Dehydration</strong> or heavy sugary drinks</li>
            <li><strong>Medications:</strong> Certain water pills (diuretics), low-dose aspirin, cyclosporine—<em>review only with clinician</em></li>
            <li><strong>Illness or surgery</strong>, rapid weight loss, crash diets</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 5 Diet guide -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Diet basics for gout (simple plate, purines, drinks) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Plate method</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>½ plate veggies (non-starchy), ¼ lean protein, ¼ smart carbs</li>
                <li>Choose low-fat dairy, beans in moderate portions, eggs, tofu, fish like salmon</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Hydration & drinks</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Water through the day; coffee and tea are usually okay</li>
                <li>Limit/avoid alcohol, especially beer, during and between flares</li>
                <li>Minimize sugary beverages and high-fructose corn syrup</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Weight & movement</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Gentle, steady weight loss lowers uric acid</li>
                <li>Regular walking supports joints and blood sugar</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 6 Prevention between flares -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Prevention between flares (build your plan) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Know your uric acid goal:</strong> Many clinicians aim for &lt;6 mg/dL (lower if tophi)—confirm your target.</li>
            <li><strong>Take long-term medicines exactly as prescribed</strong> (e.g., allopurinol, febuxostat). <em>Do not stop during a flare unless your clinician tells you to.</em></li>
            <li><strong>Have a flare plan</strong> at home (which anti-inflammatory, dose, and when to call).</li>
            <li><strong>Limit alcohol</strong>, hydrate daily, and pace portions of red meat/seafood.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 7 Medicines overview -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Medicines (talk with your clinician) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>For flares</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Anti-inflammatories, colchicine, or steroids—as prescribed</li>
                <li>Stomach, kidney, heart, and blood thinner interactions matter—<em>never self-dose</em></li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Lowering uric acid</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Allopurinol or febuxostat (long-term)</li>
                <li>May need preventive low-dose medicine during the first months to avoid flare-ups</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Gout or something else? (simple differences) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">

      <!-- 10 Myths -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Myths to ignore <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>“It’s only diet.”</strong> Food matters, but genetics, kidneys, and medicines also drive gout.</li>
            <li><strong>“Stop uric acid pills during a flare.”</strong> Most plans continue long-term medicine—confirm with your clinician.</li>
            <li><strong>“Beer is safer than spirits.”</strong> Beer is a strong gout trigger for many.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>

      <!-- 11 FAQs + Multiplex -->
      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">What uric acid number should I aim for?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Many plans target <strong>below 6 mg/dL</strong> to prevent crystals (lower if tophi). Your clinician will personalize this for you.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Do cherries or vitamin C help?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
