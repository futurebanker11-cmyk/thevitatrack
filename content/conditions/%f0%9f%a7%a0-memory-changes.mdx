          <summary><span class="num">01</span> Overview: What counts as “normal” change?</summary>
          <div class="content">
            <div class="quick">
              <div><strong>Names-on-the-tip-of-the-tongue moments</strong> and <strong>slower recall</strong> can be part of healthy aging.</div>
              <div><strong>Big clues to worry:</strong> getting lost, repeating the same questions rapidly, safety troubles with meds, stoves, or money.</div>
              <div><strong>Good news:</strong> sleep, movement, hearing/vision care, social time, and simple memory tools often make daily life easier.</div>
            </div>
          </div>

        <!-- 02 -->
        <details class="ac">
          <summary><span class="num">02</span> Normal aging vs. concerning signs</summary>
          <div class="content">
            <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Memory comparison (scrolls / card layout on phones)">
              <table class="responsive">
                <thead>
                  <tr><th>Normal aging</th><th>Concerning changes</th></tr>
                </thead>
                <tbody>
                  <tr>
                    <td data-col="Normal aging">Occasional word-finding delay; recall improves with cues.</td>
                    <td data-col="Concerning changes">Repeats the same questions or stories within minutes.</td>
                  </tr>
                  <tr>
                    <td data-col="Normal aging">Misplacing items but retracing steps successfully.</td>
                    <td data-col="Concerning changes">Frequent loss of items with unusual placements (keys in freezer).</td>
                  </tr>
                  <tr>
                    <td data-col="Normal aging">Slower processing in new tech tasks.</td>
                    <td data-col="Concerning changes">Getting lost on familiar routes; missing appointments repeatedly.</td>
                  </tr>
                  <tr>
                    <td data-col="Normal aging">Independent with bills/meds using lists or organizers.</td>
                    <td data-col="Concerning changes">Trouble managing money/meds despite reminders; safety risks.</td>
                  </tr>
                </tbody>
              </table>

            <p class="note" style="margin-top:10px"><strong>If function or safety is affected, get evaluated.</strong> Earlier help means better planning and support.</p>

        <!-- 03 -->
        <details class="ac">
          <summary><span class="num">03</span> When to seek care promptly</summary>
          <div class="content">
            <ul>
              <li>Sudden confusion, slurred speech, face/arm weakness, severe headache (possible stroke) — <strong>emergency care now</strong>.</li>
              <li>Acute confusion with fever, pain on urination, dehydration, or medication changes.</li>
              <li>New hallucinations, major behavior change, or wandering risk.</li>
            </ul>
            <p class="muted">Bring a current medication list and recent BP/sugar logs if available.</p>
          </div>
        </details>

        <!-- 04 -->
        <details class="ac">
          <summary><span class="num">04</span> Daily memory routine (10 gentle habits)</summary>
          <div class="content">
            <ol class="small">
              <li><strong>One notebook or phone notes app</strong> for all to-dos/questions.</li>
              <li><strong>Same-place storage:</strong> a “home” for glasses, keys, wallet, medicines.</li>
              <li><strong>Wall calendar + pill organizer</strong> (AM/PM). Refill weekly.</li>
              <li><strong>Short walks (5–15 min)</strong> after meals for brain blood flow.</li>
              <li><strong>Hearing & vision</strong> up-to-date; use aids consistently.</li>
              <li><strong>Sleep routine:</strong> dim lights, cool room, no big late fluids.</li>
              <li><strong>Brain tasks you enjoy:</strong> reading aloud, crosswords, puzzles, music.</li>
              <li><strong>Social touch:</strong> daily call or chat—connection protects memory.</li>
              <li><strong>Balanced plate:</strong> veggies, beans, fish/eggs; watch added salt/sugar.</li>
              <li><strong>Repeat names + write them</strong> when meeting people.</li>
            </ol>
          </div>
        </details>

        <!-- 05 -->
        <details class="ac">
          <summary><span class="num">05</span> Medical causes to rule out (treatable)</summary>
          <div class="content two">
            <div>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Hearing/vision loss</strong> (brain gets fewer signals).</li>
                <li><strong>Sleep apnea</strong> (poor oxygen/sleep fragments memory).</li>
                <li><strong>Depression/anxiety</strong> (“brain fog”).</li>
                <li><strong>Thyroid, B12, folate, iron issues</strong>.</li>
                <li><strong>Infections</strong> (especially UTIs), dehydration, poorly controlled diabetes or hypertension.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Bring meds & supplements</strong> to visits—some sedatives, strong anticholinergics, and certain pain or sleep pills can worsen memory.
            </div>
          </div>
        </details>

        <!-- 06 -->
        <details class="ac">
          <summary><span class="num">06</span> Evaluation: tests and next steps</summary>
          <div class="content">
            <ul>
              <li>History (timeline, safety issues, mood, sleep, hearing/vision, meds).</li>
              <li>Brief cognitive screen (e.g., word recall, clock draw).</li>
              <li>Blood tests (thyroid, B12, anemia, electrolytes, sugars), urine if infection suspected.</li>
              <li>Imaging only if indicated (sudden changes, neurologic findings).</li>
              <li>Plan: treat reversible causes, set follow-ups, discuss supports.</li>
            </ul>

        <!-- 07 -->
        <details class="ac">
          <summary><span class="num">07</span> Home safety & independence</summary>
          <div class="content three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Kitchen</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Stove auto-shutoff or induction; timers for cooking.</li>
                <li>Label shelves; keep daily items at waist level.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Medications</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Pillbox + phone alarms; list drug name, dose, purpose.</li>
                <li>Keep a printed med list on the fridge.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Wandering & falls</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Door chimes, night lights, clear pathways, no loose rugs.</li>
                <li>ID card/bracelet for those at risk of wandering.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </details>

        <!-- 08 -->
        <details class="ac">
          <summary><span class="num">08</span> Food, movement, sleep</summary>
          <div class="content two">
            <div>
              <h3>Plate ideas</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Half plate veggies/fruit (fresh/frozen), beans, oats/whole grains you tolerate.</li>
                <li>Fish 1–2×/week, nuts/seeds, olive/canola oil; limit salty/ultra-processed foods.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Sleep & stress</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Regular bedtime; cool, dark room; wind-down stretch or music.</li>
                <li>Breathing: slow nose-in, pursed-lip out for 5 minutes.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </details>

        <!-- 09 -->
        <details class="ac">
          <summary><span class="num">09</span> Myths & truths</summary>
          <div class="content">
            <ul>
              <li><strong>“All forgetfulness is dementia.”</strong> Many causes are treatable.</li>
              <li><strong>“Nothing helps.”</strong> Routines, movement, treating hearing/vision, and good sleep help day-to-day function.</li>
              <li><strong>“If tests are normal, it’s not real.”</strong> Memory concerns deserve support even with normal screens.</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
        </details>

              <li>Celebrate small successes—consistency beats perfection.</li>
