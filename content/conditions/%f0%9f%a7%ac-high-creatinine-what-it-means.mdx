      <!-- 1 Overview -->
      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> First things first: What is creatinine? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>Creatinine</strong> is a waste product from muscles. Healthy kidneys filter it into urine. A <strong>higher blood creatinine</strong> generally means the kidneys are filtering less well. But numbers can rise for <em>temporary reasons</em> too—like dehydration, a recent illness, or certain medicines.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Check the trend, not a single test.</strong> Your clinician may repeat labs after hydration or when you’re well.</li>
            <li><strong>eGFR uses creatinine</strong> plus age and sex to estimate kidney filtering; muscle mass and some drugs can change creatinine without true kidney damage.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 3 Common reasons for a rise -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Why creatinine goes up (common reasons) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Temporary / reversible</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Dehydration (vomiting, diarrhea, hot weather, fasting)</li>
                <li>Recent illness or infection</li>
                <li>Short-term effect from starting certain medicines (see next section)</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Kidney stress or injury</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Acute kidney injury (AKI): a sudden rise from illness, low blood flow, toxins</li>
                <li>Chronic kidney disease (CKD): gradual rise over months to years</li>
                <li>Urine blockage (prostate enlargement, stones)</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Lab context</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Very low muscle mass can make creatinine look “normal” even if kidneys are weaker</li>
                <li>High muscle or supplements (e.g., creatine) can raise creatinine</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 4 Medicines that affect creatinine -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Medicines that can raise creatinine <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Never stop a medicine on your own. Some helpful drugs <em>temporarily</em> raise creatinine a little when started; others can harm kidneys if misused.</p>
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>May cause a small bump (often monitored)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>ACE inhibitors or ARBs (used to protect kidneys/heart)</li>
                <li>SGLT2 inhibitors (diabetes/heart-kidney protection)</li>
                <li>Cimetidine, trimethoprim (can raise measured creatinine without true damage)</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note warn">
              <strong>Use with caution / avoid unless advised:</strong>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>NSAIDs pain pills</strong> (e.g., ibuprofen, naproxen) — can reduce kidney blood flow</li>
                <li>Some contrast dyes for scans (need kidney-safe planning)</li>
                <li>High-dose diuretics or dehydration while on diuretics</li>
                <li>Unknown herbal products/supplements</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Your prescriber will decide whether a change is expected and safe or a warning sign.</p>

      <!-- 5 Creatinine, eGFR & albumin -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> How creatinine relates to eGFR & urine albumin <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>eGFR</strong> estimates filtering using creatinine, age, and sex (race-free). Lower eGFR means less filtering.</li>
            <li><strong>Urine albumin (ACR)</strong> shows protein leak. Even with “okay” eGFR, protein in urine can raise kidney/heart risk.</li>
            <li><strong>Risk is a combination</strong> of eGFR, albumin, blood pressure, diabetes control, and symptoms.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 6 What to do now -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> What to do when your creatinine is high <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Right away</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Review the lab report units (mg/dL vs µmol/L) and compare with your last results</li>
                <li>Think about the week before the test: illness, diarrhea, vomiting, heavy exercise, fasting</li>
                <li>List all medicines & OTC pain pills you took</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Discuss with your clinician</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Whether to <strong>repeat labs</strong> after hydration or when recovered</li>
                <li>Check <strong>eGFR and urine ACR</strong> together</li>
                <li>Whether any medicines should be paused/adjusted</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Hydration matters:</strong> Unless you’re on a fluid restriction, sipping fluids through the day can help avoid dehydration-related bumps.
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">If you have heart failure or advanced CKD, ask for a personal fluid/salt plan.</p>

      <!-- 7 Red flags -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Red flags — seek urgent care <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="note warn">
            <p><strong>Call emergency services or seek urgent care</strong> for any of the following:</p>
            <ul>
              <li>Very little or no urine, severe vomiting, confusion, chest pain, or trouble breathing</li>
              <li>New severe swelling, especially with breathlessness</li>
              <li>Creatinine rising quickly with illness or after a dehydration event</li>
            </ul>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 Prepare for your appointment -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Prepare for your appointment <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Bring a <strong>current medication list</strong> (include OTC pain pills and supplements)</li>
            <li>Have your <strong>recent labs</strong> (creatinine, eGFR, ACR) and note any illnesses or dehydration</li>
            <li>Ask: “Should we repeat labs? Which medicines should I adjust? What’s my target BP and fluid/salt plan?”</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 9 FAQs + Multiplex -->
      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Does high creatinine always mean kidney damage?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Not always. Dehydration, temporary illness, and some medicines can raise creatinine without permanent damage. Trends and eGFR/ACR help tell the story.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">How fast should I recheck a high creatinine?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">It depends on how high it is, your symptoms, and the cause. Many clinicians repeat within days to weeks, sooner if you’re unwell.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can protein shakes or creatine supplements affect results?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">They can. Supplements and high muscle activity may raise creatinine levels. Always share supplement use with your clinician.</p>
            </div>

  // Font size toggles
  (function(){
    const html=document.documentElement;
    const normal=document.getElementById('fontNormal');
    const large=document.getElementById('fontLarge');
    function set(size){ html.style.fontSize=size; document.body.style.fontSize=size; }
    normal && normal.addEventListener('click', ()=>set('var(--fs)'));
    large && large.addEventListener('click', ()=>set('var(--fs-lg)'));
  })();

  // Expand/Collapse all (keep first open)
  (function(){
    const openAll=document.getElementById('openAll');
    const closeAll=document.getElementById('closeAll');
    const sections=[...document.querySelectorAll('details.section')];
    openAll && openAll.addEventListener('click', ()=> sections.forEach(d=> d.open=true));
    closeAll && closeAll.addEventListener('click', ()=>{
      sections.forEach((d,i)=> d.open=(i===0));
      sections[0].scrollIntoView({behavior:'smooth', block:'start'});
    });
  })();

  // Creatinine mg/dL ↔ µmol/L converter
  (function(){
    const val=document.getElementById('val');
    const from=document.getElementById('from');
    const to=document.getElementById('to');
    const go=document.getElementById('convGo');
    const out=document.getElementById('convOut');
    const span=document.getElementById('convVal');

    function convert(){
      const x=parseFloat(val.value);
      if(!isFinite(x)||x<=0){ out.style.display='none'; return; }
      let res, label;
      if(from.value==='mgdl' && to.value==='umol'){
        res = x*88.4; label = res.toFixed(0) + ' µmol/L';
      }else if(from.value==='umol' && to.value==='mgdl'){
        res = x/88.4; label = res.toFixed(2) + ' mg/dL';
      }else{
        res = x; label = x + ' ' + (to.value==='mgdl'?'mg/dL':'µmol/L');
      }
      span.textContent = 'Converted: ' + label;
      out.style.display='flex';
    }
    go && go.addEventListener('click', convert);
  })();
</script>
