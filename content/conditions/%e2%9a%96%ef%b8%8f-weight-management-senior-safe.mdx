      <!-- 2 What it is & symptoms -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What is neuropathy? Common symptoms <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <p><strong>Diabetic peripheral neuropathy</strong> is nerve damage from long-term high blood sugar, age, and other risks. It often starts in the toes and moves upward. Many people feel burning, tingling, or numbness—some feel <em>nothing</em> at all, which increases injury risk.</p>
              <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Symptoms by type">

                      <td data-col="Examples">Dry skin, less sweating → cracks, poor healing</td>

            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Loss of protective sensation:</strong> If you can’t feel a pebble, heat, or a tight shoe, small injuries can become dangerous ulcers. Daily checks prevent big problems.

      <!-- 3 Daily 60-second foot check -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> The 60-second daily foot check <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ol>
            <li><strong>Look:</strong> Use a mirror or helper. Check soles, heels, between toes, and around nails.</li>
            <li><strong>Feel:</strong> Any tender spot, swelling, warmth, hard skin (callus), or cracks?</li>
            <li><strong>Skin & nails:</strong> Trim straight across; do not cut corners. Moisturize <em>top and bottom</em> of the foot, <em>not between toes</em>.</li>
            <li><strong>Socks & shoes:</strong> Turn shoes upside down—remove pebbles or seams. Wear clean, soft socks.</li>
            <li><strong>Log & act:</strong> Note changes. If sore/ulcer appears, <strong>offload pressure</strong> and contact a clinician promptly.</li>
          </ol>

      <!-- 4 Pain relief & comfort -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Pain & burning: relief options (talk to your clinician) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Non-drug comfort:</strong> Gentle foot stretches, warm (not hot) soaks, relaxation, regular walking, cushioned insoles, elevation for swelling.</li>
            <li><strong>Topicals:</strong> Moisturizers with urea or lactic acid for dry skin; capsaicin cream/patch (may sting at first). Avoid applying between toes.</li>
            <li><strong>Prescription options:</strong> Certain antidepressants or nerve-pain medicines may help; dosing is individualized. <em>Do not self-start or change doses.</em></li>
            <li><strong>Check B-12 & thyroid</strong> if advised—deficiencies and thyroid problems can mimic/worsen neuropathy.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 5 Socks, shoes, and callus care -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Socks, shoes & callus care (prevent ulcers) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Socks</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Soft, seamless, moisture-wicking; avoid tight elastic bands</li>
                <li>Change daily; keep feet dry; consider padded areas at heel/ball</li>
                <li>Sleep: wear breathable socks if feet are cold; avoid hot water bottles</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Shoes</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Wide toe box, low heel, firm sole, smooth interior—no rubbing</li>
                <li>Break in slowly at home; check feet after 30–60 minutes</li>
                <li>Consider diabetic footwear/orthotics if advised</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Skin & callus</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Do <em>not</em> cut calluses/corns at home; gentle pumice only if taught</li>
                <li>Daily moisturizer; stop if maceration/whitish skin appears</li>
                <li>Never use chemical corn removers—can burn numb skin</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 6 Prevent progression -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Slow progression: whole-body protection <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Blood sugar:</strong> Aim for targets set with your clinician; small daily consistency matters.</li>
            <li><strong>Blood pressure & cholesterol:</strong> Protects circulation; take medicines as prescribed.</li>
            <li><strong>Stop smoking:</strong> Greatly improves blood flow to feet.</li>
            <li><strong>Movement:</strong> Daily walking, ankle pumps, and balance practice help nerves and muscles.</li>
            <li><strong>Yearly foot exam:</strong> Ask for monofilament testing and shoe evaluation; more often if high risk.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 7 Red flags & when to call -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Red flags: when to call your clinician <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>New numb area, sudden swelling, hot spots, or color change</li>
            <li>Any blister, crack, or cut that is not better in 24–48 hours</li>
            <li>Ingrown nail, thick callus, or corn causing pressure</li>
            <li>Shooting pains at night that disrupt sleep despite simple measures</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 8 Special situations -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Special situations (travel, pedicures, exercise) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Travel:</strong> Pack extra socks, bandages, and a mirror. Walk every hour on flights.</li>
            <li><strong>Pedicures:</strong> Choose clean salons, bring your own tools, and ask to avoid cutting cuticles/calluses.</li>
            <li><strong>Exercise:</strong> Start slow; choose cushioned shoes; check feet before and after.</li>
            <li><strong>Hot-cold exposure:</strong> Test bath water with elbow/thermometer; avoid heating pads on numb skin.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 9 Myths -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Myths to ignore <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>“If it doesn’t hurt, it’s fine.”</strong> Numb feet can hide serious injuries—check daily.</li>
            <li><strong>“I can shave off calluses at home.”</strong> Risk of burns and ulcers—seek trained care.</li>
            <li><strong>“Thick socks fix everything.”</strong> Fit matters more; bulky seams can rub skin raw.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 10 Caregiver plan -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Caregiver & family plan <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Help with daily checks if vision, flexibility, or memory is limited.</li>
            <li>Confirm shoes fit and socks are clean and dry; remove pebbles and seams.</li>
            <li>If a wound appears, <strong>offload pressure</strong> (do not walk on it) and call the clinician promptly.</li>
            <li>Keep a simple log with photos to show healing—or worsening.</li>
          </ul>

        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can neuropathy be reversed?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Nerve damage often improves slowly when blood sugar, blood pressure, and cholesterol are well controlled. Pain can be managed, and new injuries can be prevented with daily foot care.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">What socks are best?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Soft, seamless, moisture-wicking socks that do not leave deep marks. Change daily and keep feet dry. Avoid tight bands and thick seams that rub.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">When should I see a podiatrist?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">At least once a year—or sooner for calluses, deformities, toenail problems, prior ulcers, or any new sore. Ask about diabetic shoes and custom inserts if you’re high risk.</p>
