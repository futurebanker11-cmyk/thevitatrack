      <!-- 1 What is AFib -->
      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What is AFib? (plain words) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Simple definition</h3>
              <p><strong>Atrial fibrillation (AFib)</strong> is an irregular, often fast heartbeat that starts in the top chambers of the heart (atria). Signals become chaotic, so the heartbeat loses its steady rhythm.</p>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Paroxysmal:</strong> comes and goes on its own.</li>
                <li><strong>Persistent:</strong> lasts longer and may need treatment to restore rhythm.</li>
                <li><strong>Long-standing / permanent:</strong> rhythm is irregular long term; focus is on safety and comfort.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Why AFib matters:</strong> Blood can pool in a small pouch of the heart (left atrial appendage) and form a clot—this can cause a stroke. Preventing clots is a key goal.
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 2 Symptoms & when to call -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Symptoms & when to call <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Common symptoms</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Heart racing, fluttering, or skipping beats.</li>
                <li>Shortness of breath, fatigue, exercise intolerance.</li>
                <li>Dizziness, light-headedness, or chest discomfort.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Red flags</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Chest pain, fainting, or severe breathlessness.</li>
                <li>Stroke signs: facial droop, arm weakness, slurred speech, sudden confusion.</li>
                <li>Very fast, persistent heart rate (for example, >120) with symptoms.</li>
              </ul>
              <p class="note warn"><strong>Emergency:</strong> Chest pain, stroke signs, or collapse — call emergency services immediately.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 3 Stroke risk (CHA2DS2-VASc) -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Stroke risk score (CHA<sub>2</sub>DS<sub>2</sub>-VASc) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>The score helps decide on blood thinners. Your clinician considers both stroke risk and bleeding risk to personalize treatment.</p>
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="CHA2DS2-VASc scoring">
            <table>
              <thead>

                <tr><td>Age ≥75 years</td><td>2</td></tr>

              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Higher scores mean higher stroke risk and stronger reasons to use anticoagulation if safe.</p>

      <!-- 4 Diagnosis -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> How AFib is diagnosed <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>ECG (electrocardiogram):</strong> confirms AFib pattern.</li>
            <li><strong>Holter/patch monitor:</strong> records rhythm over days to catch episodes.</li>
            <li><strong>Echocardiogram:</strong> checks heart size, valves, and function.</li>
            <li><strong>Blood tests:</strong> thyroid, electrolytes, kidney function; sometimes anemia/iron checks.</li>
            <li><strong>Sleep apnea evaluation</strong> if snoring, poor sleep, or daytime fatigue.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 5 Treatment goals -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Treatment goals: rate, rhythm, & clot prevention <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Control the rate</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Beta-blockers</strong> or certain <strong>calcium channel blockers</strong> (non-DHP) slow the heart.</li>
                <li>Target is a comfortable, safe resting heart rate individualized to you.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Restore/maintain rhythm</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Cardioversion</strong> (reset the rhythm) may be used.</li>
                <li><strong>Anti-arrhythmic medicines</strong> help maintain normal rhythm in selected people.</li>
                <li><strong>Catheter ablation</strong> can reduce AFib burden for many, especially symptomatic cases.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Prevent clots (stroke)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Anticoagulants</strong> (blood thinners) are considered based on CHA<sub>2</sub>DS<sub>2</sub>-VASc and bleeding risk.</li>
                <li>Options include warfarin or direct oral anticoagulants; dosing and monitoring differ.</li>
                <li>For some who cannot take blood thinners, <strong>left atrial appendage occlusion</strong> may be discussed.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 6 Medicines -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Medicines: what to know (plain language) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Common groups</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Rate control:</strong> beta-blockers; non-DHP calcium channel blockers (ask if they’re right for you).</li>
                <li><strong>Rhythm control:</strong> anti-arrhythmics selected by your specialist based on heart structure and other conditions.</li>
                <li><strong>Anticoagulants:</strong> lower stroke risk—take exactly as directed; do not skip doses.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Safety tips</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Tell your clinician about all meds and supplements (interactions happen).</li>
                <li>Report bleeding, black stools, severe bruising, or sudden weakness/numbness.</li>
                <li>Keep lab and follow-up appointments to adjust therapy safely.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 7 Procedures -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Procedures: cardioversion, ablation, & LAA closure <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Cardioversion:</strong> brief, controlled shock to reset rhythm; anticoagulation is managed before/after.</li>
            <li><strong>Catheter ablation:</strong> targets misfiring areas (often near pulmonary veins). Can reduce symptoms and AFib episodes in many.</li>
            <li><strong>Left atrial appendage occlusion (LAAO):</strong> device that seals the pouch where clots can form—considered when long-term anticoagulation isn’t suitable.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 Daily living -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Daily living & triggers <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Habits that help</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Regular, gentle activity (walks after meals; pace yourself).</li>
                <li>Consistent sleep schedule; consider a sleep study if snoring/daytime fatigue.</li>
                <li>Limit alcohol; avoid binges which can trigger AFib.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Possible triggers</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Dehydration, heavy caffeine for some people, illness/fever.</li>
                <li>Unmanaged thyroid problems, very high blood pressure.</li>
                <li>High stress; try brief breathing breaks daily.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Tracking</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Keep a simple log: symptoms, pulse, BP, new meds, alcohol/caffeine.</li>
                <li>Bring your list of questions to each visit.</li>
                <li>Share any wearable rhythm alerts with your clinician.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 9 AFib with other conditions -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> AFib with BP, diabetes, heart failure, or kidney disease <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>High blood pressure:</strong> steady control lowers AFib burden and stroke risk.</li>
            <li><strong>Diabetes:</strong> paired management protects heart and blood vessels.</li>
            <li><strong>Heart failure:</strong> rate control, rhythm strategy, and anticoagulation are coordinated with HF medicines.</li>
            <li><strong>Kidney disease:</strong> dose adjustments and lab checks are essential.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 10 FAQs + Multiplex -->
      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can AFib go away on its own?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Some episodes stop spontaneously, especially paroxysmal AFib. If episodes are frequent or severe, rhythm control and ablation may be discussed.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Do all people with AFib need a blood thinner?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Not always. The decision is based on your stroke risk (CHA<sub>2</sub>DS<sub>2</sub>-VASc) and bleeding risk. Many older adults do benefit—your clinician will personalize the plan.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Is exercise safe with AFib?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Gentle, regular activity is usually encouraged, with pacing and symptom awareness. Get specific limits from your care team.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
