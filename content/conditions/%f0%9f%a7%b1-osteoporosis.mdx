      <!-- 1 Overview -->
      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> Overview: what is osteoporosis? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Osteoporosis means bones have become less dense and more fragile, so they break more easily—often from a minor fall or even a cough/sneeze in the spine. It’s common after age 60 and can be silent until a fracture happens. The good news: daily steps and the right treatment can lower risk.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Common fracture sites:</strong> hip, spine (vertebrae), wrist.</li>
            <li><strong>Who’s at risk:</strong> postmenopausal women, adults 65+, people with prior fractures, long-term steroid use, very low weight, smoking, heavy alcohol, some medical conditions.</li>
          </ul>

      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Symptoms & red flags <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Often silent:</strong> no symptoms until a fracture.</li>
            <li><strong>Spine compression signs:</strong> sudden mid-back pain, loss of height, stooped posture.</li>
            <li><strong>Hip fracture signs:</strong> pain after a fall, leg turned outward/shorter, inability to walk.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note warn"><strong>Urgent care now:</strong> sudden severe back pain after a minor strain, or any suspected hip fracture after a fall.</div>

      <!-- 3 DEXA & T-scores -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> DEXA scan & T-scores (how to read results) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>A DEXA (bone density) scan measures mineral density in the hip and spine. Results are reported as a <strong>T-score</strong> comparing your bone density with that of a healthy young adult.</p>
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="T-score categories">
            <table>
              <thead><tr><th>Category</th><th>T-score</th><th>Plain meaning</th></tr></thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr><td>Normal</td><td>≥ –1.0</td><td>Bone density in expected range</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Osteopenia (low bone mass)</td><td>–1.0 to –2.4</td><td>Below average; elevated fracture risk</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Osteoporosis</td><td>≤ –2.5</td><td>High risk for fractures</td></tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
          <p class="muted small">Clinicians may also use a 10-year fracture risk tool (like FRAX) with your age, weight, prior fractures, and other factors to guide treatment decisions.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 4 Calcium & Vitamin D -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Calcium & vitamin D (daily targets + food list) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Daily goals (general)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Calcium:</strong> ~1,000–1,200 mg/day from food + supplements combined (many adults need the higher end).</li>
                <li><strong>Vitamin D:</strong> often 800–1,000 IU/day; your clinician may adjust based on a blood test.</li>
                <li><strong>Food first:</strong> supplements fill gaps when diet falls short.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Easy sources</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Milk, yogurt, calcium-fortified plant milks, cheese.</li>
                <li>Leafy greens (kale, bok choy), canned fish with bones (sardines, salmon), tofu set with calcium.</li>
                <li>Vitamin D: fortified milks/cereals, egg yolks; sunlight helps, but varies by skin tone, season, and location.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="note tip"><strong>Supplement tips:</strong> Split calcium doses (e.g., 500–600 mg at a time). Calcium carbonate with meals; calcium citrate can be taken with or without food. Avoid taking iron and calcium together—they compete.</div>

      <!-- 5 Exercise for stronger bones -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Exercise plan for bone strength (safe & short) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Weight-bearing (most days)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Brisk walking 20–30 min (can split into 3 × 10).</li>
                <li>Stair stepping or gentle hill walks if balance allows.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Strength (2–3×/week)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Chair sit-to-stands, wall push-ups, light dumbbells or bands.</li>
                <li>Two sets of 8–12 slow reps; rest between sets.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Posture & balance</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Spine-safe core (no deep forward bends or twisting with load).</li>
                <li>Heel-to-toe walk along a counter; one-leg stands 10–20 sec.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">If you’ve had a spine fracture, ask for a physical therapy plan and spine-safe modifications.</p>

        <div class="content">

                <li><strong>Bathroom:</strong> grab bars, non-slip mats, raised toilet seat if needed.</li>
                <li><strong>Footwear:</strong> supportive, non-slip shoes even indoors.</li>
                <li><strong>Vision & hearing:</strong> up-to-date checks improve balance.</li>
              </ul>

      <!-- 7 Medicines -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Medicines (who needs them & options) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Medicines lower fracture risk when bone density is low or risk is high. Your clinician chooses based on your scan, age, prior fractures, kidney function, and preferences.</p>
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Anti-resorptives</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Bisphosphonates</strong> (alendronate weekly, risedronate weekly/monthly, zoledronic acid yearly IV): slow bone breakdown; may cause stomach upset (oral) or flu-like symptoms (IV).</li>
                <li><strong>Denosumab</strong> (injection every 6 months): effective for fracture risk; must plan transition when stopping to avoid rebound bone loss.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Anabolics (build bone)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Teriparatide / Abaloparatide</strong> (daily injections): for very high risk or multiple fractures.</li>
                <li><strong>Romosozumab</strong> (monthly injections for 12 months): builds and preserves bone; not for some with recent heart events.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="note warn"><strong>Safety notes:</strong> Rare risks include jaw osteonecrosis and atypical femur fracture with long-term use—discuss dental care and treatment duration with your clinician.</div>

      <!-- 8 Monitoring -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Monitoring & follow-up (when to repeat DEXA) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>DEXA timing:</strong> often every 1–2 years when starting/changing therapy; sometimes less often when stable.</li>
            <li><strong>Blood tests:</strong> vitamin D, calcium, kidney function as advised; check for secondary causes if bone loss is fast or unexpected.</li>
            <li><strong>Track falls:</strong> any new fall or fracture should prompt a review of your plan.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 9 Secondary causes -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Secondary causes (ask about these) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Thyroid or parathyroid problems.</li>
            <li>Long-term steroids or certain seizure/heartburn medications.</li>
            <li>Digestive conditions (celiac, IBD) that reduce absorption.</li>
            <li>Low sex hormones (men & women), chronic kidney or liver disease, heavy alcohol, smoking.</li>
          </ul>
          <p class="muted small">Treating the underlying cause can improve bone health.</p>

      <!-- 10 After a fracture -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> After a fracture (what to do next) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ol>
            <li><strong>Pain & mobility:</strong> follow the rehab plan; ask about a walker/cane for safety.</li>
            <li><strong>Bone health work-up:</strong> DEXA, labs, and a medicine plan to prevent the next fracture.</li>
            <li><strong>Home safety:</strong> prioritize fall-proofing and balance training; review medications that cause dizziness.</li>
          </ol>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 11 Food pattern -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">11</span> Bone-friendly food pattern (simple plate) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Half plate colorful vegetables/fruit, plus a palm-size lean protein (fish, poultry, beans, tofu), and a fist-size whole grain or starchy veg.</li>
            <li>Include a calcium-rich food at 1–2 meals; use olive/canola oil for cooking.</li>
            <li>Limit heavy alcohol and smoking—both weaken bone.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 12 FAQs + Multiplex -->
      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">12</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Is osteopenia the same as osteoporosis?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">No. Osteopenia is low bone mass (T-score between –1.0 and –2.4). Osteoporosis is ≤ –2.5 or a fragility fracture. Treatment choices depend on overall fracture risk, not the label alone.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can I stop medicine once my scan improves?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Sometimes. Some people take a “drug holiday” after several years on a bisphosphonate if risk becomes lower. This decision is individualized and monitored with repeat scans.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Which calcium is better—carbonate or citrate?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Both work. Carbonate is usually taken with meals; citrate can be taken with or without food and may be better if you have stomach acid issues.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
