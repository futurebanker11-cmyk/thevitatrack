  <section id="what-is-anemia" class="section">
    <h2>What is anemia?</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> Anemia means you don’t have enough healthy <strong>red blood cells</strong> or hemoglobin to carry oxygen. This can make you feel <strong>tired, weak, short of breath,</strong> or dizzy. It’s common in older adults and has many possible causes.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> Treating the <em>cause</em> (for example iron deficiency, vitamin B12 or folate deficiency, kidney disease, bleeding, or inflammation) usually improves energy and daily function.</p>
      <p><strong>Good news:</strong> Most causes are identifiable with simple tests and are treatable once the cause is clear.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to call emergency</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>Chest pain</strong>, pressure, or shortness of breath at rest</li>
        <li><strong>Fainting</strong>, confusion, or severe weakness</li>
        <li>Black or bloody stools, vomiting blood, or very heavy bleeding</li>
        <li>Fast heart rate with dizziness that doesn’t settle</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">These can be emergencies. Call your local emergency number right away.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

          <li>Pale skin, cold hands/feet, fast or irregular heartbeat</li>
          <li>Cravings for ice or non-food items (sometimes in iron deficiency)</li>

  <section id="causes" class="section">
    <h2>Common causes & types</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table">
      <thead>
        <tr><th scope="col">Type</th><th scope="col">What it means</th><th scope="col">Often due to</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Iron-deficiency anemia</strong></td>
          <td>Low iron → low hemoglobin</td>
          <td>Blood loss (GI, periods), low iron intake/absorption</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Vitamin B12 deficiency</strong></td>
          <td>Large RBCs; nerves can be affected</td>
          <td>Poor absorption, low intake, certain meds or conditions</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Folate deficiency</strong></td>
          <td>Large RBCs; often alongside poor intake</td>
          <td>Low dietary folate, alcohol use, absorption issues</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Anemia of chronic disease</strong></td>
          <td>Inflammation changes iron handling</td>
          <td>Chronic conditions (infections, autoimmune, cancer)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Kidney-related anemia</strong></td>
          <td>Low erythropoietin (EPO)</td>
          <td>Chronic kidney disease (CKD)</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Other causes</strong></td>
          <td>Less common but important</td>
          <td>Bone marrow problems, hemolysis, inherited conditions</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>

  <!-- Diagnosis -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>How anemia is diagnosed</h2>
    <div class="card">
      <ul class="list">
        <li><strong>History & exam</strong> (bleeding, diet, medicines, family history)</li>
        <li><strong>CBC</strong> (hemoglobin, hematocrit, MCV)</li>
        <li><strong>Iron studies</strong> (ferritin, iron, TIBC/transferrin)</li>
        <li><strong>B12 & folate</strong> levels; sometimes <strong>reticulocyte</strong> count</li>
        <li>Additional tests if needed (stool blood test, kidney tests, thyroid, inflammatory markers; occasionally bone marrow)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Your clinician chooses tests based on symptoms and risk.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment options (overview)</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">1) Treat the cause</p>
        <p>Stopping bleeding, improving diet/absorption, treating infections or inflammation, and managing kidney or thyroid issues are often key.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">2) Iron therapy</p>
        <p>Oral iron or, in some cases, IV iron may be used if iron deficiency is confirmed. <strong>Take only under medical advice</strong>, as iron isn’t right for every anemia.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">3) Vitamin support</p>
        <p>Vitamin <strong>B12</strong> (pills or injections) and/or <strong>folate</strong> if levels are low. Nerve symptoms from B12 deficiency need timely treatment.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">4) Other options</p>
        <p>For CKD-related anemia, medicines that stimulate red blood cell production may be considered. Transfusion is reserved for specific situations.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
    <p class="small note" style="margin-top:12px">Educational only — treatment is individualized. Always follow your clinician’s plan.</p>
  </section>

  <!-- Daily care -->
  <section id="daily-care" class="section">
    <h2>Daily care: food, iron tips, energy</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Smart plate ideas</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Iron-rich foods:</strong> lean meats, eggs, beans/lentils, spinach, beets, fortified cereals.</li>
          <li>Pair plant iron with <strong>vitamin C</strong> (lemon, tomatoes, oranges) to improve absorption.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">If prescribed iron pills</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Take as directed. Some do better with every-other-day dosing — follow your clinician’s advice.</li>
          <li>Tea/coffee and high-calcium foods can lower absorption if taken together; ask about timing.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Energy & movement</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Gentle walking or stretching most days can help stamina; increase slowly.</li>
          <li>Rest when needed; seek care if you feel worse with activity.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Safety at home</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Stand up slowly to reduce dizziness; use handrails; keep paths well lit.</li>
          <li>Keep a list of medicines and any supplements to review at appointments.</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
    <div class="small" style="margin-top:8px">

  <!-- Questions -->
  <section id="questions" class="section">
    <h2>Questions to ask your doctor</h2>
    <div class="card">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>What type of anemia do I have and what caused it?</li>
        <li>Which tests confirm the cause (iron, B12, folate, kidney, stool blood test)?</li>
        <li>Do I need iron, B12 shots, folate, or something else?</li>
        <li>Could any of my medicines be contributing?</li>
        <li>What diet changes fit my other conditions (heart, kidney, diabetes)?</li>
        <li>When should I re-check my blood counts?</li>
      </ul>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Anemia — quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can anemia cause dizziness?</p>
        <p>Yes. With fewer red blood cells carrying oxygen, you might feel light-headed—especially when standing up quickly.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How is the cause found?</p>
        <p>Simple blood tests (CBC, ferritin/iron studies, B12/folate) and, if needed, tests for bleeding or kidney/thyroid issues.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is iron safe to take?</p>
        <p>Only if iron deficiency is confirmed and your clinician recommends it. Some anemias don’t need iron and too much can be harmful.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How long to feel better?</p>
        <p>It depends on the cause and treatment. Many people improve over weeks; full iron stores can take longer. Follow-up tests track progress.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
