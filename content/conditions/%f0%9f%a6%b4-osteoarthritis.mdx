      <!-- 1 Overview -->
      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> Overview: what is osteoarthritis? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Osteoarthritis (OA) is “wear-and-repair” arthritis. The smooth cartilage that cushions a joint thins over time. Nearby bone and soft tissues react, causing pain, stiffness, swelling, and sometimes grinding or clicking. OA most often affects knees, hips, hands (base of thumb, finger joints), big toe, and spine.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Common signs:</strong> pain with use that eases with rest, morning stiffness &lt; 30–60 minutes, swelling after activity, reduced movement.</li>
            <li><strong>Risk factors:</strong> age, prior joint injury, extra body weight, family tendency, heavy/repetitive joint load, weak muscles.</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 2 Symptoms by joint -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Symptoms — by joint <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Knee & Hip</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Knee: pain climbing stairs, getting up from chair, squatting; occasional giving way; swelling after walks.</li>
                <li>Hip: groin or side-hip ache, pain putting on socks or getting into a car; reduced stride length.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Hands & Spine</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Thumb base (CMC): pinch/grip pain Opening jars, turning keys.</li>
                <li>Fingers: bony knuckles (Heberden/Bouchard nodes), stiffness.</li>
                <li>Spine: stiffness after sitting, low-back ache with standing/walking.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="note tip"><strong>Pattern matters:</strong> OA pain is usually mechanical (worse with use). Constant night pain, fever, hot/red joints, or rapid swelling may suggest other problems—seek care.</div>

      <!-- 3 Diagnosis & imaging -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Diagnosis & imaging (do I need an MRI?) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>OA is a <strong>clinical diagnosis</strong> (symptoms + exam). X-rays can show joint space narrowing, bone spurs, and alignment. <strong>MRI is not usually needed</strong> unless symptoms don’t fit OA, there’s locking/catching, or surgery is being planned.</p>
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Typical tests">
            <table>
              <thead><tr><th>Test</th><th>Why</th><th>Notes</th></tr></thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr><td>Exam</td><td>Pain map, range of motion, gait</td><td>Checks muscle strength & alignment</td></tr>
                <tr><td>X-ray</td><td>Confirm OA changes</td><td>Weight-bearing views matter for knees</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Blood tests</td><td>Rule out other causes</td><td>Used when symptoms atypical</td></tr>
                <tr><td>MRI</td><td>Soft tissue detail</td><td>Consider if locking, suspected tear, or pre-op planning</td></tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
          <p class="muted small">Imaging severity doesn’t always match pain. Treatment focuses on function and comfort.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 4 Daily movement plan -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Daily movement plan (short & gentle) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Everyday</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>🎯 <strong>Goal:</strong> 20–30 min of easy movement daily (can split into 3 × 10 minutes).</li>
                <li>Walk on level ground, cycle on a stationary bike, or try water walking / light swim.</li>
                <li>Keep pain ≤ 4/10 during and after. If higher, shorten and slow.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Strength (2–3×/week)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Chair sit-to-stand, wall push-ups, step-ups (low step), mini-squats holding a counter.</li>
                <li>Two sets of 6–10 reps; rest between sets. Quality over speed.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Mobility & balance</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Knee: heel slides, calf/hamstring stretch.</li>
                <li>Hip: gentle hip flexor and glute stretch.</li>
                <li>Balance: stand near counter—one-leg stance 10–20 sec each side.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>

      <!-- 5 Pain relief options -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Pain relief options (safer first) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Non-drug first line</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Heat for stiffness</strong> (warm shower or heating pad 10–15 min).</li>
                <li><strong>Ice for flare swelling</strong> (10–15 min with cloth barrier).</li>
                <li><strong>Topical NSAIDs</strong> (e.g., diclofenac gel for knees/hands) — less whole-body exposure than pills.</li>
                <li><strong>Bracing & sleeves:</strong> patellar strap or knee sleeve; thumb CMC brace for grip tasks.</li>
                <li><strong>Footwear:</strong> cushioned, supportive shoes; consider lateral wedge for knee OA if advised.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Medicines (talk to your clinician)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Acetaminophen</strong> (paracetamol) may help mild pain; watch liver dose limits.</li>
                <li><strong>Oral NSAIDs</strong> (e.g., ibuprofen, naproxen) can help but may raise risks for stomach, kidney, heart—ask if safe for you.</li>
                <li><strong>Duloxetine</strong> can help chronic OA pain for some people.</li>
                <li><strong>Opioids</strong> are generally <em>not</em> first-line for OA—risks often outweigh benefits.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="note warn"><strong>Safety:</strong> If you have kidney disease, heart failure, stomach bleeding history, or are on blood thinners, do <em>not</em> start oral NSAIDs without medical advice.</div>

      <!-- 6 Flares -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Managing flares (short plan) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ol>
            <li><strong>Calm the joint:</strong> reduce load 24–72 hours, switch to easier activities (cycling, pool).</li>
            <li><strong>Ice 10–15 minutes</strong> 2–3× daily; elevate if swollen.</li>
            <li><strong>Topical NSAID</strong> per label; ask about short NSAID course only if safe for you.</li>
            <li><strong>Gentle range-of-motion</strong> once pain starts easing; resume strengthening slowly.</li>
          </ol>
          <p class="muted">Severe swelling, warmth, redness, fever, or inability to bear weight—seek urgent evaluation.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 7 Joint protection & devices -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Joint protection, devices, and home fixes <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Smart positions</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Use hips and core, not small hand joints, for heavy tasks.</li>
                <li>Avoid deep knee bend holds; change positions often.</li>
                <li>“Nose over toes” when standing from chair to spare knees.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Helpful devices</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Jar opener, key turner, reacher, raised toilet seat, tub bench.</li>
                <li>Cane on the <em>opposite</em> side of the painful hip/knee.</li>
                <li>Thumb CMC brace for pinch tasks (short bursts).</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>At home</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Raise seat height (firm cushion) to ease sit-to-stand.</li>
                <li>Non-slip mats, clear pathways, sturdy handrails.</li>
                <li>Keep daily items at waist height to avoid deep bending.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 8 Weight, sleep, and mood -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Weight, sleep, and mood (quiet helpers) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Small weight changes help knees/hips:</strong> even 5–10 lb loss can reduce joint load and pain.</li>
            <li><strong>Sleep first:</strong> regular schedule, cool dark room, gentle stretch before bed; sleep and pain influence each other.</li>
            <li><strong>Stress care:</strong> brief daily breathing practice, pleasant walks, and connection with others ease pain perception.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 9 Injections & procedures -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Injections & procedures (who benefits?) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Corticosteroid injections</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>May calm a hot, swollen OA flare (especially knee).</li>
                <li>Relief is temporary (weeks to a few months for some).</li>
                <li>Limits per year—ask your clinician.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Viscosupplement (hyaluronic acid)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>“Lubricating” injections for knee OA; mixed evidence.</li>
                <li>Some patients report modest benefit; coverage varies.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box">
            <h3>Surgery (when to think about it)</h3>
            <ul>
              <li>Strong pain most days, limited walking/ADLs, and non-surgical care hasn’t helped.</li>
              <li>Knee or hip replacement can restore function for many; discuss risks, rehab, and support at home.</li>
            </ul>
          </div>

      <!-- 10 Red flags -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> Red flags & when to seek urgent care <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Sudden hot, red, very swollen joint (especially with fever).</li>
            <li>Inability to bear weight after a minor fall or twist.</li>
            <li>Rapid joint deformity, severe night pain, or new numbness/weakness.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note warn"><strong>Call now:</strong> High fever, severe unrelenting pain, or suspected fracture.</div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 11 Food & supplements (brief) -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">11</span> Food & supplements (what actually helps) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Build meals around vegetables/fruit, lean proteins, beans, oats/whole grains you tolerate, and healthy fats (olive oil, nuts).</li>
            <li>Limit heavy ultra-processed snacks and sugary drinks that can worsen weight and inflammation feelings.</li>
            <li>Supplements have mixed evidence. If you try one, trial one at a time, track benefit, and check safety with your clinician—especially if you take blood thinners or have kidney/liver issues.</li>
          </ul>
          <p class="muted small">No supplement replaces movement, sleep, and weight care for OA.</p>

      <!-- 12 FAQs + Multiplex -->
      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">12</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can cartilage grow back?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">OA cartilage loss is usually permanent. The goal is to improve comfort and function by strengthening muscles, optimizing weight, using pain-relief strategies, and protecting the joint.</p>
            </div>
