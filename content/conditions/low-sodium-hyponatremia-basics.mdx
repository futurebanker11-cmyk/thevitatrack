  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What it is</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> Sodium helps control body water and nerve/muscle function. <strong>Hyponatremia</strong> means blood sodium is too low, often due to excess body water relative to sodium. In older adults, medicines and medical conditions are frequent drivers.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Why it matters:</strong> A rapid or very low level can affect the brain—causing confusion, falls, or seizures. Correcting it too fast is also dangerous. Care plans focus on <em>cause</em> and <em>safe pacing</em>.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Seizure, severe confusion, or fainting</li>
        <li>New trouble speaking, walking, or severe headache</li>
        <li>Persistent vomiting with weakness or drowsiness</li>

      <p class="small">These can signal severe/rapid sodium drop. Call your local emergency number immediately.</p>

    <h2>Why sodium drops (by volume status)</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Hyponatremia causes by volume">

        <tr><th>Volume status</th><th>Common causes</th><th>Clues</th><th>Typical approaches</th></tr>

      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Hypovolemic</strong> (low body water & sodium)</td>
          <td>Vomiting/diarrhea, diuretics (especially thiazides), bleeding, adrenal insufficiency</td>
          <td>Dry mouth, low BP, elevated BUN/Cr ratio</td>
          <td>Careful isotonic fluids, stop culprit meds; treat cause</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Euvolemic</strong> (water gain without edema)</td>
          <td>SIADH (lung/CNS disease, malignancy), <em>SSRIs/SNRIs</em>, carbamazepine, hypothyroidism, primary polydipsia, post-op</td>
          <td>Normal exam; low serum uric acid; high urine sodium/osm</td>
          <td>Fluid restriction, stop culprit meds, consider salt tablets/urea; treat cause</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td><strong>Hypervolemic</strong> (water & sodium gain, more water)</td>
          <td>Heart failure, cirrhosis, nephrotic syndrome, advanced CKD</td>
          <td>Edema, ascites, elevated JVP, weight gain</td>
          <td>Fluid + sodium restriction, loop diuretics; optimize underlying condition</td>
        </tr>

  <!-- Testing -->
  <section id="testing" class="section">
    <h2>How it’s diagnosed</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Key labs & measures</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Repeat <strong>serum sodium</strong> to confirm</li>
          <li><strong>Serum osmolality</strong> (true hypotonic vs. pseudo-hyponatremia)</li>
          <li><strong>Urine osmolality & sodium</strong> to identify mechanism</li>
          <li>Thyroid (TSH), cortisol if suspected, kidney/liver panel</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Volume exam first:</strong> Blood pressure, orthostatics, edema, weights, JVP—these guide cause and treatment.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Treatment -->
  <section id="treatment" class="section">
    <h2>Treatment & safe correction limits</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Emergent care</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Severe symptoms: consider <strong>hypertonic saline (3%)</strong> in monitored care</li>
          <li>Frequent sodium checks (every 2–4 hrs initially)</li>
          <li>Address seizures, airway, and the underlying cause</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Safe correction</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Avoid over-correction to prevent <strong>osmotic demyelination</strong></li>
          <li>Typical targets: ≤6–8 mEq/L rise in 24 hrs (clinician-directed)</li>
          <li>Desmopressin “brake” strategy may be used to control pace</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Cause-directed steps</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Fluid restriction</strong> for SIADH/euvolemic states (amount individualized)</li>
          <li>Stop or adjust culprit meds (e.g., thiazides, SSRIs) after clinical review</li>
          <li>Loop diuretics for hypervolemic states; optimize heart/liver/kidney care</li>
          <li>Salt tablets, <strong>urea</strong>, or vasopressin antagonists in selected cases</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card note">
        Plans differ if sodium is chronic vs. acute, and by co-morbidities. Follow your clinician’s instructions closely.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Daily care -->
  <section id="daily" class="section">
    <h2>Daily care & prevention</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Medication review</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Ask about <strong>thiazide diuretics</strong>, <strong>SSRIs/SNRIs</strong>, carbamazepine, oxcarbazepine, desmopressin, and others that can lower sodium</li>
          <li>Never stop medicines on your own—request a supervised plan</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Fluids: get a personalized target</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Restrictions vary (often 1–1.5 L/day in SIADH) and must fit heart/kidney status</li>
          <li>Track <strong>daily weights</strong> and symptoms (nausea, confusion, swelling)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Nutrition & salt</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Do not “chase” sodium with salty snacks unless advised—this may worsen fluid retention</li>
          <li>Prioritize balanced meals with adequate protein; consider oral nutrition support if appetite is poor</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Fall prevention:</strong> Hyponatremia raises fall risk. Rise slowly, use handrails, and review home safety lighting.
      </div>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What sodium level is “low”?</p>
        <p>Many labs flag <strong>&lt;135 mEq/L</strong> as low. Risk depends on how low, how fast it fell, and your overall health.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can drinking too much water cause it?</p>
        <p>Yes—especially with SIADH or very low dietary solute intake. Follow fluid targets set by your clinician.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is sports drink better than water?</p>
        <p>Not necessarily. Some are high in sugar and may not address the cause. Ask your clinician what’s appropriate for you.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How fast should sodium be corrected?</p>
        <p>Slowly and safely—often ≤6–8 mEq/L per 24 hours (clinician-directed) to avoid nerve damage from rapid shifts.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
