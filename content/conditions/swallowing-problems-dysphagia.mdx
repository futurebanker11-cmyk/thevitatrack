  <section id="what-is" class="section">
    <h2>What dysphagia is & why it matters</h2>
    <p class="lead"><span class="badge">In short</span> <strong>Dysphagia</strong> means trouble moving food or liquid from the mouth to the stomach. It can lead to choking, <em>aspiration</em> (food/liquid going into the airway), weight loss, and pneumonia—especially in older adults.</p>
    <div class="card small">
      <p><strong>Good to know:</strong> Many causes are treatable. A <strong>speech-language pathologist (SLP)</strong> evaluates swallowing and teaches strategies to make meals safer and more enjoyable.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Red flags -->
  <section id="red-flags" class="section">
    <h2>When to get urgent help</h2>
    <div class="danger">
      <ul class="list">
        <li>Food stuck in the throat or <strong>inability to swallow</strong> saliva</li>
        <li>Choking with <strong>blue lips</strong>, severe breathing trouble, or fainting</li>
        <li>Coughing/fever after meals, repeated vomiting, or chest pain</li>
        <li>Sudden weakness of face/arm/leg, slurred speech (possible stroke)</li>
      </ul>
      <p class="small">Call emergency services. Avoid giving more food/drink during an acute choking event.</p>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Signs -->
  <section id="signs" class="section">
    <h2>Common signs</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Coughing, throat clearing, or wet/gurgly voice during/after meals</li>
          <li>Food “going down the wrong way,” frequent pneumonias</li>
          <li>Needing extra time to chew; food remaining in the mouth</li>
          <li>Unexplained weight loss, dehydration, or fear of eating</li>
          <li>Pill swallowing difficulty; choking on thin liquids</li>
        </ul>
      </div>

    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Dysphagia causes">

          <td>Oropharyngeal (mouth/throat)</td>

        <tr>
          <td>Esophageal (tube to stomach)</td>

        <tr>
          <td>Medication-related</td>

          <td>Review meds, sip water with pills, avoid lying flat after pills</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Dental/oral</td>
          <td>Ill-fitting dentures, missing teeth, poor saliva</td>
          <td>Dentist adjustment, denture adhesive, saliva substitutes</td>
        </tr>

  <!-- Self-check -->
  <section id="screen" class="section">
    <h2>Simple self-check</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Ask yourself (past month)</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Do you cough or have a wet voice when drinking thin liquids?</li>
          <li>Do pills or solids feel “stuck” or take effort to pass?</li>
          <li>Have you lost weight or avoid certain foods due to fear of choking?</li>
        </ul>
        <p class="small">If “yes” to any, ask your clinician for a swallowing evaluation with an SLP.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Oral care matters:</strong> Brush teeth/dentures 2× daily. Good oral hygiene lowers pneumonia risk if aspiration occurs.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Safe-swallow basics -->
  <section id="safe-eating" class="section">
    <h2>Safe-swallow basics</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">During meals</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Posture:</strong> Sit upright (90°), feet supported; stay upright 30–45 min after eating</li>
          <li><strong>Slow & small:</strong> Small bites/sips, one swallow at a time; alternate solids and liquids</li>
          <li><strong>Focus:</strong> No talking while chewing; reduce distractions</li>
          <li><strong>Moisten foods:</strong> Sauces, gravies, yogurts for dry items</li>
          <li><strong>Check the mouth:</strong> Clear pocketed food before the next bite</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Dentures & tools:</strong> Wear well-fitted dentures; try adaptive cups/utensils, nosey cups, or straws only if SLP approves.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Textures -->
  <section id="textures" class="section">
    <h2>Food & liquid textures</h2>
    <table class="table" role="table" aria-label="Texture guidance">
      <thead>
        <tr><th>Texture</th><th>Examples</th><th>Notes</th></tr>
      </thead>
      <tbody>
        <tr>
          <td>Easy-to-chew / soft</td>
          <td>Scrambled eggs, tender fish, stews, ripe bananas, oatmeal</td>
          <td>Good starting point for mild trouble chewing</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Minced & moist</td>
          <td>Finely chopped meats with gravy, mashed vegetables</td>
          <td>Less chewing; avoid mixed textures (e.g., soup with chunks) unless guided</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Pureed</td>
          <td>Smooth blends of meats/veg/fruit, puddings, yogurt</td>
          <td>No lumps/strings/seeds; add protein powders for nutrition</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Liquids: thin → slightly/thickened</td>
          <td>Water/tea/coffee → commercial thickeners as prescribed</td>
          <td>Use thickened liquids <em>only</em> if recommended by an SLP; aim to keep hydration adequate</td>
        </tr>
        <tr>
          <td>Avoid (unless cleared)</td>
          <td>Dry crumbly bread, tough meats, mixed textures, stringy veg, nuts</td>
          <td>Higher choking risk; trial later with therapist guidance</td>
        </tr>
      </tbody>
    </table>
  </section>

  <!-- Tests -->
  <section id="tests" class="section">
    <h2>Diagnosis & tests</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">How clinicians evaluate</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Bedside/clinical swallow exam</strong> by SLP (history, oral motor check, test sips/bites)</li>
          <li><strong>VFSS</strong> (videofluoroscopic swallow study, “modified barium swallow”): X-ray video while swallowing</li>
          <li><strong>FEES</strong> (fiberoptic endoscopic evaluation): small camera through nose to view throat during eating</li>
          <li><strong>GI tests</strong> for esophageal issues (endoscopy, barium esophagram, manometry)</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card info">
        <strong>Bring to visit:</strong> Dentures, medication list, and a typical problem food (if advised) to demonstrate safely.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Therapy -->
  <section id="therapy" class="section">
    <h2>Therapy & exercises</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">What SLPs may recommend</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li><strong>Compensatory strategies:</strong> Chin tuck, head turn/tilt, effortful swallow (individualized)</li>
          <li><strong>Exercises:</strong> Tongue/lingual strength, Mendelsohn, Shaker/CTAR (under supervision)</li>
          <li><strong>Pacing & energy:</strong> Small, frequent meals; nutrition consult for protein/calorie goals</li>
          <li><strong>Pill strategies:</strong> Crush/split if safe, or liquid forms; take with pureed food if approved</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card calm">
        <strong>Safety note:</strong> Postures/exercises are condition-specific. Use only those prescribed for you after an evaluation.
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- Caregivers -->
  <section id="caregivers" class="section">
    <h2>For caregivers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Support at meals</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Upright posture, small bites, slow pace; cue a second swallow as advised</li>
          <li>Offer safe textures and preferred flavors to keep intake up</li>
          <li>Stop meal and seek guidance if coughing becomes persistent or voice turns wet</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Prevent aspiration</p>
        <ul class="list">
          <li>Stay upright 30–45 minutes after meals; elevate head of bed for reflux</li>
          <li>Daily oral care; manage dentures; hydrate with approved liquids</li>
          <li>Track weight, fevers, and respiratory symptoms; call early for changes</li>
        </ul>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>

  <!-- FAQ -->
  <section id="faq" class="section">
    <h2>Quick answers</h2>
    <div class="grid">
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Is coughing during meals always bad?</p>
        <p>Occasional coughs can be normal, but <strong>repeated</strong> coughing or a wet voice after swallowing suggests risk—seek an SLP evaluation.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Do I have to thicken all liquids?</p>
        <p>Only if an SLP recommends it based on testing. Thickening can help some people but isn’t for everyone; hydration still matters.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">Can dysphagia improve?</p>
        <p>Often yes—with therapy, posture, texture changes, and treating causes like reflux or poor denture fit.</p>
      </div>
      <div class="card">
        <p class="kicker">When should I see a specialist?</p>
        <p>Any ongoing swallowing difficulty, weight loss, or suspected aspiration warrants evaluation by your clinician and an SLP; GI referral if food sticks lower in the chest.</p>
      </div>
    </div>
  </section>
