      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What is prediabetes? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Prediabetes means your blood sugar is above normal but not high enough for Type 2 diabetes. It signals insulin resistance and raises the risk of heart disease and future diabetes. The upside: small, steady habits can lower glucose and often bring numbers back to normal ranges.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>It’s a warning light, not a verdict.</strong> Trends improve with repeatable routines.</li>
            <li><strong>Focus on the next 3–6 months:</strong> plate method eating, post-meal walks, sleep routine, and weight/waist momentum.</li>
          </ul>

        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Symptoms & red flags <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Often no symptoms</h3>
              <p>Many people feel normal. Some notice extra thirst, frequent urination, fatigue, or blurry vision—especially after high-sugar meals.</p>
            </div>
            <div class="note warn">
              <strong>Seek urgent care</strong> for severe drowsiness, vomiting, breathing changes, or confusion—especially if glucose is very high.

        <div class="content">
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Diagnostic thresholds">
            <table>
              <thead>
                <tr><th>Test</th><th>Normal</th><th>Prediabetes</th><th>Diabetes</th></tr>

      <!-- 4 Why it happens -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Why it happens (quick science) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Cells become less responsive to insulin (insulin resistance), so glucose lingers in the bloodstream. The pancreas works harder to keep up; over years, this system tires. Genetics, aging, sleep disruption, certain medicines, inactivity, and abdominal fat all play roles.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Good news:</strong> Muscles act like “glucose sponges” after movement—even short walks improve sensitivity.</li>
            <li><strong>Sleep & stress hormones</strong> push glucose up; regular routines lower that pressure.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 5 Eating pattern -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Daily eating: simple Plate Method <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>1) Half the plate: non-starchy veggies</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Greens, broccoli, peppers, salad, cauliflower, mushrooms</li>
                <li>Flavor with herbs, lemon, garlic; taste before salting</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>2) Quarter: protein</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Fish, chicken, eggs, tofu; beans/lentils (adjust if CKD)</li>
                <li>Choose grilled/roasted; limit breaded and salty sauces</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>3) Quarter: smart carbs</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Oats, brown rice, quinoa, whole-grain bread, corn, sweet potato</li>
                <li>Start modest; pair carbs with protein/veggies</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
          <h3>Practical swaps</h3>
          <ul>
            <li>Sugary drinks → water with lemon, unsweet tea</li>
            <li>White bread → whole-grain; mayonnaise → yogurt-based spread</li>
            <li>Large evening meals → earlier, smaller portions</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note">Heart or kidney conditions may change sodium, protein, or fluid targets—follow your clinician’s plan.</div>

      <!-- 6 Movement -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Gentle movement that works <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Small bursts, big payoff</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>5–10 min walk after meals</strong> most days</li>
                <li>Chair stands, light hand weights, or bands 2–3×/week</li>
                <li>Balance practice near a counter; rise slowly to avoid dizziness</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Why now?</strong> Muscles soak up glucose after activity, lowering post-meal spikes and improving insulin sensitivity.
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 7 Weight & waist -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Weight & waist momentum (realistic) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Even a <strong>modest</strong> drop in waist size or weight can improve glucose and blood pressure. Aim for repeatable habits, not perfection.</p>
          <ul>
            <li>Water first; slow down the first 3 bites</li>
            <li>Plate Method at lunch/dinner; plan snacks (nuts, yogurt, fruit)</li>
            <li>Walks after meals; light strength twice weekly</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 Sleep & stress -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Sleep & stress calm glucose <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Set a consistent bedtime; cool, dark, quiet room; small night light for safety</li>
            <li>Limit screens 1 hour before bed</li>
            <li>5-minute breathing break: in through nose, out through pursed lips</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 9 Monitoring -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Monitoring: labs & learning patterns <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Your clinician will recheck <strong>A1C every 3–6 months</strong>. Some people benefit from occasional home glucose checks (e.g., before breakfast and 1–2 hours after a new meal) to learn which foods/portions work best.</p>

      <!-- 10 Medicines -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> When medicine helps (metformin & more) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Lifestyle changes are first-line. In some cases—especially if A1C is high, you have additional risks, or numbers aren’t improving—your prescriber may suggest medicine.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Metformin:</strong> lowers liver sugar output, improves sensitivity; may cause early stomach upset; kidney function is checked before/while using.</li>
            <li><strong>Others:</strong> Occasionally, a clinician considers additional options based on weight, heart/kidney health, and overall risk profile.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note tip"><strong>Don’t start/stop on your own.</strong> Ask about side effects, timing with meals, and follow-up labs.</div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 11 Can it be reversed? -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">11</span> Can prediabetes be reversed? <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>Many people bring A1C and fasting glucose back to normal ranges with steady routines: Plate Method eating, post-meal walks, sleep rhythm, and small changes to waist/weight. Think “<strong>repeatable</strong> and calm,” not perfect.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 12 Special situations -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">12</span> Special notes (65+, kidney/heart, steroids) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Age 65+:</strong> Goals are personalized to avoid dizziness or low sugars if you monitor.</li>
            <li><strong>Kidney or heart problems:</strong> Salt/fluid and protein targets may change—follow your plan.</li>
            <li><strong>Steroids or certain medicines:</strong> Can raise glucose; ask how to adjust food, checks, or meds.</li>
          </ul>

        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">How often should I recheck A1C?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Usually every 3–6 months. If you change your routine or medicines, your clinician may check sooner.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Do I have to avoid all carbs?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">No. Use the Plate Method with modest portions of higher-fiber carbs and pair them with protein/veggies. Learn which meals work by occasional post-meal checks.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Is weight loss required to improve?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Not always. Many people see better numbers with post-meal walks, better sleep, and smarter portions—even before weight changes. A small, steady waist change helps further.</p>
            </div>
