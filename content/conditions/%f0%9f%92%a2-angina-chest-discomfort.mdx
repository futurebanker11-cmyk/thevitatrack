      <!-- 1 What is angina -->
      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What is angina? (and how it differs from a heart attack) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Angina basics</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Angina</strong> = chest pressure, tightness, heaviness, or breathlessness caused by reduced blood flow to heart muscle.</li>
                <li>Typical triggers: exertion, emotional stress, cold weather, or heavy meals. Improves with rest or nitroglycerin.</li>
                <li><strong>Stable angina</strong>: predictable pattern under similar effort. <strong>Unstable angina</strong>: new, worsening, or at rest — treat as urgent.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Heart attack (acute)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Severe or persistent chest pressure, often with sweating, nausea, shortness of breath, weakness, or faintness.</li>
                <li>May radiate to jaw, neck, back, shoulders, or arms. Can be “silent” or atypical in older adults and women.</li>
                <li><strong>Emergency:</strong> symptoms lasting &gt;5 minutes, or sudden and severe. Call emergency services immediately.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 2 Red flags -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Red flags — when to call for help now <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Severe chest pressure or pain that does not improve with rest within 5 minutes.</li>
            <li>Chest discomfort with shortness of breath, fainting, cold sweat, nausea, or sudden weakness/confusion.</li>
            <li>New chest pain at rest, or rapidly worsening pattern of pain.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note warn"><strong>Call emergency services immediately</strong> for the above symptoms. Do not drive yourself.</div>
        </div>
      </details>

        <div class="content">
          <p class="muted">Chest discomfort has many causes. This table helps you think about patterns; your clinician will decide next steps.</p>
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="Common chest pain causes table">
            <table>
              <thead>
                <tr><th>Category</th><th>Clues</th><th>Notes</th></tr>
              </thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr><td>Heart (angina)</td><td>Pressure/tightness with exertion, cold, stress; better with rest/nitro</td><td>Often radiates to arm/jaw; may be “atypical” in seniors</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Heart (attack)</td><td>Severe, persistent; sweating, nausea, breathlessness</td><td>Emergency — call immediately</td></tr>
                <tr><td>Reflux (GERD)</td><td>Burning after meals/lying down</td><td>May improve with antacids; can mimic heart pain</td></tr>

      <!-- 4 Triggers & nitro -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Common triggers & how to use nitroglycerin safely <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Typical triggers</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Walking uphill or against the wind, hurrying, or carrying weight.</li>
                <li>Cold exposure or heavy meals.</li>
                <li>Emotional stress or poor sleep.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Nitroglycerin instructions</h3>
              <ol>
                <li>Sit down. Place 1 tablet under the tongue (or use spray) at the start of chest pain.</li>
                <li>Wait 5 minutes. If pain remains, take a 2nd dose and call emergency services.</li>
                <li>Up to 3 doses total, 5 minutes apart, while waiting for help.</li>
              </ol>
              <p class="note"><strong>Safety:</strong> Do <em>not</em> use nitroglycerin with medicines for erectile dysfunction (sildenafil, tadalafil, vardenafil) within the time window your clinician advises — dangerous blood pressure drop.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 5 Testing -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> How clinicians evaluate chest pain <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>ECG</strong> (electrocardiogram) to look for ischemia or prior injury.</li>
            <li><strong>Blood tests</strong> (troponin) to check for heart muscle damage.</li>
            <li><strong>Stress testing</strong> (treadmill, echo, or nuclear) for effort-related symptoms.</li>
            <li><strong>CT coronary angiography</strong> to view coronary arteries non-invasively in selected cases.</li>
            <li><strong>Cardiac catheterization</strong> (invasive angiography) when high risk or treatment is likely.</li>
          </ul>
          <p class="muted">Your history, exam, and risk factors guide which test is best.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 6 Treatment -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Treatment overview (medicines, procedures, lifestyle) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Medicines</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Antianginals:</strong> beta-blockers, calcium-channel blockers, long-acting nitrates, ranolazine.</li>
                <li><strong>Risk reduction:</strong> statins for cholesterol; blood-pressure and diabetes control; antiplatelet therapy when indicated.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Procedures</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>Stent (PCI)</strong> or <strong>bypass surgery</strong> when blockages are significant or symptoms persist despite medicines.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Daily habits</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Warm-up before activity; pace uphill; avoid heavy lifting right after meals.</li>
                <li>Low-salt, heart-friendly meals; no smoking; regular, gentle activity.</li>
                <li>Carry nitroglycerin if prescribed; keep a symptom log for your visit.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 7 Senior-focused daily plan -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Senior-friendly daily safety plan <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Morning check-in:</strong> how you feel, sleep quality, swelling, weight if advised.</li>
            <li><strong>Activity pacing:</strong> break tasks into smaller steps; rest as needed; avoid sudden sprints.</li>
            <li><strong>Temperature:</strong> in cold weather, warm up indoors before walking outside; cover mouth/nose.</li>
            <li><strong>Meals:</strong> smaller portions; avoid heavy meals before exertion; limit alcohol as advised.</li>
            <li><strong>Medication routine:</strong> pill box labeled by time; keep nitroglycerin accessible and not expired.</li>
            <li><strong>Appointment prep:</strong> bring a short question list and your symptom/angina log.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 Medication safety -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Medication safety & interactions <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Do not combine <strong>nitroglycerin</strong> with erectile-dysfunction medicines within the advised time window.</li>
            <li>Report dizziness, swelling of ankles, or extreme fatigue to your clinician.</li>
            <li>Do not stop heart medicines suddenly unless advised.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 9 FAQs -->
      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can angina happen without chest pain?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Yes. In older adults and people with diabetes, shortness of breath, fatigue, or jaw/arm discomfort may be the main symptom.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">How fast should nitroglycerin work?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Relief often begins within 1–3 minutes. If pain persists after 5 minutes, take another dose and call emergency services.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Will a normal ECG rule out heart problems?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Not always. Many people with angina can have a normal ECG at rest; further testing may be needed based on your symptoms and risk.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
