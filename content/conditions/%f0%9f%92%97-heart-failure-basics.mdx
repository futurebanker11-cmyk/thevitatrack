      <!-- 1 What it is -->
      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> What is heart failure? (plain words) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Simple definition</h3>
              <p><strong>Heart failure (HF)</strong> means the heart is not pumping or filling well enough to meet the body’s needs. Fluid can back up into the lungs or legs, leading to breathlessness and swelling.</p>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>HFrEF</strong> (reduced ejection fraction): squeezing problem.</li>
                <li><strong>HFpEF</strong> (preserved ejection fraction): stiffness/filling problem—common in older adults.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note tip">
              <strong>Good news:</strong> Daily routines plus the right medicines markedly improve symptoms and reduce hospital visits.
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 2 Symptoms & red flags -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Symptoms & red flags (when to call) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Common symptoms</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Shortness of breath with activity or when lying flat.</li>
                <li>Swollen feet/ankles, sudden weight gain (fluid).</li>
                <li>Fatigue, cough at night, needing extra pillows.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Red flags — call now</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Breathlessness at rest, new chest pain, fainting.</li>
                <li>Fast weight gain (see weight rules below).</li>
                <li>Confusion, bluish lips, severe weakness.</li>
              </ul>
              <p class="note warn"><strong>Emergency:</strong> severe shortness of breath, chest pain, or fainting — call emergency services.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 4 Diagnosis -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> How doctors diagnose HF <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>History & exam:</strong> swelling, lung sounds, neck veins, heart rhythm.</li>
            <li><strong>Blood tests:</strong> BNP/NT-proBNP (fluid strain), kidney/potassium, thyroid, iron status.</li>
            <li><strong>Echo (ultrasound):</strong> shows ejection fraction, stiffness, valve issues.</li>
            <li><strong>ECG & chest X-ray:</strong> rhythm, size, fluid signs.</li>
            <li><strong>Stress test/coronary imaging</strong> if blockages suspected.</li>
          </ul>
          <p class="muted">Your plan depends on the type (HFrEF vs HFpEF), other conditions, and tolerance of medicines.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 5 Daily plan -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Daily plan: weight, fluids, sodium, activity <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Daily weight</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Weigh once each morning after bathroom, before breakfast, similar clothing.</li>
                <li><strong>Call your clinic:</strong> gain ≥2 lb (1 kg) overnight or ≥5 lb (2–3 kg) in a week.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Fluids & sodium</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Typical sodium goal: ~1,500–2,000 mg/day (personalize with your clinician).</li>
                <li>Fluid goal is individualized (often 1.5–2 L/day if advised). Spread sips through the day.</li>
                <li>Read labels; packaged foods and restaurant meals are the biggest salt sources.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Activity & sleep</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Short walks after meals; rest as needed. Avoid sudden sprints or heavy lifting.</li>
                <li>Use 1–2 pillows or a wedge if breathless when flat.</li>
                <li>Stop and report new breathlessness or chest pain.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

            🥗 Low-Sodium Grocery List
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 6 Medicines -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Medicines that help (by class) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div>
              <h3>Core classes (common)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li><strong>ACE inhibitors / ARBs / ARNI:</strong> relax vessels, support heart. (Report cough, swelling, dizziness.)</li>
                <li><strong>Beta-blockers:</strong> steady and protect the heart.</li>
                <li><strong>MRA</strong> (spironolactone/eplerenone): reduce fluid strain; track potassium.</li>
                <li><strong>SGLT2 inhibitors:</strong> help symptoms and hospital risk—even without diabetes.</li>
                <li><strong>Diuretics (“water pills”):</strong> remove extra salt/fluid; timing matters to avoid nighttime bathroom trips.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div>
              <h3>Safety tips</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Have labs checked as scheduled (kidney function, potassium, sodium).</li>
                <li>Report dizziness, very low blood pressure, or swelling in face/tongue (urgent).</li>
                <li>Keep an up-to-date medication list; bring it to every visit.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 7 Devices & procedures -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Devices & procedures (when considered) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Revascularization:</strong> stents or bypass if blocked arteries are driving symptoms.</li>
            <li><strong>ICD (defibrillator):</strong> prevents dangerous rhythms in selected HFrEF cases.</li>
            <li><strong>CRT (“biventricular” pacing):</strong> resynchronizes weak, out-of-sync ventricles.</li>
            <li><strong>Advanced options:</strong> evaluation at HF centers for advanced therapies when needed.</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 Sick-day rules & common pitfalls -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> Sick-day rules & common pitfalls <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>When ill (flu, stomach bug, infection)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Monitor weight, blood pressure, and urine output closely.</li>
                <li>Some meds may be paused temporarily (per your clinic’s instructions) if dehydration or kidney strain is a concern.</li>
                <li>Seek help for fever, chest pain, or fast worsening breathlessness.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Pitfalls</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Extra salty foods (soups, processed meats, takeout) → quick fluid buildup.</li>
                <li>Missing diuretics for several days.</li>
                <li>Taking NSAIDs (like ibuprofen) without asking — can worsen fluid and kidney function.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 9 Living with other conditions -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> HF with diabetes, kidney disease, or AFib <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Diabetes:</strong> glucose control supports heart health; SGLT2 inhibitors may help both.</li>
            <li><strong>Chronic kidney disease:</strong> closer lab checks; avoid NSAIDs; adjust doses as advised.</li>
            <li><strong>Atrial fibrillation:</strong> rhythm/heart-rate control and stroke prevention (blood thinners when indicated).</li>
          </ul>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 10 FAQs + Multiplex -->
      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">How much weight gain is “too fast”?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Call your clinic if you gain ≥2 lb (1 kg) overnight or ≥5 lb (2–3 kg) within a week. This often means fluid buildup.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Is HF the same as a weak heart?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Sometimes. HF can be from weak squeezing (HFrEF) or stiff filling (HFpEF). Either can cause similar symptoms and benefit from a daily plan.</p>
            </div>

              <p itemprop="text">It depends on your symptoms and labs. Many people do well with modest, steady intake; your clinician will personalize a target if needed.</p>
