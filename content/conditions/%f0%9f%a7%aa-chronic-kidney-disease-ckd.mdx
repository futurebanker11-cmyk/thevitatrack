      <details class="section" open>
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">1</span> CKD in plain words (overview) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>Chronic Kidney Disease</strong> means the kidneys aren’t filtering blood as well as before. It usually develops slowly over months to years. Many people feel fine at first. The goals: protect kidneys, protect the heart and brain, and feel well day-to-day.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Key numbers:</strong> eGFR (estimated filtration) and urine albumin (protein leak).</li>
            <li><strong>Top protectors:</strong> steady blood pressure, diabetes control if present, smart salt/fluid habits, and the right medicines.</li>
            <li><strong>Teamwork:</strong> your primary care clinician + (sometimes) a kidney specialist (nephrologist).</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 2 Stages & eGFR -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> CKD stages & eGFR (with simple table) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p><strong>eGFR</strong> is an estimate of kidney filtering. Trends over time are more helpful than one number.</p>
          <div class="table-wrap" role="region" aria-label="CKD stages by eGFR">
            <table>
              <thead><tr><th>Stage</th><th>eGFR (mL/min/1.73m²)</th><th>Plain words</th></tr></thead>
              <tbody>
                <tr><td>G1</td><td>≥ 90</td><td>Normal/high eGFR (CKD possible if urine protein present)</td></tr>
                <tr><td>G2</td><td>60–89</td><td>Mildly decreased (CKD if other signs like protein)</td></tr>
                <tr><td>G3a</td><td>45–59</td><td>Mild-mod decrease</td></tr>
                <tr><td>G3b</td><td>30–44</td><td>Moderate decrease</td></tr>
                <tr><td>G4</td><td>15–29</td><td>Severely decreased</td></tr>
                <tr><td>G5</td><td>&lt; 15</td><td>Kidney failure (consider dialysis/transplant planning)</td></tr>
              </tbody>
            </table>
          </div>
          <p class="muted">Urine albumin levels (A1–A3) also matter. Higher protein leak = higher heart/kidney risk.</p>

      <!-- 3 Symptoms & red flags -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Symptoms to watch & red-flag signs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Common (often subtle)</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Fatigue, reduced appetite, nausea at times</li>
                <li>Ankle swelling, foamy urine (protein)</li>
                <li>Night urination, itchy/dry skin, muscle cramps</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="note warn">
              <strong>Call urgent care / emergency if:</strong>
              <ul>
                <li>Severe shortness of breath with swelling</li>
                <li>Chest pain, confusion, fainting</li>
                <li>No urination for a day with pain/fever, or blood in urine</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 4 Tests & labs -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Tests & labs that matter <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Serum creatinine → eGFR:</strong> the main trend number.</li>
            <li><strong>Urine albumin-to-creatinine ratio (ACR):</strong> protein leak; higher = more risk.</li>
            <li><strong>Electrolytes:</strong> potassium, sodium, bicarbonate (acid-base balance).</li>
            <li><strong>Hemoglobin:</strong> anemia is common in later stages.</li>
            <li><strong>Lipids & A1C:</strong> heart risk and diabetes control.</li>
          </ul>
          <p class="tip"><strong>Bring a list of all medicines and supplements</strong> to each visit (include over-the-counter pain pills and herbal products).</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 5 Daily plan -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Daily plan: small steps that protect kidneys <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Blood pressure & movement</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Check BP correctly; keep a log. Many people benefit from a calm morning/evening check.</li>
                <li>Walk 5–10 minutes after meals. Add gentle strength & balance weekly.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Salt & fluids</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Taste first, then season; use herbs/lemon instead of extra salt.</li>
                <li>Limit processed meats, soups, sauces, instant noodles.</li>
                <li>Ask your clinician about a personal fluid goal (especially if swelling/heart issues).</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Meals</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Colorful veggies/fruit you tolerate; moderate protein portions.</li>
                <li>Use <strong>unsalted</strong> nuts/seeds; olive/canola oil for cooking.</li>
                <li>Some stages may need potassium or phosphorus limits—always individualize with your clinician or dietitian.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

      <!-- 6 Medicines -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">6</span> Medication basics (safety first) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>ACE inhibitors / ARBs</strong> help protect kidneys and heart, especially with protein in urine. Report cough, swelling, dizziness.</li>
            <li><strong>Diuretics</strong> (“water pills”) reduce salt/fluid. Timing matters to avoid nighttime bathroom trips.</li>
            <li><strong>SGLT2 inhibitors</strong> (for many with diabetes, sometimes without): kidney and heart benefits in the right patients.</li>
            <li><strong>Statins</strong> lower cholesterol and reduce heart risk.</li>
          </ul>
          <div class="note"><strong>Do not start/stop on your own.</strong> Dose and choice depend on eGFR, potassium, blood pressure, and other conditions.</div>

      <!-- 7 What to avoid -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> What to avoid (or use with care) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>NSAIDs pain pills</strong> (many over-the-counter): can worsen kidney function and raise blood pressure. Ask about safer options.</li>
            <li><strong>High-sodium foods</strong>: packaged soups, sauces, processed meats, fast foods.</li>
            <li><strong>Herbal products</strong> with unknown kidney effects (always check before use).</li>
            <li><strong>Contrast dye scans</strong> without kidney-safe planning—remind clinicians of your CKD.</li>
          </ul>
          <p class="muted">If you need pain relief, talk to your clinician about choices that fit your stage.</p>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 8 CKD + other conditions -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">8</span> CKD with diabetes, blood pressure, or heart failure <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="two">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Diabetes</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Steady glucose (A1C goal is personal). SGLT2 inhibitors and ACE/ARB often help protect kidneys.</li>
                <li>Foot care, eye checks, and flu/pneumonia shots reduce complications.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Blood pressure & heart failure</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Accurate home BP checks; share averages.</li>
                <li>Daily weight if heart failure; sudden gain may signal fluid retention.</li>
                <li>Salt/fluids individualized to symptoms and stage.</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>
        </div>
      </details>

      <!-- 9 Dialysis & transplant basics -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">9</span> Dialysis & transplant (basics only) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <p>If kidneys decline to late stages, a kidney specialist discusses options early so you can plan calmly.</p>
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Hemodialysis:</strong> blood is filtered by a machine several times each week.</li>
            <li><strong>Peritoneal dialysis:</strong> fluid in the abdomen helps filter blood at home.</li>
            <li><strong>Transplant:</strong> a donated kidney; medicines prevent rejection.</li>
          </ul>
          <p class="muted">Many people live well with the right option and support. Decisions are personal and guided by your team.</p>

        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">10</span> Caregivers & family: how to help <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Support a calm routine for BP checks and medicine times.</li>
            <li>Cook low-salt meals together; keep labels and a grocery list handy.</li>
            <li>Prepare a short question list before appointments; bring logs and medication list.</li>
            <li>Watch for swelling, breathlessness, lower appetite, or confusion—share changes early.</li>

      <details class="section" itemscope itemtype="https://schema.org/FAQPage">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">11</span> FAQs <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Can CKD improve?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Some causes are reversible early, but most CKD focuses on <em>slowing or stabilizing</em> decline. Consistent BP, diabetes care, salt awareness, and the right medicines protect kidneys and the heart.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Should I drink more water?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Not always. Some people need to limit fluids (especially with swelling or heart failure). Ask for a personal daily target.</p>
            </div>
          </div>
          <div class="box" itemscope itemprop="mainEntity" itemtype="https://schema.org/Question">
            <h3 itemprop="name">Is a low-protein diet required?</h3>
            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Protein needs are individual. Moderate portions are common; some stages require adjustments. A dietitian can tailor safe choices for you.</p>
            </div>
