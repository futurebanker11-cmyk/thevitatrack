      <!-- 3 Fast fix -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">2</span> Fast treatment: the 15–15 rule <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ol>
            <li><strong>Check</strong> your blood sugar (meter/CGM). If you can’t check but have clear symptoms, treat anyway.</li>
            <li><strong>Take 15 grams of fast-acting carbohydrate:</strong> glucose tablets (per label), 4 oz (120 ml) regular soda or juice, or 1 tablespoon sugar/honey. <em>Avoid chocolate/fat—works slower.</em></li>
            <li><strong>Wait 15 minutes</strong>, recheck. If still low, take another 15 g and recheck.</li>
            <li>Once back to normal and the next meal is far away, <strong>eat a small snack</strong> with carb + protein (e.g., crackers + peanut butter) if your clinician advises.</li>
          </ol>
          <div class="note"><strong>On certain medicines (e.g., sulfonylureas) or insulin:</strong> Lows may last longer. Keep glucose sources handy and inform your clinician after a significant event.</div>

      <!-- 4 Why lows happen -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">3</span> Why lows happen (fix the root cause) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Meals:</strong> skipped/late meals, smaller portions than usual, vomiting/illness</li>
            <li><strong>Activity:</strong> more walking or exercise than usual, yardwork, housework</li>
            <li><strong>Medicines:</strong> insulin timing/dose mismatch; some pills (e.g., sulfonylureas) increase risk</li>
            <li><strong>Alcohol:</strong> especially without food; lows may show up hours later overnight</li>
            <li><strong>Kidney/liver issues:</strong> can change how long medicines last</li>
          </ul>

      <!-- 5 Prevention day & night -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">4</span> Prevention (day & night) <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <div class="three">
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Daytime</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Eat on a steady schedule; keep a portable carb in your bag/pocket</li>
                <li>Match insulin/med timing to meals; verify dose with your plan</li>
                <li>Check before driving; if low, treat and wait until normal</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Activity</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Carry quick sugar and ID</li>
                <li>For longer walks, plan a small carb beforehand if advised</li>
                <li>Check again after activity—lows can be delayed</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
            <div class="box">
              <h3>Night</h3>
              <ul>
                <li>Avoid heavy evening alcohol; take medicines exactly as prescribed</li>
                <li>Consider a small balanced snack if your clinician recommends</li>
                <li>Use CGM alerts if available; keep glucose tabs by the bed</li>
              </ul>
            </div>
          </div>

        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">5</span> Caregiver & family plan <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li>Know where glucose tablets/gel and the <strong>glucagon</strong> are stored; learn how to use them</li>
            <li>If the person can’t swallow safely: <strong>don’t give food or drink</strong>; use glucagon if prescribed and <strong>call emergency services</strong></li>
            <li>After recovery, note time, suspected cause, and actions taken; share with the clinician</li>
          </ul>

        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>Driving:</strong> Check first. If low, treat, recheck, and wait until normal and you feel clear-headed.</li>
            <li><strong>Illness:</strong> Vomiting/poor intake? You may need a simplified plan—contact your clinician.</li>
            <li><strong>Clinic days:</strong> Bring meter/CGM and a short log: times, readings, meals, activity, and any lows.</li>

      <!-- 8 Myths -->
      <details class="section">
        <summary class="head"><span class="badge">7</span> Myths to ignore <span class="chev">▾</span></summary>
        <div class="content">
          <ul>
            <li><strong>“Chocolate works fast.”</strong> Fat slows sugar—use glucose tabs, juice, or regular soda first.</li>
            <li><strong>“If I feel okay, I’m okay.”</strong> Some people feel few signs—use a meter/CGM and routines.</li>
            <li><strong>“One low doesn’t matter.”</strong> Repeated lows increase risk and can blunt warning signs.</li>
          </ul>

            <div itemscope itemprop="acceptedAnswer" itemtype="https://schema.org/Answer">
              <p itemprop="text">Any low with <strong>severe symptoms</strong> (confusion, seizure, fainting) or inability to swallow is an emergency—use glucagon if prescribed and call for help.</p>
